## Content

After 100+ product management interviews, 3 years building products, and an MBA focused on product strategy, I've noticed a huge gap between what people say you need to be a PM and what actually matters day-to-day.

LinkedIn is full of posts listing 37 essential PM skills. Job descriptions ask for everything from SQL to Figma to strategic thinking. Bootcamps promise to teach you the "complete PM toolkit" in 12 weeks.

But after shipping products, leading cross-functional teams, and actually doing the job, here's what I've learned: Most of those skills don't matter. Or at least, they matter way less than people think.

Here are the skills you actually need—and the ones everyone talks about but rarely use.

---

## Skills That Actually Matter

### 1. Asking the right questions

The best PMs I've worked with don't have all the answers. They ask great questions.

When an engineer proposes a solution, they ask: "What problem are we trying to solve?" When a stakeholder requests a feature, they ask: "Who is this for and why do they need it?" When a metric drops, they ask: "What changed?"

I learned this the hard way. Early in my PM career, I thought I needed to have answers for everything. I'd walk into meetings with detailed plans and get defensive when people questioned them. It didn't work.

Now, I walk into meetings with questions:
- What problem are we solving?
- Who are we building this for?
- How will we know if it works?
- What's the simplest version we can ship?
- What are we not going to do?

Good questions lead to better products. Answers just lead to feature factories.

**How to develop this skill**: Before every meeting, write down 3-5 questions you want answered. Practice asking "why" without sounding confrontational. Get comfortable with silence—people need time to think.

---

### 2. Ruthless prioritization

Every PM I know has a backlog with 200 feature requests. The difference between good PMs and great PMs is what they say no to.

When I started at Open Progress, our backlog had 150+ items. Engineering was overwhelmed. Stakeholders were frustrated. Nothing was shipping on time.

I spent a week scoring every feature using the RICE framework (Reach, Impact, Confidence, Effort). Then I cut the backlog by 70%. We focused on 10 high-impact features and said no to everything else.

The result: We shipped those 10 features on time. User satisfaction went up. Engineering was less stressed. And the features we cut? No one missed them.

Prioritization isn't about saying yes to the right things. It's about saying no to almost everything.

**How to develop this skill**: Use a framework (RICE, Value vs. Effort, Kano Model). Force yourself to rank order features—no ties. Practice saying "not now" instead of "no" to preserve relationships.

---

### 3. Communicating clearly

PMs are translators. You translate user needs to engineering. You translate technical constraints to stakeholders. You translate business goals to designers.

If you can't explain your product in simple terms, you don't understand it well enough.

I test my clarity by explaining every feature to my non-technical friends. If they get confused, I revise. If they immediately understand the value, I know I'm on the right track.

Bad: "We're implementing a machine learning model that leverages historical data to optimize targeting parameters."

Good: "We're using AI to figure out which voters are most likely to respond to outreach, so campaigns can focus on people they can actually persuade."

The second version is clear. The first sounds impressive but means nothing.

**How to develop this skill**: Write everything twice—once in technical terms, once in simple language. Practice the "explain it to your grandma" test. Avoid jargon. Use examples.

---

### 4. Listening (actually listening)

User interviews are useless if you're just waiting for your turn to talk.

I used to think I was good at user research. I'd conduct interviews, ask questions, take notes. But I was listening for confirmation of my ideas, not insights that challenged them.

Then I shadowed a campaign for a week. I watched field organizers use our tools, saw where they struggled, heard them complain about features I thought were great. I learned more in that week than 20 interviews combined.

The best user insights come from watching people work, not asking them questions. People will tell you what they think you want to hear. But their behavior doesn't lie.

**How to develop this skill**: Shadowing > interviews. Ask "show me" instead of "tell me." Take notes on what people do, not just what they say. Follow up on throwaway comments—that's where the gold is.

---

### 5. Shipping

Products don't matter until they're in users' hands.

The worst thing you can do as a PM is spend months planning the perfect product and never ship. I've seen teams spend a year building features no one asked for because they never validated with real users.

I'd rather ship a simple v1 in 4 weeks, learn it's wrong, and pivot than spend 6 months building the wrong thing perfectly.

When we built the AI QA automation system, I wanted to include custom ML models, advanced analytics, and automated test generation. It would have taken 12 weeks.

Instead, we shipped visual regression testing and basic flow automation in 6 weeks. It wasn't perfect. But it solved 80% of the problem and gave us feedback to improve.

Shipping creates momentum. Momentum creates confidence. Confidence creates better products.

**How to develop this skill**: Set aggressive deadlines. Ruthlessly cut scope to ship faster. Celebrate launches, not plans. Measure time-to-ship as a key metric.

## Skills Everyone Talks About (That Matter Less Than You Think)

### 1. SQL / Technical skills

Yes, it's helpful to write SQL queries or read code. But it's not essential.

I know great PMs who can't code. They partner with data analysts for queries and engineers for technical feasibility. What matters is asking the right questions and understanding trade-offs, not writing the queries yourself.

If you're technical, great—use it. But if you're not, don't let it stop you from being a PM.

---

### 2. Wireframing / Design skills

PMs don't need to be designers. You need to articulate user needs and communicate intent. Your designer will make it look good.

I've seen PMs spend hours in Figma creating pixel-perfect mocks. Meanwhile, their designer is waiting for clear requirements.

Instead of wireframing, I write specs:
- What problem does this solve?
- Who is it for?
- What are the key user flows?
- What does success look like?

Then I let the designer design.

---

### 3. Industry expertise

You don't need 10 years in fintech to be a fintech PM. You need curiosity, user empathy, and the ability to learn quickly.

When I started working on political campaigns, I knew nothing about campaign tech. I learned by shadowing organizers, reading campaign post-mortems, and asking dumb questions.

Industry knowledge helps, but it's not a prerequisite. In fact, outsiders often see problems insiders miss.

---

## The Meta-Skill: Learning How to Learn

The skills I've listed will evolve. New frameworks will emerge. AI will change workflows. The product tools we use today will be obsolete in 5 years.

The only skill that will always matter is learning how to learn.

Great PMs are curious. They read widely. They ask questions. They seek feedback. They reflect on what worked and what didn't.

If you can learn fast, adapt to new contexts, and apply lessons from one domain to another, you'll be a great PM no matter what the job description says.

## What to Focus on If You're Breaking Into PM

If you're trying to land your first PM role, here's what I'd prioritize:

**1. Build something (anything)**: Side projects, internal tools, volunteer work. Doesn't matter what—just ship something and learn the process.

**2. Talk to users**: Conduct user interviews. Shadow people using products. Practice asking "why" and listening.

**3. Practice prioritization**: Take any product backlog (yours, a friend's, a case study) and force rank it. Defend your choices.

**4. Learn frameworks, but don't worship them**: RICE, Jobs-to-be-Done, OKRs—they're useful, but they're tools, not answers.

**5. Reflect on what you learn**: After every project, write down what worked, what didn't, and what you'd do differently.

The skills you need are simpler than people make them out to be. You don't need to be a coder, designer, data scientist, strategist, and therapist all at once.

You need to ask good questions, prioritize ruthlessly, communicate clearly, listen actively, and ship products that solve real problems.

Everything else is noise.
