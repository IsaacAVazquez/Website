# World Cup 2026 Countdown, No. 3: England Did Everything Right Except Win

At number three in my [2026 World Cup countdown](/writing/world-cup-2026-top-ten-contenders) is the team that the data loves and that history keeps interrupting. England is a clean third on the betting board, third on the Opta supercomputer, and one of only two European sides, along with Norway, that won every single one of its qualifiers. By the numbers, there is no argument about where they belong. The argument, the one that follows this team to every tournament, is whether the numbers mean anything when the games get tight, because England has spent the last decade getting closer than anyone and still not winning.

## A decade of nearly

The pedigree question with England is the inverse of most teams. They have one World Cup, in 1966, and have never won anything else at senior level. But they are not the perennial disappointment of the 1990s and 2000s anymore. This is a side that reached the semifinals of the 2018 World Cup, the quarterfinals in 2022, and the finals of both the 2021 and 2024 European Championships, losing both. That is a remarkable run of getting to the final weekend of major tournaments. It is also a remarkable run of not finishing the job, and that is the precise thing I am trying to weigh. England is no longer a team that bottles the group stage. It is a team that has not figured out how to win the one game at the very end.

## Tuchel made the hard calls

Thomas Tuchel named his squad on May 22 in a broadcast from Wembley, and the most striking thing about it was the ruthlessness. He left out Cole Palmer, Phil Foden, Trent Alexander-Arnold and Harry Maguire, all of them players who would walk into most national teams at this tournament. You can argue with individual omissions, but the message was unmistakable. Tuchel is picking the team he thinks wins knockout games, not the team that looks best on paper or keeps everyone happy, and I respect that, because England's failures have often looked like a coach trying to fit too many big names into one side rather than building a functional team.

Harry Kane captains the side at his third World Cup, which equals a record, and he remains one of the best center-forwards in the world and the player England's whole attack runs through. Jude Bellingham is the talisman in midfield, a player capable of dragging a team through a tournament on sheer force of will, and Bukayo Saka gives them a genuine match-winner on the right. Declan Rice anchors the midfield. The spine is genuinely elite, and the depth, even after the cuts, is the envy of almost every other team here.

## The path

England drew Group L with Croatia, Ghana and Panama, and on paper it is manageable, but Croatia is a dangerous second seed and a side that has knocked England out before, most painfully in the 2018 semifinal. Topping the group matters more than usual here, because the bracket flips meaningfully depending on whether England finish first or second, and the projected reward for winning the group is still a heavyweight quarterfinal, with Brazil the model's favorite to be waiting there. That would be one of the games of the tournament and exactly the kind of test that has ended England's recent campaigns.

## The betting angle

The market makes England a clear third at around +650 to +700, with the best price at +700, and the line actually wobbled when the squad was announced, with one book briefly showing +600. That volatility tells you the market takes the squad selection seriously and is not entirely sure what to make of Tuchel's choices. In the staged markets England sits around +170 to reach the semifinal, which reflects how strong the path looks before the quarterfinals. I am reading these prices as analysis rather than advice, and they will keep moving, but the consistent message is that the market sees England as the best of the chasing pack behind Spain and France.

## The case against, and where I land

The case against England is simply the last ten years. At some point, falling short repeatedly stops being bad luck and starts being a trait, and a rational ranker has to price that in. There is a version of this tournament where England cruises through the group, wins a quarterfinal, and then freezes in a semifinal or final exactly as they have before, and nobody who has watched this team would be shocked.

But I keep coming back to the fact that the underlying quality and the qualifying form are genuinely third-best in the world, and that Tuchel has shown a willingness to make the cold decisions that previous England regimes avoided. A flawless qualifying campaign and an elite spine earn the number three spot on merit. Whether they can finally win the game that has broken their hearts four times is the question that defines their tournament, and I am not betting against the talent just because the history is painful.

That is my number three. From here, the countdown reaches the two teams I believe are clear of the entire field, a two-horse race I went back and forth on more than any other call. At number two is the deepest squad in the tournament, dragged just below the top spot by the cruelest draw of all. [France](/writing/world-cup-2026-contenders-2-france) is next.
