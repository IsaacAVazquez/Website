# Building an Investment Research Platform That Prefers Clarity Over Spreadsheet Sprawl

If you want to see the product before reading the breakdown, open the [Investment Research Platform](/investments).

Most personal investing workflows break down in the same place: the analysis is scattered. Price charts live in one tab, financial statements in another, notes in a spreadsheet, and portfolio tracking in a tool that has no idea why you bought the stock in the first place.

I built this project because I wanted a research workflow that felt more like a decision workspace than a collection of disconnected utilities. The goal was not to recreate a full institutional terminal. The goal was to make the most important parts of equity research easier to review, compare, and revisit without adding more friction.

## The Problem This Product Tries To Solve

Retail research tools often force a tradeoff between breadth and clarity.

- Broad platforms surface a huge number of metrics, but they bury the signal under dense tables and too many navigation layers.
- Simpler trackers make portfolio monitoring easy, but they stop short of helping you understand the business behind the position.
- Spreadsheet-based workflows are flexible, but they are tedious to maintain and hard to keep consistent over time.

That combination creates a quiet failure mode: you can gather plenty of information without actually improving the decision.

I wanted a workspace that answered three practical questions quickly:

1. What does this company look like right now?
2. How does the valuation compare with the operating profile?
3. What does this mean for the positions I already hold?

## Why The Product Uses Curated Snapshots Instead Of Chasing Infinite Live Data

One of the earliest product decisions was to avoid pretending this needed to be a tick-by-tick trading terminal.

The platform is built around curated company snapshots, valuation panels, price history, and financial statement views. That structure matters because the highest-value research questions usually do not require every possible data point in real time. They require trustworthy context with clear fallbacks.

That is why the product leans on a curated symbol set and snapshot-backed research rather than an unlimited search surface. It narrows the scope enough to keep the experience reliable while still giving the user a strong research loop.

This tradeoff improves the product in a few ways:

- The research experience is more stable because every symbol can be shaped into the same interface.
- The UI can emphasize interpretation instead of raw data dumping.
- The system can degrade more gracefully when a data source is incomplete.

In other words, curation is not a limitation here. It is part of the product strategy.

## The Core Product Decisions

### 1. Research And Portfolio Tracking Live In The Same Workspace

The route is organized around two tabs: research and portfolio. That sounds simple, but it changes the product meaningfully.

Instead of treating research as a separate activity from portfolio management, the platform lets both workflows share the same mental model. You can move from a holding to a deeper review of the underlying company without changing tools or losing context.

That matters because investing decisions are rarely isolated. The useful question is not just "Is this stock interesting?" It is "Is this stock interesting relative to what I already own?"

### 2. Browser-Saved Portfolio State Beats Heavy Account Setup For This Use Case

I chose browser-local persistence for portfolio tracking instead of building a full account system.

For this project, that was the right call. The value of the portfolio tab is fast iteration, not multi-user infrastructure. Local persistence keeps the workflow immediate and private, removes sign-in friction, and lets the product behave more like a personal research notebook than a brokerage dashboard.

This is a good example of scope discipline. Adding authentication would have made the product feel more complete on paper, but it would not have made the core research loop materially better.

### 3. Valuation Needs Context, Not Just Ratios

A ratio by itself rarely helps. A low multiple can signal value, but it can also reflect deteriorating fundamentals. A premium multiple may be justified if growth, margins, and balance-sheet quality support it.

That is why the experience is organized around adjacent panels instead of isolated numbers. The user can move across valuation, growth, profitability, financial statements, industry context, and price history without reorienting every time.

The product design principle here is simple: if a metric invites interpretation, the supporting context should be close by.

## How The Experience Works

At a high level, the platform behaves like a structured research desk:

- A research view surfaces company snapshots, valuation context, statement data, and supporting panels.
- A portfolio view tracks holdings and portfolio-level state in the browser.
- Route state allows specific symbols and sections to be linked directly, which makes the interface easier to revisit with intent.

The implementation choice underneath that experience is just as important as the interface: stable snapshots instead of fragile ad hoc queries everywhere.

That allows the product to focus on what users actually need from a public research tool:

- fast page loads
- consistent panel structure
- understandable coverage boundaries
- fewer moments where the UI looks "live" but the data is incomplete

## Tradeoffs I Would Make Again

### Reliable Fallbacks Over Endless Coverage

A smaller but cleaner research universe is better than a giant index with uneven quality.

### Workspace Framing Over Feature Creep

The product is intentionally framed as a workspace, not a brokerage clone. That keeps the experience centered on research and portfolio understanding instead of trying to imitate execution platforms.

### Decision Support Over Quant Theater

There are plenty of finance products that use metric density as a stand-in for usefulness. I would rather show fewer things, arranged with more intent, than turn the page into a wall of numbers that looks impressive but slows down judgment.

## What This Project Taught Me

Building finance products is often an exercise in restraint.

The temptation is to add more coverage, more models, more feeds, more views, and more technical sophistication. But most of the user value comes from making it easier to move from raw information to a coherent point of view.

This project reinforced a product lesson I trust across domains: good tools reduce decision fatigue before they add decision power.

For research products specifically, that means:

- define the workflow before expanding the dataset
- treat fallback behavior as a first-class feature
- keep the interface close to the real questions investors are asking
