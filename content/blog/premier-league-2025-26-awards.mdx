# The 2025-26 Premier League Awards: Bruno's Record, Haaland's Boot, and the Debate Worth Having

I said when Arsenal clinched the title that the individual awards had a couple of outcomes worth arguing about, and now that they are in, the one I want to spend the most time on is Player of the Season, because it went to Bruno Fernandes, and Bruno Fernandes plays for Manchester United, who finished third. There is a real debate underneath that, the kind that comes up every year in some form, about whether the best individual award should follow the best individual or the best team. This year the voters chose the individual, and I think, on balance, they were right, which is not where I expected to land when I started thinking about it.

The case for Fernandes is the assist record. He set up 21 goals across the season, which broke the Premier League record for assists in a single campaign, a record that had been shared by Thierry Henry and Kevin De Bruyne at 20. Breaking a mark held by two of the best creative players the league has ever seen is not a stat you can wave away, and the more important context is what I wrote about United in March, that their climb from fifteenth the previous season into the Champions League places was close to a single-handed transformation. A team does not usually move that far in a year, and the reason United did is that one player produced the best creative season the competition has ever recorded while carrying the captaincy of a club that had been in disarray. He also picked up the writers' Player of the Year, so it was not a split decision.

## Why I came around to rewarding a non-champion

My instinct is usually that the Player of the Season should come from the team that actually won, because winning the league is a collective achievement and the best player on the best team has, by definition, been excellent in the games that mattered most. By that logic the award should have gone to an Arsenal player, and there is a strong argument for David Raya on exactly those grounds. But the more I sat with it, the more I think the assist record tips it, because the whole point of an individual award is to recognize individual greatness, and a record that stood as a shared mark between Henry and De Bruyne for years is about as clear a marker of individual greatness as the sport produces. Rewarding the champion is the safe choice. Rewarding the historic season is the correct one, at least this year.

## Haaland's third boot, and Raya's quiet one

Erling Haaland won the Golden Boot with 27 goals, his third, following the ones he won in 2022-23 and 2023-24. There is a slight oddity in the league's top scorer coming from the team that finished second rather than first, but that is not unusual, because the Golden Boot rewards volume scoring and the title rewards a team that does everything well, and those are not the same thing. Haaland is simply the most reliable goalscorer in the league, and 27 in a season where City fell short of the title is a reminder that you can have the best striker in the world and still lose the league to a team that defends better than everyone else. Which brings me to the award I care about most.

David Raya won the Golden Glove with 19 clean sheets, his third in a row, and I would argue this is the award that best explains the season. I spent the entire year saying Arsenal's title was built on not conceding, and Raya is the clearest single embodiment of that. Nineteen clean sheets is the statistical shape of a title won on defense, and a goalkeeper taking the Golden Glove three seasons running while his club finally converts all that defensive solidity into a championship is the most fitting award of the lot. If Player of the Season is the argument, Golden Glove is the explanation.

The Young Player of the Season went to Nico O'Reilly of Manchester City, capping a breakthrough year, and I do not have a quarrel with it. City had a frustrating season by their standards, finishing second and watching a rival end a 22-year wait, but a genuine young talent emerging is the kind of thing that softens a near-miss and matters more for the next few years than one lost title race.

The pattern I would pull out of the awards is that they told the truth about the season in the aggregate even where individual choices were debatable. The best creative season the league has ever seen got the headline award, the most reliable finisher got the Golden Boot on a team that came up short, the goalkeeper who anchored the champions got the recognition that best explains how they won, and a young player from a disappointed City got a reason to look forward. You can quarrel with rewarding a third-place player over a champion, and I did quarrel with it for a while, but a record is a record, and this was the year to honor it.
