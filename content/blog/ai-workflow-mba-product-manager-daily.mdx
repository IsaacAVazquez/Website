# How I Actually Use AI as an MBA and Aspiring PM (Day-to-Day Workflow)

The honest version of my AI workflow is less impressive than what gets shared on LinkedIn and more useful than most productivity content. It is not a stack of twenty tools running in parallel. It is a small set of tools used consistently for specific tasks where they actually compress time, combined with a clear sense of what I still do without them.

I am a first-year MBA at Haas, recruiting for PM roles, and shipping side projects that are live products. That is the context for why my workflow looks the way it does. Your context is different, so some of this will translate directly and some will not.

## The stack

Claude is where most of my thinking happens. I use it for synthesis, drafting, stress-testing arguments, and working through structured problems. Perplexity handles research tasks where I need grounded, current information with sources I can actually check. Notion is where everything lands and where I organize what I am working on. That is basically it. I have tried more tools than this and found that the switching cost of maintaining a complex stack is not worth it.

## How the day actually runs

My mornings during the school year involve a lot of reading: cases, course materials, pre-reads, the news, whatever is relevant to recruiting conversations happening that day. The part AI actually changes is the synthesis step. I used to spend 30 to 45 minutes reading a case and pulling out what mattered. Now I spend about 15 minutes reading it myself first, then use Claude to surface the analytical frames I may have missed, stress-test my interpretation, and generate the questions I should be able to answer in class.

The order matters. I read first because I want my own reaction to the material before I see what Claude pulls out of it. If I prompt first, I am pattern-matching to its framing instead of forming my own. That distinction sounds minor but it has meaningful effects on whether I can actually defend a position in class versus reciting what the AI thought.

For news and market context relevant to recruiting, I ask Perplexity to synthesize recent developments in a specific company or space and give me the three to five things that are genuinely new and consequential. I am not outsourcing judgment about what matters, but I am compressing the time it takes to get oriented on a topic from scratch.

## Research and synthesis for projects

A significant portion of my side project work involves understanding a problem space before I can build in it. The investment research platform, the news pulse dashboard, the analytics work I have done: all of them required forming a view of the landscape before writing a line of code.

The workflow I use for this is a three-step synthesis process. First, I use Perplexity to build an initial research base with sources, asking it specifically to surface the strongest arguments on each side of contested questions and to flag where the evidence is thin. Second, I move that synthesis into Claude and ask it to identify the contradictions and the assumptions that most of my sources are implicitly making. Third, I write a brief problem framing document myself based on what I have now read and processed.

The AI handles the volume. The judgment about which problems are worth solving, which assumptions are worth questioning, and which opportunity is actually real is mine. That is not a modesty statement. It is a practical point about what the tools are actually good at.

## Recruiting preparation

This is where AI has probably saved me the most time in the last year. PM recruiting requires a lot of contextual knowledge about companies, products, and industries, plus a lot of structured practice with frameworks, and a lot of iterative work on how you tell your own story.

For company research before interviews and coffee chats, I build a one-page brief using Perplexity and Claude combined. The Perplexity pass gets me current information about the company's products, recent strategy moves, and how they talk about themselves publicly. The Claude pass helps me identify questions that are actually interesting to ask. Not "what does success look like in this role" but questions that would signal I understand the product and have a perspective on it.

For case prep, I use Claude as a sparring partner. I describe the case, give my initial answer, and ask it to find the weaknesses in my reasoning and generate the two or three follow-up directions an interviewer would most likely take it. I do not use it to generate answers I then memorize. The point is to stress-test the reasoning I am building, not to substitute for building it.

For behavioral interview prep, I write out my stories in my own words first, then use Claude to identify where the structure breaks down and where the impact is not specific enough. The editing process is faster than starting from scratch, and the AI is better than I am at noticing when I have implied an outcome rather than stated it.

## Building and shipping

I am a solo builder working in Next.js, TypeScript, and the usual modern web stack. AI has changed this work more than any other part of my day.

The pattern I have settled into is that I write the architecture and the decisions. I define what needs to exist, why, and how the pieces connect. Then I use Claude to write implementation code for the pieces once I know what they are. I review everything that gets written and push back when the implementation does not match the intent. This is not vibe coding. It is closer to technical direction. The speed difference compared to writing everything from scratch is significant. The quality difference compared to accepting AI output without review is also significant, in the other direction.

The places where I still write code myself are anything that involves a design decision I have not fully resolved, anything touching security or data handling, and anything where I want to understand the implementation thoroughly because I will need to modify it later. For scaffolding, boilerplate, and clearly specified feature implementations, AI generates a solid first draft that I then edit.

## What I never outsource

Strategic judgment about what is worth building and what is not. My actual opinions in class discussions and in recruiting conversations. The final editing pass on anything that represents me publicly. The decision about whether a piece of work is good enough to ship.

The common thread in that list is that these are the things where the quality of the output depends on the quality of the underlying thinking, and where substituting someone else's thinking, even a very capable model's thinking, produces output that sounds right but does not hold up to scrutiny.

I have made the mistake of outsourcing too much in each of these areas at least once, and the result is always the same: something that looks fine until it is actually tested, at which point it becomes obvious that the surface quality was covering for a gap in genuine understanding.

## The actual time savings

Being concrete about this: a week of coursework, recruiting prep, project research, and shipping time that used to take roughly 60 hours of active work takes closer to 40 now. The 20 hours that compressed are almost entirely the mechanical parts: reading synthesis, initial drafting, code scaffolding, research orientation. The 40 hours that remain are the parts that require judgment, which is where the work actually happens.

The leverage is real. The way most people describe it, some version of "AI does everything, I just review," is not accurate to my experience or to any workflow I have seen produce consistently good work. The better frame is that AI handles volume and structure, and you handle judgment and specificity. Getting that division right is the actual skill.
