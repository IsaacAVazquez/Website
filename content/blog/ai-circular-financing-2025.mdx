# The AI Deals Are Not Obviously a Bubble, but the Circularity Is the Real Worry

The debate about this fall's run of enormous AI deals has hardened into two camps that are both, I think, using words that are too blunt to be useful. The bulls call the web of investments and contracts an ecosystem, as though interlocking commitments among the biggest players are simply what a maturing industry looks like. The bears call it a bubble, which is a satisfying word that mostly signals a mood rather than a mechanism. I do not find either framing very helpful, because the specific thing that has been bothering me is narrower and more precise than "bubble." It is the circularity, the fact that in several of these deals the same dollars appear to get counted as an investment, as revenue, and as a purchase, depending on which company's press release you are reading, and I want to be careful and measured about exactly when that becomes a real problem rather than a normal feature of how large partnerships get structured.

## The loop is real and it is worth tracing slowly

It helps to trace one version of the loop concretely, because the numbers are large enough that they stop feeling real. Nvidia agreed to invest up to $100 billion into OpenAI to help finance a very large buildout of data center capacity. OpenAI, in turn, committed to pay Oracle something on the order of $300 billion over five years for computing infrastructure, as part of a broader data center effort that has been described in the range of half a trillion dollars. And Oracle, to build the capacity it just sold, bought billions of dollars of chips from Nvidia, with reports putting one such purchase around $40 billion. So a dollar can start as an Nvidia investment in OpenAI, travel to Oracle as OpenAI's spending, and return to Nvidia as Oracle's chip purchase, and at each stop it can be booked as a different thing by a different company.

That is the pattern that repeats across the season, and OpenAI sits at the center of a set of commitments that in aggregate have been described as roughly a trillion dollars for the year. There was also a deal with a second chip vendor that took the structure a step further, handing OpenAI warrants for a large equity stake that vests as OpenAI buys more of that vendor's chips, which means the customer gets richer the more it spends with the supplier. None of this is hidden. It was announced openly and the market loved it, sending one of these companies' shares up more than a third in a single day and adding tens of billions to the fortunes of the people at the center. The enthusiasm was not irrational, but it was enthusiasm about a structure that deserves a closer look than a one-day stock pop gives it.

## Circularity by itself is not fraud and not even unusual

I want to be fair to the bull case, because circularity on its own is not evidence of anything wrong. Strategic investment and vendor financing are ordinary tools, and ecosystems genuinely do bootstrap this way. A chip maker investing in the customer who will buy its chips is aligning incentives, and a cloud provider signing a giant supply contract before it has the customers to fill it is making the same kind of forward bet that built the cloud in the first place. If you own the picks and the shovels, seeding the miners so they can afford your picks is a defensible way to expand the whole market, and if the underlying demand is real then the loop is just the plumbing that lets a young industry build ahead of demand rather than lagging it. So I do not think the existence of the loop tells you the deals are hollow. Plenty of durable industries have this exact wiring in their early years, and it looked circular then too.

The reason it still makes me uneasy is that the same properties that make the loop efficient when demand is real make it dangerous when demand is soft, and from the outside you cannot always tell which world you are in. It is the habit behind the [investments](/investments) tools I keep, separating what a structure looks like from what actually services it.

## What would actually make it dangerous

So here is where I would land on the risk, as precisely as I can. The circularity becomes dangerous under a few specific conditions rather than as a general matter. The first is if the loop is substituting for end demand instead of seeding it. As long as there is genuine paying demand at the end of the chain, the circular financing is just how the capacity got built early, but if the demand at the end is thin and the companies are mostly buying from each other to sustain the appearance of a booming market, then you have manufactured revenue that flatters everyone's numbers until it stops.

The second condition is leverage. Strategic investment made with equity is a bet the investor can afford to lose, but if these commitments increasingly get funded with debt, then a shortfall in demand does not just disappoint shareholders, it triggers obligations that have to be paid regardless of whether the revenue showed up. The telecom buildout of the late 1990s is the cautionary version of this, where equipment makers financed their own customers' purchases, booked the sales, and then watched the whole thing unwind when the customers could not pay and the vendor financing turned into losses on the lender's own books. The third condition is concentration, and this one is already present, because so much of the structure runs through a single company at the center. A loop with one indispensable node is only as sound as that node, and if the entity everyone is counting as their future revenue stumbles, the accounting that looked like an ecosystem reveals itself as a single point of failure.

## The pattern I would pull out of this

The pattern I would pull out of this fall is that circularity is a risk amplifier, not a verdict. It does not by itself tell you whether these deals are visionary or reckless, but it does tell you that the honest and the fragile versions of this story look almost identical from the outside right up until the end demand is tested, and it magnifies whatever the truth turns out to be. So I am not calling it a bubble, because I do not think the word earns its keep, and I am not calling it an ecosystem, because that word is doing public relations work. What I would watch, quarter by quarter, is whether real outside customers are paying enough for AI to service the loop, and whether the commitments are being funded with money the players can afford to lose. If both hold, the circular financing was just the scaffolding. If either breaks, the same structure that looked like alignment this fall is the mechanism through which one company's problem becomes everyone's at once.
