# Spain Won the 2026 World Cup by Making Argentina Wait for a Chance That Never Came

I ranked Spain first before this World Cup because I thought they were the most complete team in the field, but I did not expect the case to end up being this literal. They won the tournament by beating Argentina 1-0 after extra time, and that score makes the final look like a narrow contest settled by one late bounce. The match was much clearer than that. Spain produced the first twenty attempts, took nine of the first ten corners, conceded only one attempt before the final whistle, and needed a World Cup final record twelve saves from Emiliano Martinez to keep the game scoreless as long as it was. Ferran Torres supplied the goal in the 106th minute, but Spain had been supplying the argument for nearly two hours.

The strange thing about the final is that Argentina's resistance was both extraordinary and almost entirely defensive. Martinez kept saving everything, Spain kept recycling the ball and coming again, and Lionel Scaloni's side kept waiting for the transition or Messi touch that had rescued them all tournament. That approach had worked against Egypt when Argentina came back from two goals down, and it worked against England when Enzo Fernandez and Lautaro Martinez turned a semifinal in the final minutes. Spain did not give them that opening. [The Associated Press recorded Argentina's first attempt](https://apnews.com/article/argentina-spain-world-cup-final-score-messi-fccc26aa12d9226e63d06b601b770617) in the second half of extra time, by which point Enzo had already been sent off for a second yellow card and Argentina were chasing the match with ten men.

Torres came on in the 62nd minute and scored his first goal of the tournament when a bouncing ball dropped inside the area. He took it with his left foot and put it under the crossbar, which is the kind of finish that becomes larger with every replay because of the context around it. I think the more revealing detail is what happened afterward. Spain stayed composed, and Argentina never found a wave of chances. Giuliano Simeone put one late effort over after a Messi corner took a useful skip, but the defending champions never built the sustained pressure they had found in the previous two rounds. Spain closed the match the same way they had controlled it, with the ball, with their spacing, and with a back line that had spent the entire tournament removing the opponent's best route to goal.

That defense is the real record. Spain conceded once across eight matches, fewer goals than any World Cup champion before them, and Unai Simon finished with six clean sheets in seven starts. I spent most of the countdown writing about Rodri, Lamine Yamal, Pedri, Nico Williams, and the way Spain can keep the ball without becoming passive, because those are the obvious reasons to pick them. The tournament ended up turning on the less glamorous part. Pau Cubarsi and Aymeric Laporte let Spain play high without constantly getting exposed, Simon stopped the few shots that reached him, and the midfield treated defensive control as something that started with where the next pass went. What I think this means is that Spain did not win by choosing between possession and protection. Their possession was the protection.

The scale of the run is hard to dismiss as one good month. Spain leave the final unbeaten in 38 matches, with 29 wins and nine draws, the longest unbeaten run by a European men's national team. They are also the first country to hold the men's and women's World Cups at the same time, and this second men's title arrives sixteen years after the 2010 team won its final by the same score after extra time. The comparison is obvious, but this version feels broader to me. The 2010 side could suffocate a match with control. This one can do that, attack from both wings, change its midfield shape, and bring on the eventual match winner after an hour.

Argentina deserve a cleaner read than the final statistics alone give them. They reached another final four years after winning in Qatar, Messi scored eight times in what he has indicated was his last World Cup, and the older core kept producing the late interventions that had defined Scaloni's tenure. Martinez gave them a chance to steal a match in which almost nothing else worked. But the final also showed the limit of living on those interventions. Enzo's dismissal removed what little margin remained, Messi could not manufacture one more escape, and a side that had made a habit of scoring at least twice finished with one real opening.

The pattern I would pull out of this final is that Spain were the best team before the tournament, the best team across it, and the better team in the match that decided it. That sounds obvious now, but knockout football usually finds a way to separate those claims. Spain kept them together. I had them first in my [pre-tournament ranking](/writing/world-cup-2026-contenders-1-spain), and after 104 matches the tournament landed in the most defensible place it could have landed, with the trophy in the hands of the side that gave opponents the fewest ways to beat it.
