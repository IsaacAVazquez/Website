# Ranking the 2026 World Cup Contenders: How I Built My Top Ten

The 2026 World Cup kicks off on June 11, and over the next few weeks I am going to count down the ten teams I think are most likely to win it, starting at number ten and working toward the team I would put my own credibility behind at number one. This piece is the foundation for that series. I want to be upfront about how I built the list before I start defending individual placements, because a ranking is only worth reading if you can see the reasoning underneath it.

There are forty-eight teams in this tournament for the first time, twelve groups of four feeding a new round of thirty-two. That expansion matters for the countdown, because it widens the gap between the handful of teams that can realistically lift the trophy and everyone else who is mostly playing for a memorable run. My top ten is a list of genuine contenders, not a list of good teams.

## The four things I weighed

I did not want to lean on a single source, because every single source has a blind spot. Betting markets are sharp but they price in public money and narrative. The FIFA ranking is objective but slow, and it rewards results that are already months old. Models are rigorous but they can only see what they can measure. So I built the order around four spines and paid the most attention to where they agreed.

The first is the betting market. As of early June, the outright title odds across the major books tell a clear story at the top. Spain sits around +450 and France around +475, close enough that they are effectively co-favorites, and the no-vig prediction markets like Kalshi and Polymarket have them level at roughly seventeen percent each. England is a clean third near +700. Then there is a logjam of Argentina, Brazil and Portugal between +850 and +1000, Germany as the bridge at +1400, and everything from the Netherlands at +2000 downward sitting in long-shot territory. I should say plainly that I treat odds as analysis and not as advice, and that these numbers move hourly this close to kickoff, so anything I quote is a snapshot rather than a guarantee.

The second spine is the FIFA World Ranking from April, which actually puts France first and Spain second, with Argentina third, England fourth and Portugal fifth. The next ranking lands on June 11, so it may shuffle, but the April order is a useful counterweight to the betting market because it disagrees in interesting places.

The third is the Opta supercomputer, which ran the tournament thousands of times in May and came out with Spain as the favorite at roughly sixteen percent, France next at thirteen, England at eleven and Argentina at ten. The detail I keep coming back to is that Spain is the only team the model rates as better than a coin flip to reach the quarterfinals. Nobody else in the field clears fifty percent to get that far.

The fourth spine is the part that no model fully captures, and it is where I earn or lose your trust as a ranker. I looked hard at current form, at the announced squads, and above all at the draw. Every one of these teams has now named its twenty-six, and the rosters carry real signal. I also weighed how hard each group is and how brutal the projected knockout path looks, because a championship-grade squad in the wrong quarter of the bracket is a different proposition than the same squad with a clear road.

## Why the draw changes everything

If you take one thing from this introduction, make it the draw, because it is the reason my order is not just the betting board copied out. When FIFA seeded the bracket, it separated Spain and Argentina into opposite halves, which means the defending champions and the Opta favorite can only meet in the final. The top four seeds cannot collide before the semifinals. That structure rewards the teams that landed soft groups and punishes the ones that did not.

France is the cleanest example. On talent alone France might be my number one, because it has the deepest squad in the tournament and just rose to FIFA's top spot. But France drew the Group of Death, the only group with three top-twenty-five teams, sharing it with Senegal and a Norway side carrying Erling Haaland in the form of his life. That is the hardest possible start for a top seed, and it is enough to keep France a notch below Spain in my ranking even though I think the French squad is marginally better. Germany sits at the other extreme, with the softest draw of any contender, which is a real argument for taking a flawed team further than its raw talent suggests.

## The shape of my top ten

I am not going to spoil the full order here, because the whole point of a countdown is the reveal. But I will tell you the shape of it. The top is a two-horse race between Spain and France that I went back and forth on more than any other call. England is my clear third on the strength of flawless qualifying, even with the country's long history of falling just short. Then comes the most interesting cluster in the field, the defending champions Argentina with Lionel Messi's sixth and final World Cup, a five-time champion Brazil chasing the end of a twenty-four-year title drought under Carlo Ancelotti, and a Portugal side with what might be the best midfield at the tournament and Cristiano Ronaldo chasing the one trophy that has always escaped him.

The back half of my ten is where the disagreements get sharper. Germany made it on the back of that easy draw and a young creative core in Jamal Musiala and Florian Wirtz, even after two straight group-stage exits that should make anyone cautious. The Netherlands made it on the strength of its defense, though losing Xavi Simons to an ACL tear in April genuinely hurt them. And the final two places came down to a bubble of teams that I will defend properly when I get to them, including a Morocco side with real semifinal pedigree from 2022 but a manager who only took the job in March, and a Belgium golden generation getting one last shot at it.

## The dark horse I could not fit in

The hardest team to leave out was Norway. The Opta model actually rates Norway ahead of a couple of teams I included, the qualifying campaign was perfect, and Haaland scored sixteen goals across eight matches to drag a nation back to the World Cup for the first time since 1998. If I were ranking ceilings, Norway would be in the ten. The reason they are not is the floor. This is a group of players with no tournament experience together, drawn into the Group of Death, where finishing third and flying home after three games is a completely live outcome. A contender, to me, has to have a believable floor as well as a high ceiling, and Norway's floor is the lowest of any team I seriously considered.

There are a few others worth keeping an eye on as the group stage unfolds. Colombia has James Rodríguez and Luis Díaz and the talent to ruin somebody's bracket. Uruguay under Marcelo Bielsa drew straight into Spain's group, which is cruel, but the squad is good enough to make it ugly. Croatia keeps doing this, a 2018 finalist and a 2022 semifinalist that is older now but never seems to read the script. And the hosts are a separate conversation. The United States drew the hardest group to call and lost its final warm-up to Germany, so I have it as a sentiment play rather than a contender, with Mexico and Canada in the same boat despite friendlier draws.

That is the frame. The countdown starts now, at [number ten with Belgium](/writing/world-cup-2026-contenders-10-belgium), and I will make the full case for each team one at a time, from the squad and the history to the path through the bracket and where I think the betting market has it wrong. If the tournament does what tournaments do, some of these placements will look smart and some will look foolish within two weeks, and I will own both. Let's start at the bottom of the list and work up.
