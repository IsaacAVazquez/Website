# What Actually Mattered at CES 2026, Once You Discount the Robots

Everyone I know came back from the CES coverage talking about humanoids. The framing this year was "physical AI," meaning robots that see and reason and act in the world, and it took the buzzy spot that agentic AI held twelve months ago. I understand why. A cowboy-hatted humanoid checking people in at Nvidia's booth is a better photo than a rack of GPUs, and Jensen Huang opening with a line about "the ChatGPT moment for physical AI" is built to be quoted. But CES rewards the demo, and the demo is the least reliable signal in the building. The honest exercise every January is separating what actually ships from what was a staged concept built to be filmed, and when I run that filter over this year, the humanoids fall to the bottom and the datacenter silicon and the token-cost math rise to the top. Let me walk through what I'd keep.

## The physical AI story was the headline and the hardest thing to verify

The show floor obliged the theme, with humanoid robots from what felt like a dozen vendors, Samsung leaning into AI appliances, and Nvidia's own set of demo bots including one simulating surgery and one helping people check in at the event. I don't want to be dismissive of the underlying research, because some of it is real. Nvidia showed a new version of its Gr00t vision-language models that turn sensor input into robot body control, and a Cosmos model aimed at reasoning and planning, and that's genuine infrastructure that other people will build on. But a controlled booth demo tells you almost nothing about whether a humanoid works in an actual warehouse or kitchen, on a bad day, with a person standing in the wrong place. The gap between "we filmed it doing the thing once" and "it does the thing reliably at a cost that makes sense" is exactly where CES optimism goes to die every year, and physical hardware has a much longer road from demo to deployment than software does. So I'd file the humanoids under interesting, unproven, and at least a couple of years away from the numbers mattering. The version of robotics I take more seriously showed up quieter, with Hyundai pushing into the space through Boston Dynamics and working with Google's AI research group to train its Atlas humanoids. That's aimed at industrial deployment with named partners and real balance sheets behind it, which is a different thing from a robot that waves at a camera.

## The Vera Rubin news is the part that actually moves the industry

The announcement I'd circle is Nvidia's Vera Rubin platform, which is boring in exactly the way that matters. It's six new chips, including a Vera CPU and a Rubin GPU, and Huang put the headline at roughly ten times the throughput of the current Grace Blackwell generation with about a tenfold reduction in token cost. That second number is the one I care about, because token cost is the input to nearly every AI product decision being made right now. A 10x reduction, if it holds up outside the keynote and in real deployments, changes what is economically viable to build, and whole categories of product that don't pencil out at today's inference prices suddenly start to. That's the mechanism by which a spec sheet turns into new companies. The detail that stuck with me is that a Rubin pod draws about twice the power of the prior generation but can be cooled with 45-degree water, which sounds like a footnote and is actually most of the game, because the binding constraint on the AI buildout right now is power and cooling at the datacenter, not chips sitting on a shelf. Being able to run a denser system on hot water is the kind of unglamorous engineering that decides how much compute the industry can physically stand up this year, and it got a fraction of the humanoid coverage. Nvidia also showed a self-driving model called Alpamayo, pitched around a car understanding a genuinely novel situation like a child chasing a ball into the street, and that at least points at a real deployment target rather than a stage.

## The competitive silicon picture is finally a real race

The other thing worth keeping is that Nvidia wasn't alone at the rack level this year. AMD's Lisa Su showed the Helios rack system and previewed MI500 chips, Intel pushed its Core Ultra Series 3 AI PC parts, and Qualcomm showed the Snapdragon X Plus 2. None of that dislodges Nvidia in 2026, and I wouldn't pretend otherwise, because the software moat and the installed base are still doing most of the work. But the shift I find genuinely important is that the competition has moved up from the individual accelerator to the full rack system, which is the level enterprises actually buy and finance at. A credible second source at the rack level is what eventually pressures margins and hands buyers some leverage, and even the promise of it changes how the big datacenter deals get negotiated. So I read AMD's Helios less as this year's threat and more as the first sign that the every-hyperscaler-buys-only-Nvidia phase has a clock on it.

The pattern I'd pull out of this year's show is that CES pays out in attention, not in shipped product, and the two currencies diverge most exactly where the cameras point. The humanoids got the coverage and are the furthest from mattering. The token-cost curve and the rack-level silicon got the footnotes and are what I'll actually be tracking through the year, because they set the price of everything built on top of them. If you want a single question to carry out of Las Vegas, I don't think it's when the robots arrive. I think it's how fast the cost of a token falls and who can physically cool the buildings it runs in, and both of those got answered more honestly this January than anything with arms and legs.
