# My 2026 Predictions, With the Hedging Left In

Prediction season rewards boldness, and I've never liked that, because boldness and accuracy are different things and the incentives in a prediction post pull hard toward the former. A confident, specific, wrong call gets more attention than a hedged, roughly-right one, so the genre fills up with bold numbers nobody revisits in December. I'd rather be right than bold, which means the honest version of a predictions post has the uncertainty left in rather than sanded off. So here's what I actually believe about 2026, with the hedges intact, across the handful of things I think about most, from where agents go next, to model economics, to the capex bet underneath all of it, to what happens to tech jobs.

## Agents keep spreading, but unevenly and slower than the demos imply

I'm fairly confident agents keep spreading into more workflows in 2026, and much less confident about the pace. The reason I hedge on speed is that a lot of the agentic deployments in 2025 didn't actually deliver much, because teams pointed them at problems that weren't well-scoped or reached for general autonomy where narrow, well-defined tasks were what actually worked. So my real prediction is specific. The agents that spread in 2026 are the centrally-managed, tightly-scoped kind aimed at software, support, operations, and back-office work, and the "agent does your whole job" version stays a demo. I'd bet on breadth of small wins and against the dramatic version, and I'd put more confidence on the direction than on how far it gets by December.

## Model price-performance keeps improving and the frontier keeps commoditizing

I'm reasonably confident the per-token cost of a capable model keeps falling through 2026 and that the gap between the best model and the third-best on most real-world tasks stays small. What follows from that is that the interesting margin keeps moving away from raw capability and toward the things around it, from integration, to evaluation, to distribution and the workflow a model sits inside. The caveat I'll name honestly is that a genuine step-change, a model that's discontinuously better rather than incrementally so, would break this prediction, and I can't rule that out, because the labs are spending enough that a surprise is possible. I just wouldn't bet on it, and my base case is continued commoditization rather than a breakout.

## The real risk of 2026 is an AI-capex reckoning, and I don't know the timing

This is the prediction I'm least certain about and most concerned by. The hyperscaler capital spending plans for 2026 are enormous, on the order of three-quarters of a trillion dollars for the year, and the longer-range estimates from places like Morgan Stanley talk about something like three trillion dollars of AI infrastructure by 2028 with most of the spending still ahead. If the revenue to justify that materializes on roughly the timeline the spending assumes, then it holds together. If it doesn't, you get a reckoning, a pullback in capex that would ripple straight into growth and markets, because a meaningful share of recent growth is that spending. I'm not going to predict a crash in 2026, because I genuinely don't know the timing and neither does anyone claiming to. What I'll say is that the probability of a visible air-pocket at some point is high enough that I'd plan for it rather than be surprised by it, and that the timing is the part I'd treat as unknowable rather than pretend to have pinned down.

## The tech job market keeps splitting rather than simply shrinking

I'm confident about the direction here and hedged on the magnitude. The story isn't the simple one where AI takes the jobs, it's a divergence, with people who have AI-native skills doing better on both pay and hiring, and roles built around routine production compressing. The wage data already points this way, with reporting through 2025 suggesting workers with advanced AI skills command something like a 56% pay premium over peers in the same roles without them. My prediction is that gap widens in 2026 rather than closes. The part I won't pretend to be confident about is the junior pipeline, because if entry-level roles built around routine work keep compressing, the path people used to take toward senior judgment gets narrower, and I don't have a confident read on how that resolves. It's a real problem, it's unresolved, and anyone who tells you they know how it plays out is guessing.

The pattern I'd pull out of this is that the honest 2026 outlook is mostly continuation with one fat tail. Agents spreading, models commoditizing, and the job market splitting are all continuations of trends that were already visible by the end of 2025, and they're matters of degree rather than surprises. The capex bet is the exception, the one thing that could make 2026 look nothing like the year before it, and it's exactly the thing I'm least able to time. So if I'm keeping myself honest, my strongest conviction is the mundane one, that most of next year rhymes with this one, and my biggest worry is the one I can't put a date on. I'd rather tell you that plainly than hand you a confident number I'd have to walk back later.
