# The New Baseline: AI Skills Every PM Internship Will Expect in 2026

AI fluency in PM recruiting has crossed from differentiator to baseline. In 2024, mentioning that you used AI tools in your process was a talking point. In 2026, not being able to speak concretely about how you use AI in research, synthesis, and execution is the gap that interviewers notice. The expectation is not that you have a complex AI stack. It is that you have thought about where AI belongs in PM work and have real examples of using it well.

Here is what that looks like by skill area, and what it signals when it is missing.

## Research synthesis

The PM skill that AI has changed most significantly is synthesizing large amounts of qualitative information quickly. User interview transcripts, support ticket themes, competitive intelligence, stakeholder inputs — the job of turning this into a coherent problem statement used to take days. It can now be compressed substantially.

What competent looks like here is being able to describe a specific synthesis workflow. Not "I used Claude to summarize my interviews" but something like: "I extracted distinct problems and direct quotes from each transcript separately, then ran a cross-document pattern analysis to find themes that appeared across multiple users, then manually reviewed the groupings to look for contradictions and edge cases before writing the problem statement." The manual review step and the contradiction-seeking step are what separate someone who understands what they are doing from someone who is just running things through a chatbot.

What signals you have not kept up is describing synthesis in terms of time savings without describing the process. Saying "AI helps me analyze research faster" is technically true and tells the interviewer nothing about whether you trust the output appropriately or whether you have built judgment about when it misleads.

## PRD and spec drafting

Most PM interview processes now include some version of a written deliverable — a product brief, a one-pager, a feature spec. The expectation is no longer that you did not use AI to draft it. The expectation is that the output reflects genuine thinking that AI helped structure and express, not a generic output that AI generated and you lightly edited.

The tell is specificity. A spec that AI generated from a generic prompt sounds like every other spec. A spec that reflects the actual research you did, the specific tradeoffs you weighed, and the judgment calls you made about scope reads differently. Interviewers who read a lot of these can tell the difference.

What competent looks like is being able to talk about which sections of a spec you drafted yourself, which sections you used AI to structure and then rewrote, and specifically where AI wanted to include things you cut and why. That level of engagement with the drafting process signals that you were the author and AI was the tool.

## Data and analytics interpretation

PM roles increasingly involve working with data directly — running basic queries, interpreting dashboards, forming a view on what a metric change means. AI has changed this work by making it possible to get to an initial analysis significantly faster, but it has also made it easier to produce analysis that sounds rigorous without being so.

What competent looks like is being able to describe what you use AI for in data work and what you do not. Using AI to suggest the right SQL query structure or to check the logic of a calculation is appropriate use. Using AI to form the hypothesis about what a metric change means or to decide whether a result is significant is the part where you need to be the one doing the thinking.

What signals a gap is being unable to talk about what you verify manually in data work. If everything comes from the AI and you have not developed a habit of pressure-testing the analysis against your own intuition and against the underlying data, that becomes apparent quickly in a technical interview or in a case where the data is ambiguous.

## Experimentation design

The ability to design a clean A/B test — define the hypothesis, identify the right metric, size the experiment correctly, anticipate confounders — is a core PM skill that AI can assist with but cannot replace. AI is useful for checking your experiment design for obvious errors and for generating edge cases you may have missed. The actual judgment about what you are testing and why requires understanding the product and the user deeply enough that a model without that context cannot do it for you.

What competent looks like is knowing which parts of experiment design AI can help with and being specific about how you use it. "I described the experiment setup to Claude and asked it to find the three most likely ways this test would give a misleading result" is a useful pattern. "I asked AI to design the experiment" is not.

## Prompt engineering as a transferable skill

One thing I have found useful to be explicit about in recruiting conversations is that prompt engineering — knowing how to get useful output from AI systems — is itself a PM skill that transfers across tools and contexts. The same thinking that goes into writing a useful prompt goes into writing a useful spec, a useful brief, and a useful user research question. Being precise about what you want, anticipating how your input might be misinterpreted, and checking the output critically against your intent are skills that show up everywhere in PM work.

The candidates who stand out in this area are not the ones who know the most tools. They are the ones who have developed genuine judgment about how to work with AI systems well — what to give them, what to withhold, when to trust the output, and when to push back on it. That judgment is what is actually being evaluated when an interviewer asks how you use AI in your work.

## How to demonstrate this in recruiting

The clearest signal you can send is having a concrete example for each area. Not a general statement about being comfortable with AI tools, but a specific story about a time you used AI in research synthesis or drafting or data work and what you actually did differently because of it. Include what the AI got wrong or where you had to override it, because that is the part that shows you are the one driving.

The goal is to demonstrate that AI makes your judgment faster and more evidence-backed, not that AI substitutes for judgment you have not developed. That distinction is the whole game in 2026 PM recruiting.
