# World Cup 2026 Final: Spain Are Champions and Messi's Last Act Ends One Goal Short

For a month this tournament told everyone the same story about itself, that 48 teams and three hosts and a longer path had finally blown the old hierarchy open, and that anything could happen. And it could, right up until the last game. The final was [Spain](/world-cup-2026?team=spain) against [Argentina](/world-cup-2026?team=argentina), the team I ranked first in my [pre-tournament top ten](/writing/world-cup-2026-top-ten-contenders) against the team I ranked fourth, and Spain won it one-nil in extra time to take their second World Cup. What I keep coming back to, a few hours after the final whistle at MetLife, is that the most expanded, most chaotic-feeling World Cup in the sport's history produced about the most old-fashioned outcome it could have. The best team won.

I do not say that to flatten the drama, because the game itself was tense in the specific way a scoreless final is tense, and it turned on a single moment deep into extra time. But the shape of it was Spain being Spain, and I think the result is more a confirmation of what this Spain side is than an upset of anything.

## How the final actually went

Ninety minutes came and went without a goal, and if you watched the whole tournament that will not surprise you, because Spain spent six games making the game happen at their tempo and Argentina spent this one content to let them have the ball and wait for the half-chance that a Messi team can always manufacture. It was not a bad final, it was a careful one, two sides who both understood exactly how the other wanted to hurt them and spent an hour and a half refusing to allow it. Argentina's best spell came right at the end of regulation, the stretch where you could feel the whole thing tilting toward the one piece of Messi magic that would have settled it, and it did not quite arrive.

The goal came in the 106th minute, early in extra time, and it went to [Ferran Torres](/world-cup-2026?team=spain), which is fitting in a way I did not see coming but probably should have. Spain's tournament was never about one forward carrying them, it was about the collective wearing you down until someone arrived in the right place, and that is exactly how the winner looked. One-nil in extra time of a World Cup final is the scoreline of a team that controls risk better than anyone else in the field, and Spain conceded so little across the whole tournament that a one-goal final was almost the natural conclusion of how they had played the entire month.

## Spain won it the way they won all of it

I ranked Spain first before a ball was kicked because they were the most complete team in the field, and I never talked myself out of it even when the Round of 16 was putting Brazil and Germany and the Netherlands out and making the whole bracket feel unsafe. The case for Spain was always that repeatable beats spectacular over seven games, that a side which controls the ball and the tempo and gives up almost nothing does not need to be the most thrilling team to be the last one standing. That is what happened. They were not the team that produced the tournament's best highlight, but they were the team that never lost control of a game, and in a knockout format that is the trait that survives.

The semifinal against [France](/world-cup-2026?team=france) was the real test, the two most complete sides in the [last four](/writing/world-cup-2026-quarterfinals-recap), and Spain won it two-nil with the same suffocating control they brought to the final. When I watch a team beat France and Argentina back to back by keeping both of them at arm's length, I stop looking for the asterisk. This was not a soft draw or a lucky run. It was the best team in the tournament winning the tournament.

## Messi's last act ends one goal short

The story I flagged before any of this started was that Argentina's run was the last act of Lionel Messi, and Argentina kept finding another gear whenever that story needed one, all the way to a final. It just did not find the last one. There is a version of this final where a single moment falls his way in the eighty-fifth minute and the entire framing of the tournament changes, and it was that close, and it did not happen. Losing a World Cup final one-nil in extra time is a cruel way for a career like that to close, and I am not going to pretend the analytics make it feel any less like the wrong ending for the player.

What I would say, trying to be fair rather than sentimental, is that Argentina overachieved the seeding to even be there. I had them fourth, they were the fourth-best team on paper, and they reached the final by being more than the sum of their parts every time it mattered. They ran into the one side in the world built specifically to deny them the game they wanted to play. That is not a collapse. It is the best team beating a very good team, which is what finals usually are once you strip the narrative off them.

## England took third, and the third-place game was the anti-final

The one result that broke the tournament's late turn toward control was the third-place game, where [England](/world-cup-2026?team=england) beat France six-four, Bukayo Saka scored a hat trick, and Jude Bellingham settled a ten-goal game in stoppage time. It was the exact opposite of the final, two tired teams with nothing structural left to protect trading blows in a game that no longer needed to be careful, and it was the most entertaining ninety minutes of the knockout stage precisely because the stakes had drained out of it. England finishing third, having lost the semifinal to Argentina two-one, is a real step and also the same open question they carried in, which is whether they can win the one game where the other side is also trying to control it.

The pattern I would pull out of the whole tournament is that the 48-team format widened the door and then produced the most conventional result possible. The first three weeks belonged to the teams who made the expansion feel like a genuine reshuffle, and the last week belonged to the four sides that were always most likely to be here, and the final belonged to the one I thought was best. The format debate will keep running, and it should, but the tournament itself just answered the loudest version of it. You can add sixteen teams and three weeks and a reseeded bracket, and the best team can still win.
