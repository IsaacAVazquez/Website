# The Hyperscalers Keep Raising the AI Spend, and the Bill Comes Due Later

The reaction to this earnings season has settled into a comfortable consensus that I think is only half right. The four big hyperscalers all reported, all raised their AI capital spending guidance again, and the market mostly cheered, treating a higher number as proof of conviction and rewarding the stocks that spent the most. The story being told is that these companies see demand the rest of us cannot, and that the willingness to keep raising the spend is itself the bullish signal. I do not entirely disagree, and I am not in the camp that thinks this is obviously a bubble. But the thing I keep watching is not the size of the spend. It is the distance between the capital going out the door and the revenue that is eventually supposed to justify it, and the fact that almost no one on these calls wants to talk about when that distance is supposed to close.

## The numbers went up again, and the market liked it

The specifics from this quarter are worth stating plainly because the trajectory is the whole story. Alphabet raised its 2025 capital expenditure guidance to around $85 billion, up from the $75 billion it had guided to earlier in the year, which is a $10 billion increase announced almost in passing. Meta raised the top of its own range as well and spent much of its call talking about staffing a superintelligence effort, framing the spend as an investment in a capability rather than a cost. Microsoft reported more than $30 billion of capital spending in a single quarter and signaled that the next fiscal year would be higher still, all while Azure kept growing at a rate that let management point to demand it said it could not fully meet. Amazon guided toward roughly $100 billion for the year on the back of AWS. Add it up across the four and you are looking at combined spending well into the several hundreds of billions a year, and the direction of every revision was up.

What struck me is how thoroughly the framing has shifted. Two years ago a capex increase of this size would have been something a CFO had to defend, and now it is something the market treats as a feature. The companies have learned that the way to be rewarded is to spend confidently and to describe the spending as demand-constrained rather than supply-constrained, meaning the story is always that they would deploy even more if they could get the chips and the power. That may well be true. But "we would spend more if we could" is a statement about appetite, not about returns, and the two are easy to conflate when the stock is going up.

## The revenue is real but it is not the size of the spend

The part that gets underweighted is that the AI revenue is genuinely showing up, it is just not showing up at anything close to the scale of the investment yet. Cloud growth reaccelerated for the providers that lead on AI, and the backlog figures, the contracted future revenue these companies disclose, got larger. That is not nothing, and it is the honest bull case. But if you are putting several hundred billion dollars a year into data centers, the incremental revenue attributable to AI specifically still has to grow into that number, and right now the gap between the two is very wide. The spend is being justified by a forecast of demand rather than by demand that has already arrived, and a forecast is a fragile thing to underwrite tens of billions of concrete and silicon against.

I do not think that makes the spending irrational. I think it makes it a bet with a long fuse, where the downside is not that the demand never comes but that it comes later and slower than the depreciation schedule assumes. GPUs do not age gracefully. A cluster bought today is on a clock, and if the revenue that pays it back shows up three years late, a lot of that hardware is closer to obsolete by the time it earns out. That is the specific risk I would underline, not that AI is fake, but that the timing between when you spend and when you collect is exactly the kind of mismatch that has punished infrastructure builders before even when they were fundamentally right.

## We have watched this shape before, twice

The comparison I find most useful is the fiber buildout of the late 1990s. The thesis behind it, that internet traffic would grow enormously and that whoever owned the fiber would own the future, turned out to be completely correct. It was also brutal in the interim. The builders laid far more capacity than near-term demand could use, a great deal of it sat dark for years, and several of the companies that did the building went bankrupt before the demand they had correctly predicted actually arrived. The people who were right about the destination still lost, because they were early and levered and the timing ran them over.

The cloud buildout is the more encouraging comparison and I think the more relevant one. For years Amazon poured capital into AWS while plenty of analysts questioned the spend, and that bet compounded into one of the best businesses of the last two decades. So there is a real version of this where the hyperscalers are simply doing the AWS playbook again at larger scale and the doubters look foolish in a decade. The difference that matters is balance sheet and funding. The cloud buildout was largely self-funded by companies that could afford it, and the ones I would worry about in this cycle are wherever the AI spend starts getting financed with debt or with vendor arrangements that assume the demand curve, because that is the ingredient that turned the fiber story from disappointing into catastrophic.

## The pattern I would pull out of this

The pattern I would pull out of this earnings season is that the market is currently pricing the spend as if being right about the destination is the same as being right about the timing, and it is not. I think the AI demand is real and that these companies are probably right about where it goes. But the gap between capex and the revenue that justifies it is the number I would track every quarter, not the capex figure by itself, because the whole history of infrastructure buildouts says the destination takes care of itself and the interim is where fortunes are made and lost. The question worth asking on each of these calls is not how much they are spending. It is how long they are asking shareholders to wait for the spend to earn out, and whether the money funding the wait is patient enough to actually get there.
