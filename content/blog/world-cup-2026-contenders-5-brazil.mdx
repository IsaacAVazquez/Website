# World Cup 2026 Countdown, No. 5: Brazil, Ancelotti, and a 24-Year Itch

At number five in my [2026 World Cup countdown](/writing/world-cup-2026-top-ten-contenders) is the most decorated nation in the history of the sport, and that is exactly why this placement is complicated. Brazil has five World Cups, more than anyone, and has not won any of them since 2002. An entire generation of Brazilians has grown up without seeing their country lift the trophy, which for Brazil is a kind of national emergency. I have them fifth because the talent is unquestionably top-five, but the way this squad was built carries more risk than any other team near the top of my list.

## The weight of the drought

You cannot rank Brazil without understanding the pressure they carry, because it shapes everything about how they play at a World Cup. Since that 2002 title, the story has been a slow accumulation of pain. There was the 7-1 loss to Germany on home soil in 2014, a result so traumatic it still gets referenced in ordinary conversation. There were quarterfinal exits in 2018 and in 2022, the latter a penalty-shootout loss to Croatia in a game Brazil dominated and somehow did not win. Twenty-four years matches the longest the country has ever gone without a World Cup, the same span as the gap between 1970 and 1994, and that history sits on this team whether the players talk about it or not.

## A foreign coach and some bold calls

The biggest change is on the touchline. Carlo Ancelotti is the first foreign manager to lead Brazil into a World Cup, which for a country that has always seen the national team as an expression of its own identity is a genuine cultural shift. Ancelotti is also one of the most accomplished tournament coaches alive, and the bet Brazil has made is that his calm and his knockout-football pedigree are exactly what a talented but psychologically scarred team needs.

He named his squad on May 18 in Rio, and he was not afraid to make statements. Vinícius Júnior is the focal point of the attack, and Marquinhos captains the side. The headline was the recall of Neymar at thirty-four, a player whose talent has never been in doubt but whose body has betrayed him repeatedly in recent years. The bigger shock, to me, was the other direction. Rodrygo, a Real Madrid regular, was left out of the squad entirely, alongside other notable names. That is a manager backing his own judgment hard, and it cuts both ways. If the calls land, Ancelotti looks like a genius who saw something the rest of us missed. If Neymar breaks down and the attack misfires, those same decisions will define the post-mortem.

## The talent is still ridiculous

For all the questions about how it was assembled, the raw quality here is enormous. Vinícius on the left is one of the most dangerous attacking players in the world on his day, and Raphinha has grown into a leader who scores and creates in equal measure. The supporting cast of Gabriel Martinelli, Matheus Cunha and Richarlison gives Ancelotti more attacking options than almost any team in the tournament. When this group clicks, there is no defense on earth that looks comfortable. The question, as it has been for a decade, is whether Brazil can be more than a collection of brilliant individuals when the pressure of a knockout game tightens around them.

## The path

Brazil drew Group C with Morocco, Haiti and Scotland, and it is trickier than the usual gentle Brazil draw because Morocco is a genuine contender in its own right, a 2022 semifinalist that beat Brazil in a friendly back in 2023. I still expect Brazil to top the group, but that Morocco game is a real test and a measuring stick rather than a formality. From there the bracket points toward a quarterfinal against England, which would be one of the marquee ties of the entire tournament and exactly the kind of game that has ended Brazil's recent World Cups.

## The betting angle

The market has Brazil around +800 to +900, level with Argentina, and the books have not really settled which of the two South American giants is the stronger bet, which is itself interesting. The best available price is +900, and I would take that over the shorter +800 at one book. In the staged markets Brazil sits around +430 to reach the final. I read these numbers as analysis rather than advice, and they will move as the group stage plays out, but the rough message is clear, the market sees Brazil as a genuine contender that is a notch below the Spain-France-England tier and roughly tied with the defending champions.

## Where I land

Brazil at five is a bet on ceiling over certainty. I rank them a hair above Portugal because the attacking talent is deeper and the individual ceiling is higher, and because Ancelotti is the kind of coach who has steadied bigger names in bigger moments. But I want to be honest that this is the most volatile team in my top six. The Neymar gamble and the Rodrygo omission are the decisions that will define whether Ancelotti is celebrated or second-guessed, and a squad carrying twenty-four years of drought into a projected quarterfinal against England is not a safe bet, it is a swing for the fences. I think it is a swing worth respecting.

That is my number five. From here, the countdown reaches the four teams I genuinely believe can win this tournament. At number four is the defending champion, the team that holds the trophy right now, walking into what its greatest player has confirmed will be his sixth and final World Cup. [Argentina](/writing/world-cup-2026-contenders-4-argentina), and the last act of Lionel Messi, is next.
