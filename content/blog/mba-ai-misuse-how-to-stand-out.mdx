# How MBAs Misuse AI (and How to Stand Out Instead)

I have been in enough recruiting conversations, case prep sessions, and class discussions over the past year to notice that the ways MBAs misuse AI are predictable. Not because people are careless or lazy, but because the failure modes are intuitive. If AI can produce something that looks like the output you need, the temptation to use it as a shortcut is real, and the cost of doing so is not always immediately obvious.

The problem is that the costs do show up. Just not where you expect them.

## The substitution trap

The most common pattern is using AI to produce the surface of thinking without doing the underlying thinking. Writing a case analysis by prompting for a framework rather than developing your own read on the situation. Drafting a position paper for a class by feeding the prompt into Claude and editing the output. Preparing interview answers by having AI generate responses to behavioral questions and then memorizing them.

The outputs look fine. They hit the required structure, they use the right vocabulary, they are coherent and professional. The problem is that when a professor asks a follow-up question, or an interviewer pushes on the reasoning behind an answer, there is nothing there. The position was not built on a genuine point of view, so it cannot be defended.

I have heard this described as the difference between AI as a thinking partner and AI as a ghostwriter. The distinction is real. Using AI to stress-test your argument after you have formed it is different from having AI generate the argument and then attaching your name to it. The first makes you sharper. The second makes you dependent on something that will not be in the room when it matters.

## The class discussion problem

A version of the substitution trap shows up in class discussions in a specific way. Before class, some people use AI to generate a comprehensive set of observations about the case, internalize them, and then reproduce them in discussion. The problem is that what gets reproduced sounds generic, because it was generated without the texture of having actually thought through the case yourself.

What I read first is the case itself, and I let myself form an initial reaction before I use AI for anything. That initial reaction is often wrong or incomplete, but it is mine. When I bring Claude in to push back on my interpretation or surface frameworks I missed, I am evaluating its suggestions against a position I actually hold. That is different from starting with Claude's position and presenting it as my own, and the difference is audible in how I defend it when challenged.

## The interview tell

In PM recruiting specifically, the AI tell comes through in how candidates talk about their process. The giveaway is when someone describes what an AI output said rather than what they concluded. "Claude suggested that the main opportunity was..." is a different thing from "the research pointed to..." which is different again from "my read on this is..."

The interviewer does not care whether you used AI in your preparation. They care whether you have a point of view and can defend it. AI-generated opinions that you have not internalized cannot be defended, because you never actually held them. The moment you are asked to extend the argument or apply it to a different scenario, the gap between understanding and borrowing becomes clear.

What I do in interview prep is use AI to pressure-test the answers I have already written, not to generate answers I then learn. I write my behavioral stories in my own words, bring them to Claude to find where the structure breaks down or the impact is vague, and then rewrite them based on that feedback. The story is mine. The editing process is faster. The result holds up under follow-up because I actually lived it.

## What standing out looks like

The candidates and students who stand out are not the ones who use AI the most or the most elaborately. They are the ones who have developed a clear sense of what AI does well and where their own judgment is irreplaceable, and who have calibrated their use accordingly.

In practice that means forming your own view before consulting AI, using AI to challenge that view rather than to generate it, and being specific in your work about where AI helped and where you did the thinking yourself. That specificity is itself a signal. Someone who can say "I used Claude to flag the weaknesses in my argument, and here is what I changed and why" is demonstrating more sophisticated judgment than someone who cannot account for the decisions in their work.

The goal is not to avoid AI. It is to use it in ways that make your thinking more rigorous rather than in ways that let you skip the thinking. The distinction seems obvious when you say it that way. But in a competitive program with constant deadlines, the temptation to take the shortcut is real, and most people will take it at least some of the time. Recognizing when you are doing it is most of the skill.
