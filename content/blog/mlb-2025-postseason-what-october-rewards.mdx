# October Baseball Doesn't Crown the Best Team, It Crowns the Team That Fits October

Every fall the same sentence gets written when the World Series ends, some version of this team proved it was the best in baseball, and I have come to think that sentence is close to meaningless. Not because the champion is not good, since you cannot win eleven or thirteen games in October by accident, but because the thing the postseason measures is not the thing the regular season measures, and we keep pretending they are the same measurement. A hundred and sixty-two games is a test of depth and consistency across half a year. A best-of-five or best-of-seven series is a test of how a roster performs in a small number of high-leverage games against good pitching, and those are different tests that reward different rosters. The team that aces the first one is often not the team built to ace the second.

What I think this means is that we should read October less as a verdict on who is best and more as a verdict on who fits the format, because the format is doing an enormous amount of the work.

## Small samples reward the bullpen and the hot streak

The clearest way the postseason diverges from the season is sample size, and sample size is not a technicality here, it is the whole story. Over six months, a deep lineup and a reliable rotation grind out wins and the noise averages out. Over a five-game series, a team can ride three dominant relief arms and a couple of hot bats and beat a demonstrably deeper opponent, because there is not enough baseball for the depth to assert itself. This is why bullpen quality, and specifically a few high-leverage relievers a manager trusts in the seventh and eighth innings, matters far more in October than its regular-season weight suggests. The regular season is won by the twenty-sixth man and the fifth starter, and the postseason is won by the three relievers who never have to pitch a game that is out of hand.

## The expanded bracket made the regular season a weaker filter

The other thing that has changed the character of October is the expanded playoff field, which added teams and gave the top seeds a bye, and I do not think the incentives it created are fully appreciated. A bye is supposed to be a reward, and it is, but it also means the best regular-season teams sit for several days and then open against an opponent who arrives hot from a series they just had to win. Whether that rest is worth more than the rhythm the wild-card team built is genuinely unclear, and the fact that it is unclear is the point, because it means the format has softened the advantage that a great regular season is supposed to buy. When the reward for winning ninety-five games is a layoff against a streaking team, the ninety-five wins predict less about who advances than they used to.

## What actually travels to October is pitching you can shorten the game with

If the regular season and the postseason reward different things, the practical question is what travels, and my answer is pitching depth of a specific kind. Not innings, not a strong top-to-bottom rotation in the regular-season sense, but the ability to shorten a game, to get to a trusted arm early and hand the last few innings to relievers who miss bats. Teams that can turn a postseason game into a six-inning game with three shutdown relievers behind their best starter are built for October in a way their record may not capture, and teams that depend on outslugging opponents or on a deep but unspectacular staff tend to run into a night where the bats go quiet and there is no shutdown arm to bail them out. Offense is streaky in small samples and pitching that can suppress a hot lineup for a few innings is the thing that most reliably shows up.

## This is what makes October fun, and also why best is the wrong word

I want to be clear that none of this is a complaint, because the variance is exactly what makes the postseason compelling. The reason a lower seed can take down a juggernaut, the reason a series can turn on one reliever or one swing, is the same reason October baseball is more gripping than a random Tuesday in June, and I would not trade that for a format that just handed the trophy to the best regular-season team. But enjoying the variance and understanding it are not in tension, and understanding it means dropping the habit of treating the champion as proof of superiority. The champion is proof of fitting the format under pressure, which is a real and admirable thing, and a different thing than being the best team over a full season.

The pattern I would pull out of the postseason is that October is its own game with its own requirements, and the mistake we make every year is scoring it as if it were the regular season with higher stakes. It is not. It rewards bullpen depth over lineup depth, format fit over record, and the ability to shorten a game over the ability to grind one out, and the team that wins it earned something real that is simply not the same as being the best team in baseball. Once you hold those two ideas separately, the whole month makes more sense, and you stop being surprised when the best team goes home early.
