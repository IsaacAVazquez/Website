# Use AI to Interrogate Your Annual Plan, Not to Write It

Planning season is here, which means the pitch is everywhere, that you should point AI at your annual plan and let it draft your goals, your OKRs, your strategy narrative, and save yourself the weeks of work. I have tried enough of this to have a firm opinion, which is that it gets the leverage backwards. The place AI is weakest in planning is authoring the plan, because it will hand you a confident, well-structured, completely generic strategy that reads fine and commits you to nothing real. The place it is strongest is interrogating a plan you already drafted, stress-testing the assumptions, generating the scenarios you did not think of, and synthesizing the pile of inputs you never have time to read. The mistake is using it for the first thing and skipping the second, when the second is the part of planning that teams reliably rush and the first is the part that actually requires you.

What I think this means is that the useful division of labor is to keep authorship human and delegate interrogation to the model, because interrogation is tedious and mechanical in a way the model is good at, and authorship is a judgment call the model cannot actually make.

## Synthesis of the input pile is where AI earns its place in planning

Every planning cycle starts with a mountain of inputs nobody fully reads, last year's metrics, the customer feedback, the sales themes, the support tickets, the docs from every team, and the honest truth is that most plans get written from the handful of inputs the author happened to remember. This is exactly where AI helps, because it can actually work through the whole pile and pull out the recurring themes, the tensions between what different sources are saying, and the things that come up a lot but never made it into anyone's summary. I trust it for this because it is a reading-and-clustering task with a checkable result, and having a model surface the ten themes across a thousand pieces of feedback is a real improvement over the author's memory of the loudest three. The judgment about which themes matter is still mine, but the synthesis that feeds that judgment is work I am happy to hand off, and the plan is better for being built on all the inputs instead of the memorable ones.

## Scenario generation and pre-mortems are the model's best planning trick

The single most useful thing I do with AI in planning is adversarial, which is to hand it the draft plan and ask it to argue why it fails. A good pre-mortem, imagining the plan has already failed and working backward to why, is one of the most valuable exercises in planning and one of the hardest to do honestly, because the people who wrote the plan are motivated not to find its holes. A model has no such attachment, and asking it to generate the ways the plan goes wrong, the assumptions that turn out false, the dependencies that slip, the market that does not cooperate, surfaces risks the team talked itself out of seeing. Same with scenarios, because asking what the plan looks like if a key assumption breaks forces a robustness the optimistic base case never does. This is interrogation, not authorship, and it is where the model's lack of skin in the game becomes an asset instead of a liability.

## Letting AI author the goals is where the false confidence creeps in

The reason I am firm about not letting the model write the plan is that its output in that mode is seductive and empty. Ask an AI to write your strategy or your goals and it will produce something articulate, structured, and plausible, and that polish hides the fact that it is generic, because a model drafting a plan is pattern-matching to what plans look like, not making the specific bets your specific situation calls for. Real planning is choosing what not to do, committing to a small number of priorities at the cost of everything else, and those choices come from values, context, and tradeoffs the model does not hold and cannot feel the weight of. A plan that everyone can tell was authored by the model tends to be a plan nobody actually owns, and ownership is most of what makes a plan get executed, so the fluent draft is worse than a rougher one the team fought over.

## The prioritization judgment is the part that has to stay human

Underneath all of this is the thing that does not delegate, which is prioritization, because prioritization is not an information problem the model can solve, it is a values problem the team has to resolve. Deciding that this bet matters more than that one, that we will accept this risk to pursue this upside, that we will disappoint this stakeholder to serve that goal, these are judgments about what the organization cares about and what it is willing to trade, and no amount of synthesis produces them. The model can lay out the options, quantify the tradeoffs, and make the choice legible, and that is genuinely helpful, but the choice itself is the job, and handing it to the model is handing away the part of planning that was actually yours to do. I want the model to make the decision easier to see and harder to fudge, and I want the decision to be mine.

The pattern I would pull out of this is that planning has a mechanical half and a judgment half, and AI is strong at the first and weak at the second, so the winning move is to delegate the interrogation and keep the authorship. Let it read the whole input pile and surface the themes, let it attack your draft and generate the scenarios you avoided, and do not let it write your goals or make your priority calls, because those come from a place the model cannot reach. Used that way, AI makes a plan more robust and more honest, and used the other way it makes a plan more polished and less real, and the difference between those two outcomes is entirely about which half of the work you handed over.
