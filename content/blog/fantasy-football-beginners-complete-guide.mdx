# Fantasy Football for Beginners: What to Know Before Your First Draft

I remember my first fantasy draft as a blur of names I half-recognized and picks I immediately regretted. That's pretty normal. Fantasy football has a steep-ish learning curve not because it's complicated, but because there are a lot of small things to learn at once. Once they click, the game opens up.

I'll walk through what you actually need to know before your first season, from how scoring works to how drafts work to where most beginners go wrong.

## How It Works

You act as the general manager of a fictional football team. Before the season, you draft real NFL players onto your roster. Each week, those players earn fantasy points based on their actual NFL performance (yards, touchdowns, catches, that kind of thing). You set a lineup of starters for each week, and your total points are compared against another team in your league. More points wins.

The standard lineup is one quarterback, two running backs, two wide receivers, one tight end, one flex spot (usually a third running back or receiver), a kicker, and a defense. You also have a bench of backup players who don't score until you put them in your starting lineup.

The season runs parallel to the NFL regular season. Most leagues have playoffs in weeks 15 through 17, with the championship game in week 17. Win your matchup enough weeks during the regular season and you make the playoffs.

## Scoring Basics

Touchdowns are worth 6 points regardless of how they're scored. Yardage is worth less, typically 1 point per 10 rushing or receiving yards, and 1 point per 25 passing yards for quarterbacks. So a receiver who catches 8 passes for 120 yards and a touchdown earns roughly 12 plus 6, or 18 points in a standard league.

The format that changes everything is PPR, which stands for Point Per Reception. In PPR leagues, each catch is worth 1 additional point. This sounds small but it fundamentally shifts which players are valuable. A slot receiver who catches 100 passes for 900 yards isn't as exciting as one who catches 60 for 1,000 yards in a standard league, but in PPR they're roughly equivalent. Most modern leagues use either full PPR or Half PPR (0.5 points per catch). Check your league settings before you do anything else.

## The Draft

The draft is where your season begins. In a snake draft (the most common format), teams take turns picking players. The pick order reverses every round, so if you pick first in round one, you pick last in round two. This is designed to balance the advantage of picking early.

There's real strategy in when to draft which positions. Running backs are the position where I'd tell every beginner to focus early. There are only so many backs who touch the ball 20-plus times a game, and once those players are gone they're gone. Wide receivers are deeper, and you can find solid contributors in rounds four through six. Quarterbacks are the position most beginners draft too early. The difference in fantasy production between the fifth-best quarterback and the fifteenth-best is much smaller than the equivalent gap at running back or tight end. Wait on your quarterback.

Tight end has an unusual structure where there are maybe five genuinely elite options, and then a steep drop to players you'd be comfortable streaming week to week. If one of those elite tight ends falls to you at a reasonable pick, take them. Otherwise, don't reach.

I'd suggest doing three to five mock drafts before your real one. Mock drafts are available on every major platform for free, and they help you understand the pace of how players come off the board. You'll develop a feel for when to take your quarterback, when the receiver runs happen, and where the running back tier breaks occur.

## Managing Your Team Weekly

After the draft, the work shifts to weekly lineup decisions and roster management. The most important thing to check before every game is the injury report, which the NFL releases officially on Fridays and Saturdays. A player listed as Doubtful rarely plays. A player listed as Questionable might play with limited snaps. Set your lineup based on what you know by Sunday morning.

The waiver wire is where a surprising amount of fantasy value gets created. When a starting running back gets injured, the backup becomes instantly relevant. When a receiver suddenly sees 12 targets in a game where he had 5 the week before, that's worth investigating. Checking the waiver wire on Tuesday and Wednesday each week is one of the simplest habits that separates active managers from passive ones.

Trading is the other lever. My honest advice to beginners is to be cautious trading in your first season until you have a feel for player values. It's easy to get fleeced by more experienced managers who know the market better than you. If you're considering a trade, use a trade analyzer tool from FantasyPros or ESPN and make sure the players you're receiving grade out at least as well as the ones you're sending.

## Mistakes That Sink First-Time Players

The most common one is drafting based on team loyalty. This seems obvious when you say it out loud, but it's surprisingly hard to overcome in the moment. Your favorite team's players are not better fantasy players than they are. Draft the best player available, period.

The second is starting a player on their bye week. Every NFL team has one week off during the season. Players on bye weeks score zero points. Check bye weeks every single time you set your lineup, because forgetting costs you a matchup.

The third is overreacting to a single bad week. Good players have bad games. If your running back gets 40 yards against a great defense and you drop him, you'll regret it. Fantasy is a volume game over a long season, and one bad week rarely tells you anything meaningful about a player's value.

The fourth, and this might be the most damaging, is setting your lineup on autopilot after the draft. Fantasy requires weekly attention. Injuries, breakouts, role changes, and matchups all shift over the course of a season. The managers who stay active throughout the year consistently outperform the ones who draft and disappear.

## A Few Things Worth Tracking

Once you have the basics down, target share is the metric I'd point you toward first. Target share is the percentage of a team's passing attempts directed at a specific player. A receiver or tight end who consistently draws 25-plus percent of their team's targets has a stable, valuable role that tends to translate into fantasy production even in down weeks. It's a better predictor of future performance than points scored last week.

For running backs, snap count and goal line role matter most. A back who gets 70 percent of his team's snaps and handles most of the red zone work is a reliable starter. A back in a committee situation, splitting work 60-40 with someone else and losing the touchdown opportunities to a goal line specialist, is much less reliable than his overall yardage might suggest.

None of this is complicated. It just takes a few weeks of paying attention before it starts feeling intuitive. Give yourself permission to not know everything in week one, stay engaged, and the game makes more sense as you go.
