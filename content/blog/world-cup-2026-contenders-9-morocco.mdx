# World Cup 2026 Countdown, No. 9: Morocco and the Weight of 2022

At number nine in my [2026 World Cup countdown](/writing/world-cup-2026-top-ten-contenders) is the team that makes my own ranking system slightly uncomfortable, and I mean that as a compliment to them. Morocco is the only outsider in this field that has actually done the thing the rest of the bubble can only dream about. They reached a World Cup semifinal. That is not a projection or a model output, it happened, in front of all of us, less than four years ago. The question that decides everything about where I place them is whether that run was the start of something durable or a moment in time that is already slipping away.

I landed on ninth, one spot above [Belgium](/writing/world-cup-2026-contenders-10-belgium), because the pedigree and the core are real enough to outrank a fading golden generation, but a chaotic spring and a brutal group draw stopped me from going any higher. This is the widest range of outcomes of any team in my top ten. I can talk myself into a second straight deep run, and I can just as easily see a group-stage exit, and both feel honest.

## What 2022 actually proved

It is worth being precise about what Morocco did in Qatar, because the highlight reel undersells how hard it was. They topped a group that included Croatia and Belgium, then knocked out Spain on penalties and beat Portugal in the quarterfinal, becoming the first African and the first Arab nation ever to reach a World Cup semifinal. That was not a fluke of one hot goalkeeper or a single lucky bounce. It was a defensive structure that frustrated better-resourced teams over six matches and a refusal to be intimidated by bigger names, and it carried over. They ran all the way to the final of the Africa Cup of Nations on home soil this past winter, with Brahim Díaz emerging as a genuine tournament-level creator.

So the pedigree is current, not historical, and that is the single biggest reason I have Morocco in my ten while leaving out outsiders with shinier underlying numbers. When a team has shown you it can win knockout matches against elite opposition, I weight that heavily, because knockout nerve is the hardest thing to model and the easiest thing to underestimate.

## The earthquake in March

Now the part that kept me from ranking them higher. In early March, three months before the tournament, Walid Regragui resigned. This is the manager who built the 2022 run, the architect of the entire defensive identity that made Morocco so hard to beat, and he walked away on the eve of the biggest tournament of these players' lives. You cannot wave that away. Continuity is a real competitive advantage at a World Cup, and Morocco threw theirs out the window at the worst possible moment.

What gives me some confidence is who replaced him. Mohamed Ouahbi stepped up from the under-twenty side, and he did not just hold that job, he won the under-twenty World Cup in 2025, beating Argentina two-nil in the final. That is a serious credential, and it suggests the federation promoted a coach it genuinely rates rather than reaching for a safe veteran name. Still, leading a senior World Cup squad through knockout pressure is a different job than developing youth prospects, and we simply do not know how Ouahbi handles it because nobody does yet. That uncertainty is priced into my ranking.

## A spine you can trust

The reason I think the floor is not as low as the managerial chaos might suggest is the squad itself. Ouahbi named his twenty-six in late May, and nine players from the 2022 semifinal run are back, which means the muscle memory of that campaign is still in the dressing room. Achraf Hakimi captains the side and is the squad's best player and biggest threat, a genuinely world-class full-back who attacks like a winger. Yassine Bounou in goal and Sofyan Amrabat in midfield are the other survivors who define how this team defends.

The newer engine is Brahim Díaz, who has grown into the creative focal point this team lacked in Qatar, when they were brilliant defensively but often blunt going forward. If Brahim is the player he was at the Africa Cup of Nations, Morocco have something they did not have last time, a reliable source of goals from open play. The notable absences are Youssef En-Nesyri and Sofiane Boufal, both heroes of the Qatar run, which tells you Ouahbi is willing to make his own calls rather than simply re-running Regragui's team out of sentiment.

## The group is the catch

Morocco drew into Group C with Brazil, Haiti and Scotland, and that draw is the difference between comfortable and complicated. Brazil is a five-time champion and, despite its own issues, clearly the favorite to win the group. Haiti and Scotland are beatable, so I expect Morocco to advance, most likely in second place, but second place sends them into a tougher round of thirty-two than a group winner would face, with a projected meeting against the Netherlands that would echo their 2022 group stage.

The headline match is obvious. Morocco versus Brazil is the showcase game of Group C, and it is a rematch of a 2023 friendly that Morocco won two-one, which is exactly the kind of result that tells you this team does not flinch against the giants. If they can repeat that and steal top spot, the whole complexion of their tournament changes. I would not bet on it, but I would not be shocked by it either, and that sentence captures Morocco in a nutshell.

## The betting angle

Here is where I think the market is the most interesting on Morocco. They are priced around +5000 to win the tournament, which is longer than Belgium and roughly level with or longer than Colombia, even though Morocco sits above both in the FIFA ranking as the top-rated African side and has far better recent World Cup pedigree than either. The books are clearly docking them for the managerial change and the Brazil draw, and that is defensible, but if you believe the 2022 template survives the coaching upheaval, there is a value argument here that does not exist on the shorter-priced bubble teams. As always I am reading these odds as analysis rather than advice, and they will move, so treat the number as a starting point and not a conclusion.

## Where I land

Morocco at nine is a bet on pedigree over polish. The underlying models are lukewarm because they see a new manager and a hard group, and those are real concerns. But I keep coming back to the fact that this group of players has stood on a World Cup semifinal stage and did not blink, and that almost none of the teams ranked near them can say the same. If Ouahbi steadies the ship and Brahim Díaz gives them the cutting edge they lacked in Qatar, ninth will look conservative. If the coaching change unsettles them and Brazil and the bracket squeeze them out early, it will look generous. I will take that range, and I will take the team that has actually been there before.

That is my number nine. At number eight, the countdown moves from the bubble into the teams I genuinely expect to be playing in the second week, starting with a side whose defense is good enough to carry it deep but that lost the one player it could least afford to lose. [The Netherlands](/writing/world-cup-2026-contenders-8-netherlands) is next.
