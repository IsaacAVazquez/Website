# World Cup 2026 Countdown, No. 8: The Netherlands and the Hole Where Xavi Simons Should Be

At number eight in my [2026 World Cup countdown](/writing/world-cup-2026-top-ten-contenders), the list crosses an important line. From here up, I am no longer ranking teams I merely expect to reach the knockouts. I am ranking teams I expect to still be playing in the second week, and possibly deep into July. The Netherlands belong in that group, but they belong at the bottom of it, and the reason is a single torn knee ligament in April.

If you want the short version, I have the Dutch eighth because the spine of this team, especially the defense, is good enough to carry it into the quarterfinals, and Virgil van Dijk is exactly the kind of player who wins tournaments rather than just qualifying campaigns. What keeps them from climbing higher is that the most creative player in the squad is watching this World Cup from a rehab bike.

## The eternal nearly-men

There is a particular flavor of heartbreak that belongs to Dutch football, and you cannot rank this team without acknowledging it. The Netherlands have reached three World Cup finals, in 1974, 1978 and 2010, and lost all three. They gave the sport the idea of Total Football and a generation of players that other countries would have built a trophy cabinet around, and they have nothing at world level to show for it. The most recent near-miss was the 2022 quarterfinal, a chaotic, bad-tempered loss to Argentina on penalties that Dutch fans will tell you they should have won. This is a program that knows how to get close and has never quite learned how to finish.

I bring that up not as trivia but as context for the ranking. When I weigh the Netherlands, I am weighing a team that reliably reaches the latter stages and reliably runs out of something before the end. Eighth feels like the honest place for a side whose floor is high and whose ceiling has a long history of staying just out of reach.

## A defense you can build a run on

Ronald Koeman named his twenty-six in late May, and the squad is heavily Premier League based, with fifteen of the twenty-six playing their club football in England. That tells you something about where this team's strength lives, because the back line is genuinely one of the best at the tournament. Van Dijk captains it, and even a step past his absolute peak he remains the kind of defender who organizes everyone around him and wins the duels that decide knockout games. Around him there is real quality and depth, with Micky van de Ven's recovery pace and Jurrien Timber's composure giving Koeman options that most teams would envy.

In midfield, Frenkie de Jong is the metronome, the player who sets the rhythm and carries the ball through the lines, and Cody Gakpo gives them a genuine goal threat from the left. On paper that is a serious knockout-football skeleton, a great goalkeeper situation, a great defense, a controlling midfielder and a reliable scorer.

## The injury that defines them

Here is the catch, and it is the whole reason the Netherlands are eighth and not higher. In April, Xavi Simons ruptured his ACL and was ruled out of the tournament. Simons was the team's most creative player, the one who turned that solid structure into something that could actually break a packed defense down in a tight game. Without him, I worry that this Dutch side becomes exactly the thing its critics fear, well organized and hard to beat but short of the spark needed to win a knockout match it does not control. Jeremie Frimpong is also out, which thins the options further down the right.

This is the difference between a top-five contender and a top-eight one. The teams above the Netherlands on my list can lose a key attacker and reach into their squad for another. The Dutch lost the player who made them more than the sum of their defensive parts, and they cannot fully replace what he gave them.

## The path and the betting angle

The draw was kind enough. The Netherlands landed in Group F with Japan, Sweden and Tunisia, and the market makes them favorites to win it at roughly a forty-eight percent chance, with something near an eighty-eight percent chance to advance. I would temper that slightly, because Japan is one of the best non-European sides in the tournament and exactly the kind of quick, technical team that can expose a Dutch side missing its creative hub. The projected round of thirty-two points toward Morocco, which would be a rematch of their 2022 group meeting and a genuinely awkward draw.

In the title market the Netherlands sit around +2000 to +2200, which is the first real long-shot tier and which I think is fair. They are priced as a team that should reach the quarterfinals and would be live, not favored, against the heavyweights on the left side of the bracket, where a France collision lurks. If you are shopping the number, the ESPN +2000 beats the FanDuel +2200, but I am reading these prices as analysis rather than a recommendation, and they will drift as the group stage unfolds.

## Where I land

The Netherlands at eight is a bet on structure with a clear ceiling attached. The defense and Van Dijk are real, and they are enough to make this a team nobody in the bracket wants to draw in a one-off knockout tie. But the Simons injury took away the part of this side that could win an ugly game with a moment of invention, and at a World Cup that is the difference between a quarterfinal and a final. I respect the floor enough to put them in my top eight. I cannot trust the ceiling enough to put them any higher.

That is my number eight. At number seven is a four-time champion that has spent the last two World Cups embarrassing itself in the group stage, but that drew the single easiest path of any contender in this tournament and has the young attacking talent to make that soft draw count. [Germany](/writing/world-cup-2026-contenders-7-germany) is next.
