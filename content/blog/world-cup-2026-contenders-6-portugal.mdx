# World Cup 2026 Countdown, No. 6: Portugal's Midfield Might Be the Best at the Tournament

At number six in my [2026 World Cup countdown](/writing/world-cup-2026-top-ten-contenders) is the team that the headlines will tell you is about one man, and the team that I think is actually about the eight or nine players around him. Cristiano Ronaldo is heading to a record sixth World Cup at forty-one years old, chasing the one major trophy that has always escaped him, and that is a genuinely great story. But if you rank Portugal on Ronaldo, you will rank them wrong. The reason this is a contender, and the reason I have them ahead of a Germany side with an easier draw, is a midfield that may be the single best unit at the tournament.

## Loaded for years, short of the deep run

Portugal's history is a strange one for a country with this much talent. They have never won a World Cup, with their best finishes a third place in 1966 and a fourth in 2006. They did finally break through at major-tournament level by winning Euro 2016 and have added Nations League titles since, so the "they never win anything" line is out of date. But at the World Cup specifically, they have spent two decades arriving with elite squads and leaving without a deep run to show for it. That is the pattern I am weighing. The talent has rarely been the problem. Turning it into seven knockout-level performances in a single month has been.

## The midfield is the reason

Roberto Martínez named his squad on May 19, choosing a symbolic group of twenty-seven plus one, the extra place a tribute to Diogo Jota, the forward who died in a car accident last year. Ronaldo captains the side, and at forty-one his role is now more about presence and penalty-box poaching than carrying the team, which is the right way to use him at this stage.

The strength sits in front of and behind him. Bruno Fernandes arrives as one of the best attacking midfielders in the world after a season that earned him the FWA Player of the Year award, and he is the engine of this team. Behind him, Portugal can field Vitinha and João Neves, the double pivot that has been so effective at Paris Saint-Germain, with Bernardo Silva's intelligence available on top of all of it. That is a midfield with control, creativity and legs in equal measure, and at a World Cup the team that controls the middle of the pitch usually controls the tournament. Up front there is no shortage of options either, with Rafael Leão, Pedro Neto and João Félix all capable of winning a game. This is a deep, balanced, genuinely frightening squad, and it is why the models keep rating Portugal higher than their odds suggest.

## A hard group, and what it tells you

Now the complication. Portugal drew Group K with Colombia, DR Congo and Uzbekistan, and it is the second-toughest group in the tournament, the only one besides France's that contains two top-ten sides. Colombia is a real team with James Rodríguez and Luis Díaz, and the group game between them likely decides who tops the group and who takes the harder route through the bracket.

Here is the part I find most telling. Despite that difficult draw, the Opta supercomputer still rates Portugal as a top-five contender, ahead of teams in much softer groups. When a model looks at a hard group and still ranks you highly, it is paying you a compliment, because it means the underlying squad strength is overcoming the difficulty of the path. That is the strongest argument for taking Portugal seriously, and it is why I have them sixth even though their road is harder than the teams ranked just below them.

## The betting angle

The title market is where Portugal gets genuinely interesting, because the books disagree about them more than almost any other top contender. You can find them as short as +850 at one book and as long as +1000 at another, which is the single biggest price discrepancy among the leading teams. Portugal had been actively shortening in the market, which suggests the longer prices were lagging, so if you are shopping the number, the +1000 is the one to take rather than the +850. In the staged markets they sit around +460 to reach the final. As always, I am reading these prices as analysis rather than a tip, and they move quickly this close to kickoff.

## The case against, and where I land

The honest case against Portugal is age and dependence. This is an older team in several key spots, and the questions about how to balance a forty-one-year-old Ronaldo against younger, more mobile options never fully go away. If Martínez gets that balance wrong, the whole thing can look slow and disjointed, which is how Portugal's previous World Cup campaigns have tended to unravel. The hard group only raises the degree of difficulty.

I went back and forth between Portugal and Brazil for the fifth and sixth spots more than any other call on this list, and I landed on Portugal sixth by the thinnest of margins, mostly because I trust Brazil's ceiling and squad depth a hair more. But this is essentially a coin flip, and on the days when Portugal's midfield is humming, they look like the better team. If Bruno Fernandes and the Vitinha-Neves axis take over a knockout match, sixth will look low.

That is my number six. At number five is the team I just flipped them with, a five-time champion chasing the end of a twenty-four-year title drought, with a new manager, a recalled veteran, and one of the most surprising squad omissions of the entire tournament.
