# Setting Up the Investing Year Without Pretending I Can Forecast It

This is the time of year the market outlooks come out, and they are almost all written in the same confident register, a forecast for where rates go, where the index lands, which sectors lead, delivered as if the author has a real edge on the future. I read a lot of them and I have come to treat the confidence itself as the tell, because the honest truth about the year ahead is that nobody knows, and the people acting like they do are selling certainty rather than offering it. So when I set up my own investing year, I try to do the opposite of forecasting, which is to build something that does not depend on my prediction being right, because my prediction is probably wrong and so is everyone else's. The useful January exercise is not guessing the year, it is looking hard at the structure of what I own and asking whether it can survive being surprised, and this year that question has an obvious focal point.

I want to be clear this is how I think about my own money and not financial advice for yours, because everyone's situation and risk tolerance is different, and the point here is the reasoning, not a recommendation.

## The concentration is the risk most index investors cannot see

The single thing I would actually look at heading into this year is how concentrated the market has become, because a handful of large AI-exposed companies now make up a strikingly large share of the main US index, and that changes what owning it means. A lot of people hold a broad index fund and think of it as diversified, which it was designed to be, but when the top few names are a third of the whole thing and they are all levered to the same AI story, a plain index position is quietly a large bet on that one theme continuing to work. I am not saying that bet is wrong, and the companies driving it are genuinely profitable in a way past bubbles were not, but I think you should at least know you are making it, because diversified and concentrated-in-one-theme are different things wearing the same ticker. The risk that catches index investors off guard is the one they did not know they were holding, and this year that risk has a name.

## Rate uncertainty is a reason not to build around a rate call

The macro backdrop is a Fed that has paused after cutting, holding rates in a middling range with inflation easing but not gone, and the market split on whether the next move is a cut, a hold, or something forced by events, and I think the correct response to that picture is to not build a portfolio that needs a particular rate path to work. It is tempting to position for cuts, to load up on the assets that do well when money gets cheaper, but that is a bet on a forecast, and the last few years should have taught everyone how badly rate forecasts age. I would rather hold a mix that is fine across a range of outcomes than one optimized for the path I happen to expect, because the cost of being wrong on rates is large and the reliability of my rate prediction is low, and that combination argues for robustness over positioning every time.

## The discipline that survives being wrong is rebalancing, not timing

If you accept that you cannot forecast the year, the question becomes what you actually do, and my answer is boring on purpose, which is to rebalance on a schedule rather than trade on a view. Rebalancing, meaning trimming what has grown too large and adding to what has lagged to keep your allocation where you decided it should be, is a discipline that works precisely because it does not require predicting anything, it just requires following the plan you set when you were calm. It also quietly addresses the concentration problem, because in a year where a few names have ballooned, rebalancing is the mechanism that trims that swollen position back toward your target instead of letting it ride to whatever size the market gives it. The investors who get hurt are usually the ones who either freeze or chase, and a rebalancing rule is a way to do neither, to act on a schedule instead of on emotion, and being able to remove the in-the-moment decision is most of the value.

## The whole point of the setup is to make the year require less of me

What ties all of this together is a goal that sounds modest and is actually the entire game, which is to set up the year so that it demands as few good decisions from me as possible. Every point where my returns depend on me making the right call at the right moment is a point where my psychology can cost me, and the history of individual investors is mostly a history of psychology costing them, selling into fear and buying into greed at exactly the wrong times. A setup built on a chosen allocation, an awareness of what I am actually concentrated in, and a rebalancing rule I follow regardless of the headlines is a setup that does not require me to be smart about the future, only disciplined about the plan, and disciplined is a thing I can actually be. Smart about the future is not, and neither is anyone writing the confident January outlook.

The pattern I would pull out of this is that the useful January exercise is not forecasting the year, it is structuring for it, and the two are almost opposite activities. Look honestly at your concentration, because a plain index is a bigger single-theme bet than it looks, do not build around a rate call you cannot actually make, and set up a rebalancing discipline that acts on a schedule instead of on fear. None of that predicts anything, which is the point, because the year is going to surprise all of us, and the only real question is whether what you own can take the surprise. You can [look at how I structure and inspect this](/investments) if it is useful, and then do the thing that fits your own situation, which is the part no outlook can do for you.
