# World Cup 2026 Countdown, No. 4: Argentina and the Last Act of Lionel Messi

Number four in my [2026 World Cup countdown](/writing/world-cup-2026-top-ten-contenders) is the team holding the trophy right now, which should tell you how strong the top of this field is. The defending champions come in fourth, and I want to be clear that ranking Argentina here is not a knock on them so much as an acknowledgment of how good the three teams above them are. This is a side with the softest path of any top seed, the core of a World Cup-winning team still intact, and the best story in the entire sport attached to it. It is also a side whose ceiling and floor both run through the fitness of a thirty-eight-year-old.

## Champions, and the weight of defending it

Argentina has won the World Cup three times, in 1978, 1986 and 2022, and the most recent one was the coronation the country and Lionel Messi had spent a lifetime chasing, sealed on penalties against France in a final that may be the best ever played. They followed it by winning the Copa América in both 2021 and 2024, so this is not a team riding one hot tournament. Under Lionel Scaloni they have been the most consistent national side in the world for half a decade.

Defending a World Cup is its own particular challenge, though, and history is not kind to holders. The squad that won in Qatar is four years older, and four years is a long time at the back end of careers. The question I keep weighing is whether this is still the team that won it all, or a slightly faded version coasting on the same names.

## The spine held, the legend returns

Scaloni answered part of that question with his squad selection. He kept the spine of the Qatar winners together rather than tearing it down, which is the right call for a team whose biggest asset is its chemistry and its belief. He left out Ángel Di María along with several other 2022 champions, and brought in fresh legs like Nico Paz, Valentín Barco and Giuliano Simeone to inject some youth around the veterans.

And then there is Messi. He has confirmed that this will be his sixth and final World Cup, and the entire tournament bends around that fact for Argentina. He will turn thirty-nine during the competition, and he picked up a minor injury in late May that kept him out of a warm-up against Honduras, which the staff handled with obvious caution. When he is on the pitch and healthy, he remains the best player Argentina has and the one most capable of deciding a knockout game with a single moment. The honest concern is how much a player of that age can give across a full month of tournament football in an American summer, and whether the team has a plan B if his body does not cooperate.

## The softest road of any top seed

The draw could hardly have been kinder. Argentina landed in Group J with Algeria, Austria and Jordan, and the models give them roughly a ninety-seven percent chance of advancing, the softest group any of the top seeds received. That matters for a team relying on an aging core, because it means Scaloni can manage minutes, protect Messi, and build into the tournament rather than spending everything in the group stage.

FIFA seeded the bracket so that Argentina and Spain sit in opposite halves and can only meet in the final, which means Argentina's road to the last game avoids the Opta favorite entirely. The catch is the round of thirty-two, where finishing top of the group likely sends them into a meeting with Uruguay, a Río de la Plata derby that is never simple and never friendly. Further on, the bracket dangles the storyline everyone wants, a quarterfinal against Portugal that would be Messi against Ronaldo one last time.

## The betting angle

The market has Argentina around +800 to +900, level with Brazil, with the best price at +900. In the staged markets they sit around +210 to reach the semifinal, which the books see as a strong likelihood given the soft group and friendly half of the draw. I read these numbers as analysis rather than advice, and they will shift with Messi's fitness news more than almost any other factor at the tournament. If there is one player whose health can move a title price on its own, it is him.

## Where I land

Argentina at four is a team I would never want to face and would not be surprised to see lift the trophy again. The ceiling is champion, the path is soft, and the motivation of sending Messi out on top is the kind of intangible that has carried teams further than their talent alone. I have them fourth, behind the three teams above, because the core is aging and because so much depends on a thirty-eight-year-old staying healthy and sharp across seven games. That is a real cap on the floor, even for a defending champion. But if Messi has one more month of magic in those legs, this ranking will look far too low, and I will be glad to have been wrong.

That is my number four. At number three is the team that has spent a decade producing the most talent in its history and the most heartbreak, a side that just qualified without dropping a single point and still cannot quite shake the question of whether it knows how to win.
