# Launching RunningMate by Treating Quality as Product Definition

A lot of product launches treat quality as something that happens at the end. The team builds the feature, hands it off to QA, and waits to see what comes back. That model works when the calendar is generous and the customer is patient. It does not work when you are launching an AI-powered campaign management platform that has to be reliable on day one because the customers are running real campaigns with real deadlines.

I helped launch RunningMate by building the connective tissue between product discovery, acceptance criteria, QA planning, and release operations. The bigger move was reframing what quality even meant inside the work. Quality was not a separate check at the end of the line. It was part of how a feature was defined.

## What Was Actually Hard

The team needed to ship the platform while keeping product feedback, QA planning, and release readiness aligned across functions. Three things kept showing up as friction. Feedback from leadership, users, and client teams needed to become clearer engineering work, instead of vague directional input. Release validation was too manual for the pace the product needed. And quality signals were not visible enough across product, engineering, and client services, which meant launch confidence was uneven depending on who you asked.

A launch with avoidable defects would have weakened customer trust at the moment the platform needed adoption momentum the most. That is the underlying reason the work mattered. Launch quality is not just an internal metric. It is part of the customer's first impression of the product.

## Treating Acceptance Criteria As Product Work

The most useful change was making quality criteria part of product definition rather than a final release gate. When acceptance criteria sit alongside the requirements, they shape what gets built. When they show up at the end, they become a list of reasons not to ship. The first version of that practice felt like overhead. It stopped feeling like overhead the first time a critical defect was caught during refinement instead of during launch week.

I designed structured manual and automated QA coverage across core workflows so the team could trust the validation work without having to repeat it manually each cycle. AI-assisted triage and workflow automation reduced coordination time, which let the team spend their attention on the decisions that actually moved the launch forward.

I also focused automation on repeatable triage and validation work before expanding coverage. That order mattered. Broad shallow automation creates noise, and noise breaks trust faster than missing coverage does. Stable critical-path automation gave the team confidence first, and the rest of the suite grew on top of that.

## What Changed

Critical defects dropped by 90%. NPS moved from 23 to 36. The release cadence shifted from monthly to biweekly. AI-powered workflow automation cut bug triage time by roughly 40%, which compounded across every release after the first one.

The release metrics also became part of how the team talked about delivery risk. Instead of arguing about whether something was "ready," conversations moved toward the actual signals. Defect rate in the last two cycles, NPS movement, triage backlog, and the specific workflows where customers were running into trouble.

## What I Would Bring Forward

Launch quality improves when acceptance criteria are treated as product work, not QA cleanup. That sounds obvious, but it is operationally hard, because it requires product, engineering, and QA to share a definition of done that survives the pressure of a launch window. AI workflow automation is most useful when it removes coordination drag around repeated decisions, not when it tries to replace judgment that the team should be making.

If the team had needed to reconstruct what "ready" meant at the last minute, the launch would have looked very different. The fact that it did not is the thing I am proudest of from this work.
