# My AI Competitive Analysis Workflow, and Why the Agent Never Gets the Last Word

Competitive analysis has always had a dirty secret, which is that almost nobody actually does it. It exists as a quarterly slide in a strategy deck, assembled in a panic the week before the review, stale before it is presented, and never consulted again. I have built those slides. The reason the practice decays is not that PMs think competition does not matter. It is that the manual version has a brutal effort-to-freshness ratio. Hours of tab-hopping across changelogs, pricing pages, job postings, and reviews buys you a snapshot that starts rotting immediately.

This is close to an ideal delegation target for AI agents, and over the past several months I have rebuilt my own practice around them. The result is a standing workflow that costs me roughly two hours a month and produces something I actually use in product decisions. I want to walk through the real setup, because the interesting part is not that AI can summarize competitors. It is where the line sits between what I delegate and what I refuse to.

## The gathering layer is fully delegated

The monthly cycle starts with an agent run that sweeps a defined set of sources for each competitor I track. Changelogs and release notes, pricing pages diffed against last month's capture, new job postings filtered to product and engineering roles, app store review deltas, and anything that hit the trade press. The agent's instructions are deliberately narrow. Collect what changed, cite where it came from, flag anything that did not exist last month. No interpretation, no strategy commentary, just a structured change report per competitor.

Two design choices made this reliable where my first attempt was not. The first is diffing against stored snapshots instead of asking for a fresh summary each time. Fresh summaries drift, and the model will confidently describe a pricing page it half-remembers from training data rather than the one that exists today. Forcing everything through a capture-and-compare structure grounds the run in what is actually on the page. The second is requiring a citation for every claim, which turns hallucinated changes from a silent failure into a checkable one. When a claimed change has no source link, I know exactly what to distrust.

What I found is that this layer alone eliminates most of the cost that killed the manual practice. The tab-hopping hours are gone. What used to make competitive tracking unsustainable is now a background job.

## Synthesis is shared, and judgment is not

The second stage is where most people either over-delegate or under-delegate, so it is worth being precise. I do let the model do real synthesis work. It clusters the month's changes into themes, connects signals across competitors, and drafts hypotheses. Three competitors shipping usage-based pricing in the same quarter is a pattern worth surfacing, and models are genuinely good at surfacing it. A hiring surge in forward-deployed engineering roles at a competitor says something about their go-to-market that a changelog never will, and the agent now flags that connection without being asked.

But hypotheses are where the delegation stops, and I want to argue for why rather than just assert it. Competitive analysis is only valuable when it changes a decision you make, and the link between a competitor's move and your response runs through context the model does not have. Your roadmap capacity, your customers' actual switching costs, which deals you lost last quarter and why, what your sales team is hearing. An agent reading public signals sees the competitor's behavior. It cannot see your exposure to it. When I let a model draft the so-what section early in this experiment, the output was always plausible and always generic, the kind of analysis that reads fine in a deck and changes nothing. The recommendations were unfalsifiable because they were not anchored to any real decision I was facing.

So the last word is mine, and it takes the form of a constraint I have come to like a lot. Every monthly cycle ends with me writing, by hand, a short section answering one question. What, if anything, should we do differently because of what changed this month? Most months the honest answer is nothing, and writing nothing is fine. The discipline of asking is the point. It keeps the document a decision tool rather than a news digest, and news digests are exactly what fully automated competitive intelligence produces.

The whole thing lives in one running document per market rather than a per-quarter deck, which sounds cosmetic and is not. A living document accumulates memory. When a competitor kills the feature they launched eight months ago, the document knows, and that pattern, what competitors abandon, has turned out to be one of the most informative signals in the entire workflow. Decks forget. The agent plus the document remembers.

If you want to start somewhere, start with the gathering layer and a single competitor, and resist the temptation to automate the judgment layer even when the drafts look good. Especially when the drafts look good. The plausible-but-generic failure mode is the dangerous one precisely because nothing about it looks broken. The work the agent saves you is real, two hours a month against what used to be days. Spend a fraction of the savings on the one step that was always the actual job.
