# Premier League 2025-26 in September: The Promoted Clubs Are Breaking the Pattern

When I wrote about the opening weeks, I moved the three promoted clubs from the column where I expected them to be, which was the doomed one, into the column where I said I would actually watch them. A month later I am glad I did, because through September the most interesting thing in the league is not happening at the top, it is happening in the part of the table that is supposed to be a foregone conclusion. Leeds United, Burnley, and Sunderland are all still standing, and Sunderland are doing considerably better than standing.

The context that makes this notable is that the recent record for promoted clubs has been about as bad as it can be. Both of the previous two seasons ended with all three promoted teams going straight back down to the Championship, which is a level of failure that had started to feel structural rather than accidental. The gap between the money and the squads in the two divisions had widened to the point where promotion looked like a one-year visit before an automatic return. So the reasonable prior coming into 2025-26 was that at least two of these three, and probably all three, would be cut adrift by winter. That is not what September looked like.

## Sunderland are the story, and I did not see it coming

Of the three, Sunderland are the one making me rethink my assumptions. They came up through the play-offs, which is usually the least stable route into the league, and the play-off winner is historically the club most likely to struggle. Instead they have been picking up points steadily and sitting comfortably in the upper reaches of the table rather than scrapping at the bottom of it. I want to be careful here, because a good September is not a good season, and the winter is where promoted clubs actually get found out. But the way they have accumulated points is not the way a team that is about to fall apart usually does it. It looks like a plan holding together rather than a run of luck.

Leeds and Burnley have been quieter but functional. Neither of them is flying the way Sunderland are, but neither is being pulled apart either, and for promoted clubs a functional September is genuinely more than the last two seasons managed. What I think this means is that the gap between the divisions, which looked like it had become permanent, is at least a little narrower than the last two years suggested, or these three clubs recruited better for the step up than their predecessors did. Probably some of both.

## One early managerial casualty, which tells you about the league's patience

September also produced one of the season's first managerial changes, which is worth noting less for the individual case and more for what it says about the environment. It came early, only a few games into the season, and it is a reminder that the tolerance for a slow start at this level is close to zero now. Clubs are making decisions on samples that are almost too small to draw conclusions from, which is the opposite of the patience I was just praising the promoted clubs for having with their own plans. I do not think firing a manager three games in is usually a good decision, but I have stopped being surprised by it.

## The top of the table is quietly rearranging

Behind the promoted-club story, the top of the table is starting to move. Liverpool made the early running as I expected the defending champions to, but through September the sense I get is that Arsenal are edging into the picture without much fanfare, which is the same quiet-competence thing I flagged in August. It is too early to call it a title race, and I am not going to, but the shape of one is beginning to form, with Arsenal looking like the team most likely to turn early consistency into a genuine position rather than a fast start that fades.

The pattern I would pull out of September is that the interesting questions this year might not all be at the top. A season where the promoted clubs are competitive changes the character of the whole division, because it means the teams in the bottom third are harder to beat and the points that big clubs used to bank almost automatically have to actually be earned. If that holds up through the winter, it will have been the quiet story of the season. If it does not, and the promoted clubs get dragged back down when the fixtures pile up, then September was the mirage I was warning about in August. I lean toward it being real, at least for one of them.
