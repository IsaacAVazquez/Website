# Most Fantasy Drafts Are Lost to Behavior, Not to Bad Rankings

The way people talk about draft prep, you would think the whole game is having the right rankings, and I used to believe that too. Then I paid attention to my own drafts and the drafts of people who lose the same way every year, and I came around to a different view, which is that the rankings are rarely the problem. You can hand two managers the exact same board and one will build a strong roster and the other will build a weak one, and the difference is almost entirely in how they behaved under pressure across three hours in a room full of other people making decisions. The draft is a social environment, and the mistakes that cost you are the ones the social environment pulls out of you.

What I think this means is that the highest-value prep is not a slightly better projection, it is a process that survives contact with the room, because the room is specifically designed to make you abandon your process.

## Reaching is the tax you pay for certainty you do not have

The most common way I watch managers hurt themselves is reaching, which is taking a player a round or more before his range, because they have decided they love him and cannot bear to risk losing him. The problem is not the player, it is the price, because every pick you spend a round early is a pick you did not spend on the value that was actually going to fall to you. I understand the emotional logic, since watching a guy you targeted get taken right before your pick feels terrible, but that feeling is not a cost you should be optimizing against. The right move is almost always to take the best value in front of you and accept that you will miss some specific players, because the alternative, systematically overpaying to guarantee names, is how you end up with a roster of players you loved and a bench that cannot cover a bye week.

## Positional runs are herd behavior, and the herd is usually wrong on timing

Every draft has the moment where three quarterbacks or three tight ends go in a row and suddenly everyone at the table feels like they have to grab one or be left behind. This is a run, and runs are contagious because scarcity feels urgent, and the urgency is mostly manufactured by the run itself. When I feel the pull to panic into a position because the last four picks were all that position, that is precisely the moment I try to do the opposite and take the value the run is creating everywhere else, because while everyone reaches for the scarce position they are ignoring the players falling past their range at the positions with depth. The run makes the room worse at valuing everything that is not the run, and that is an edge if you can keep your head.

## Anchoring on last year's points is drafting the past

The other quiet killer is anchoring on name brand and prior-season production, drafting a player for what he did last year rather than for what his role and situation say about this year. Last season's points are the single most available number in everyone's memory, which is exactly why the market overweights them, and it is why the player coming off a huge year in an offense that has since changed keeps going a round or two too high. I try to draft the situation, meaning the role, the offense, and the opportunity, and to treat last year's box score as one input rather than the anchor the whole room is tethered to. The players who beat their draft price are usually the ones whose situation improved in a way that last year's numbers cannot show you.

## Not having a roster plan is a decision you make by accident

The last mistake is subtler, which is drafting without a plan for how the roster fits together, so that your build, whether it ends up running-back-heavy or receiver-heavy or balanced, is an accident of which picks felt good in the moment rather than a shape you chose. I do not think any single construction is correct, and I am not here to sell you zero-RB or hero-RB as gospel. I am saying that whatever you do should be a plan you decided on before the pressure started, because a plan gives you a reason to pass on a tempting pick that does not fit, and without one you will take the tempting pick every time and discover in Week 3 that your team has four of one thing and none of another.

The pattern I would pull out of this is that a draft is a sequence of decisions made under social pressure, and the managers who win are not the ones with marginally better rankings, they are the ones whose process holds when the room tries to knock it loose. Prep your board, sure, but spend at least as much energy deciding in advance how you will behave when someone reaches, when a run starts, and when the player you loved is gone. The [board I draft from](/fantasy-football) is built to make those moments calmer, and calm is most of the edge.
