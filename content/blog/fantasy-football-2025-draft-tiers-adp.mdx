# Draft in Tiers, Not Rankings, and Watch the Cliffs Instead of the Names

The standard way fantasy rankings get published is a list from one to two hundred, and I think that format quietly lies to you every single year. It implies that the difference between the sixth-ranked player and the seventh is the same size as the difference between the sixth and the twelfth, and that is almost never true. Real player value comes in clumps, with groups of roughly interchangeable players separated by gaps where the quality drops off, and a flat numbered list flattens exactly the information you most need on draft day. What I actually want to know when I am on the clock is not who is ranked highest among the players left. It is which group of players I am about to lose access to if I wait another round.

That reframing, from names to groups, is the whole idea behind drafting in tiers, and once you internalize it the decisions get easier and calmer, because you stop agonizing over choices that do not matter and start paying attention to the ones that do.

## The gap between two players matters more than their order

If the last two backs in a tier are close enough that I would be fine with either, then their exact ranking is noise, and picking between them is a coin flip dressed up as analysis. The decision that carries weight is whether a real gap sits just below them. When I am looking at a tier with three players left and my pick plus the picks before my next turn are going to clear all three out, that is the moment the tier is telling me something, because if I wait I am not getting a slightly worse version of the same player, I am dropping to the next group down, and the next group is worse in a way that shows up in the standings. Ordering within a tier is where people spend their agonizing, and the cliff at the bottom of the tier is where the actual decision lives.

## Where 2025 ADP is systematically wrong

Average draft position is useful as a map of where the room is going, but it is a consensus, and consensus misprices the same spots almost every year. The clearest one is the middle-round running back stretch, the range where you are paying a premium for backs who are one injury or one committee change away from irrelevance, and where the market keeps reaching because everyone is scared of missing the position. I would rather let that run happen around me and take the receivers the room is passing over to chase those backs. The other recurring mispricing is at the top of the tight end and quarterback positions, where a genuinely scarce elite option is worth reaching for by a round while the replacement-level version a few rounds later is nearly free, and the market tends to either overpay across the board or ignore the scarcity entirely. The rookie hype tax is the third one, where a rookie with an exciting profile goes a full round or two ahead of where the range of outcomes justifies, because upside is easier to sell than a boring floor.

## How I build the tiers in the first place

The honest version of tiering is not vibes, it is looking at projected points relative to the replacement level at each position and finding the natural breaks. When I sort a position by expected value and look for the spots where the drop between consecutive players gets large, those breaks are the tiers, and they are more stable than the exact projections that produced them. I trust the shape of the curve more than any single number on it, because the number is a point estimate with a wide error band and the shape, the pattern of clumps and gaps, survives being slightly wrong about any individual player. That is why I can be uncertain about whether a specific back finishes as the RB14 or the RB19 and still be confident about which tier he belongs in, and the tier is what I am drafting.

## The discipline is taking the last player in a tier and moving on

The behavior that tiers are supposed to produce is simple to state and hard to do in a live draft, which is to take the last acceptable player in a tier before it empties, and then feel fine about it. When a tier has one player left and the tier below is a real step down, that last player is worth more to me than a higher-ranked name in a tier that still has depth, because I can get the deeper tier later and I cannot get this one. Most of the value I have ever captured in a draft came from this one move, waiting on a position where the tier stayed deep and pouncing on a position right before its tier fell off, and it only works if you are watching the cliffs instead of the names.

The pattern I would pull out of this is that a good draft is not a sequence of getting the best available player, it is a sequence of avoiding cliffs, and the two are not the same thing. Rankings tell you who is best, and tiers tell you when it is about to get worse, and the second question is the one that actually decides your roster. The [rankings I use](/fantasy-football) are grouped with the cliffs marked for exactly this reason, because the number next to a name was never the point.
