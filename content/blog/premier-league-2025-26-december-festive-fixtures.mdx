# Premier League 2025-26 in December: The Festive Pile-Up Is Where Seasons Get Decided

Every year I try to remind myself that the December fixtures matter more than their individual scorelines suggest, because the festive pile-up is the one stretch of the season that tests something the rest of the calendar does not. In a normal week a club plays once and can put its best eleven out rested. In the two weeks around the holidays it plays three times in barely more than a week, and suddenly the question is not how good your starting team is, it is how good your fourth center-back and your third striker are. December is where the teams that have a squad pull away from the teams that have a starting eleven and a lot of hope.

Arsenal came into the month leading, and the thing I wanted to know was whether the lead I said I trusted in November could survive the congestion. It did. They held their position through the festive fixtures, which is a different and harder achievement than holding it in a normal month, because it means the structure that had been keeping clean sheets did not fall apart the moment it had to be assembled from a rotated team. A defensive identity that survives December is a defensive identity that has been stress-tested, and I came out of the month more confident in Arsenal, not less. When I checked [the dashboard](/premier-league) over the holidays, the top of the table looked essentially the way it had at the start of the month, and after three games in nine days that stability is the story.

## The promoted clubs reached the moment they usually break

I have spent two of these check-ins on the promoted clubs, and I said each time that the real test was not the autumn but the winter, because the last two seasons the promoted teams did not fall apart in September, they fell apart when the fixtures piled up and the thin squads ran out of bodies. December was that test arriving. This is the month I had circled as the one that would tell me whether the competitiveness I was praising in the fall was real or whether it was the same mirage that had fooled people the previous two years.

What I saw was mixed in the way I probably should have expected, and mostly encouraging. Sunderland kept doing the thing that has made them the story of the season for me, holding a position in the table that nobody predicted for a play-off promotion side, and holding it through the exact stretch that was supposed to expose them. The other two had a harder time of it, which is what the congestion does to shallow squads, and December was the month where the gap between "competitive promoted club" and "promoted club about to get dragged into a fight" started to open up. I am not calling anyone down yet. But December is usually where the winter math starts, and it started here.

## The halfway table is the first one I mostly believe

By the end of December the season is roughly at its midpoint, and the halfway table is the first standings of the year that I take mostly at face value. The sample is finally big enough, the fixtures have evened out enough, and the congestion has revealed enough about squad depth that the table is now describing the teams rather than the schedule. What it described at the turn of the year was Arsenal in front on the strength of a defense that had survived every test I could think of, Manchester City sitting in the range they always sit in before they make their move, and Liverpool's title defense having slipped far enough that the conversation about them had changed from whether they could win it to whether they could stay in the top few.

The pattern I would pull out of December is that the congestion confirmed rather than upset the picture, which does not always happen and is itself a kind of information. A festive period that leaves the table looking the way it did going in is telling you the leaders are leaders for real reasons, because the month that usually shuffles the deck did not. Arsenal go into the new year in front and stress-tested, the promoted clubs are entering the phase where the ones with the shallowest squads start to feel the strain, and the second half of the season is going to be about whether anyone can force Arsenal into the scoring drought I keep saying is their one real vulnerability. The transfer window opens in a few days, and January tends to be the month where the neat story a season has been telling itself gets complicated. I will check back in when it does.
