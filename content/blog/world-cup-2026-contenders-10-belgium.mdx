# World Cup 2026 Countdown, No. 10: Belgium's Golden Generation Gets One Last Shot

This is where my countdown of the [2026 World Cup contenders](/writing/world-cup-2026-top-ten-contenders) begins, at number ten, and I want to be honest that Belgium was not an easy pick for this spot. There are teams with higher ceilings sitting just outside my ten, Norway chief among them. What got Belgium over the line is the least glamorous quality in tournament football and one of the most underrated. They have a floor.

If you want the short version, I have Belgium tenth because they are the safest bet of any outsider to actually reach the knockout rounds, they still have enough individual quality to win a one-off match against almost anyone, and they are carrying a story that I think pulls a few extra percentage points out of this squad. This is the last realistic World Cup for the best generation of players the country has ever produced, and players in that situation tend to leave something on the field.

## A generation that has run out of next times

To understand why Belgium tenth feels right, you have to remember how good this group was and how little it has to show for it. Belgium reached the semifinals of the 2018 World Cup and finished third, which remains the high-water mark for the national team, and for a stretch around that tournament they sat at the very top of the FIFA ranking. That is the version of Belgium that people remember, all of that midfield creativity and a front line that could beat you four different ways.

Then came 2022, and the fall was hard. A flat, aging Belgium went out in the group stage in Qatar without ever looking like the team that had thrilled four years earlier. The lesson I took from that tournament is that this core had probably peaked, and nothing since has really changed my mind. So when I rank them now, I am not ranking the 2018 team. I am ranking what is left of it, which is still considerable, plus a clear-eyed understanding that the window is basically shut after this.

## The squad still has match-winners

Rudi Garcia, the French coach now in charge, named his twenty-six in mid-May and chose to keep faith with the veterans rather than tear the thing down. I understand the instinct. Thibaut Courtois remains one of the two or three best goalkeepers on the planet, and a great goalkeeper is the single most valuable thing to have in knockout football, where one save can carry you through a tie you did not deserve to win. Kevin De Bruyne, now in Serie A with Napoli, is no longer the relentless force he was at his Manchester City peak, but he is still the most intelligent passer in this squad and the player most likely to unlock a stubborn defense in a tight game.

Around them, Garcia has Jérémy Doku, who is genuinely frightening in space and exactly the kind of player who can win a knockout match on his own with one run. Leandro Trossard gives them a clever, low-maintenance option in the final third. The gamble is Romelu Lukaku, included despite an injury-hit season, and I understand why, because his World Cup scoring record is strong and Belgium do not have an obvious replacement for what he offers when fit. The squad is older than I would like and thinner than it was, but the top end of it is still good enough that I would not want to face them with a final-third lead to protect.

## The draw is why they made my ten

Here is the real argument for Belgium at ten, and it is about the bracket more than the badge. They landed in Group G with Egypt, Iran and New Zealand, which is about as gentle a draw as a team of this caliber could have asked for. The betting market has Belgium as favorites to win the group and gives them somewhere around an eighty-nine percent chance of advancing, and I think that is roughly right. Compare that to the outsiders I left out. Norway drew into the Group of Death and might not survive three games. Belgium are all but booked into the knockout rounds before the tournament starts.

That matters because of what knockout football actually is. Once you are in the round of thirty-two, the format compresses everything into single games where a goalkeeper, a moment of quality, and a bit of luck can take a flawed team a long way. I do not think Belgium have the depth or the legs to grind through four knockout rounds and win this tournament. But I think they will be there in the knockouts, with Courtois behind them and De Bruyne in front, and that is more than most of the teams ranked just outside my ten can say with any confidence. A safe floor plus a few elite players is a real contender profile, even if it is an unfashionable one.

## Where the betting market sits

The market has Belgium around +3500 to +4000 to win the tournament, which is the shortest price of any team on the bubble, shorter than Morocco and roughly level with Colombia depending on the book. I think that is a fair reflection of exactly what I have described. The books are not pricing Belgium to win it, they are pricing the combination of a near-certain run to the knockouts and enough quality to be live in a tie or two after that. As always, I am treating these numbers as analysis rather than a tip, and they will drift as squads firm up and the public picks its sides, so check a current board before you read too much into any single figure.

If there is value anywhere in the Belgium markets, I suspect it is not on the outright, where the price is honest, but on the smaller stages. A goalkeeper this good in a one-off knockout game is the kind of edge that outright odds do not fully capture.

## The case against, and where I land

The honest case against Belgium at ten is that this is a sentimental ranking dressed up as an analytical one, and that the higher-ceiling teams I left out could blow straight past them in a head-to-head. I take that point. If Norway click and survive their group, they are a more dangerous team than this Belgium side. But a countdown of contenders is a ranking of likely outcomes, not best-case ones, and on likely outcomes Belgium clears the bar that the boom-or-bust outsiders do not. They will be in the knockouts. They have players who can win a knockout game. And they are playing the last act of a story that has gone unfinished for too long.

That is my number ten. Next up at number nine is a team with the opposite profile, an outsider that has actually done the thing Belgium never quite managed, reaching a World Cup semifinal, but that walks into this tournament with a brand-new manager and a five-time champion waiting in its group.
