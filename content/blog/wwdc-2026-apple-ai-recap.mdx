# Apple's New Siri Is Impressive, and It Runs on Google's Model

Most of the WWDC coverage I read framed this keynote as the moment Apple finally caught up on AI. The new Siri looks and behaves like a real assistant now, there's a next generation of Apple Intelligence woven across the system, and iOS 27 got a design refresh on top of it. Taken at face value, it reads like a comeback. I watched the same presentation and came away reading it as a concession, because the single most important detail is that the impressive new Siri runs on Google's Gemini under the hood. That one fact reframes everything around it. Apple didn't catch up at the model layer this year so much as it rented its way past the gap, and I think that's the thing actually worth arguing about, more than any individual feature that got demoed on stage.

## The new Siri is genuinely better, and that's not the interesting part

I want to give the product its due, because the improvement is real and I'm not being grudging about it. The new Siri is far more capable and conversational than the thing it replaces, it carries system-wide personal context so it can reason about your own information across apps, and it has on-screen awareness so it can act on whatever you're actually looking at. Apple gave it a standalone app in addition to threading it through the system, and the assistant now reaches into Messages, the Phone app, and Safari in ways that look genuinely useful rather than bolted on, down to details like tab management and faster one-tap actions. For a company whose assistant had been a running joke for years, this is a substantial reset, and if you only care about whether your iPhone got better, it did. But the quality of the demo is exactly why I don't think the demo is the story. Apple has always been able to ship a polished experience on top of a capability. The open question all year was never whether Apple could build a good interface. It was whether Apple had a competitive model to put behind it, and this keynote answered that question in a way the staging tried to soften.

## The detail that matters is that it runs on Gemini

The answer is that Apple's competitive model is Google's. The new Siri's intelligence comes from Gemini running underneath, and once you know that, the year makes a coherent story. For the last several quarters Apple looked visibly behind at the frontier, shipping careful, narrow AI features while everyone else shipped models, and the read a lot of us had was that Apple simply didn't have something at the frontier to ship. This deal is the confirmation. You don't route your flagship assistant to a rival's model if your own is close enough to use, especially not a rival you compete with directly in phones and search and everything else. So I take the Gemini arrangement as Apple conceding, in the most consequential possible way, that the model layer got away from it, and choosing to solve the product problem by buying the capability rather than by waiting to build it. That's a defensible decision. It's also an admission, and I think the coverage that treats it purely as a triumph is skipping past what it cost Apple to make the triumph possible.

## Renting the model can be smart platform strategy or slow surrender

The honest version of this is that leaning on Gemini could turn out to be either a smart platform move or a slow surrender of the layer that matters, and I don't think it's obvious yet which one it is. The case for it being smart is genuinely strong. Apple's advantages were never the model. They're distribution to a billion-plus devices, the tight integration between silicon and software, the privacy framing that lets it process a lot on-device, and control of the surfaces where people actually do things. If frontier models are becoming an interchangeable commodity input, then the rational move is to buy the best one, wrap it in the things only Apple can do, and not burn years and enormous cost trying to win a race that doesn't confer much advantage even if you win it. In that reading, Apple is doing exactly what a platform owner should, keeping the parts it's best at and renting the part that's becoming a utility. The case against it is that whoever owns the model tends to own the roadmap and a growing share of the margin, and Apple just handed one of its sharpest competitors a seat inside the most personal software on the iPhone. If models don't commoditize, if the frontier keeps mattering and keeps pulling away, then Apple has made itself dependent on Google for the capability that increasingly defines the product, and dependency like that is expensive to unwind later and gives the other side leverage in every future negotiation. Both of these can be true at once for a while, which is why I'd hold off on calling it.

The pattern I'd pull out of this is that Apple is placing a specific bet, whether or not it framed it that way on stage, that the model layer is a commodity it can safely rent while it keeps the layers it's best at. That's a coherent bet and it might well be the right one, because a lot of the value in these products really does live in distribution and integration rather than in the raw model. But it only holds if models actually commoditize, and that's the part nobody knows yet. If they do, Apple looks smart for refusing to overpay for a race that didn't matter. If they don't, Apple just outsourced the most important part of the stack to a rival and called it a comeback. This keynote validated the read that Apple was behind at the model layer all year. What it didn't settle is whether being behind there is going to keep mattering, and that's the question I'd actually keep watching.
