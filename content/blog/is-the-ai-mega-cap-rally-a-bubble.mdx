# The AI Rally Is Expensive, but the Bubble Everyone Fears Is in the Wrong Place

Every few weeks someone asks me whether the AI trade is a bubble, and I have come to think the question is built wrong. It assumes there is one thing called "the AI trade" and one binary answer, when what is actually happening is at least three different stories stacked on top of each other that happen to share the same tickers. There is the question of whether the biggest companies in the market are too expensive. There is the separate question of whether the index has become dangerously dependent on a handful of them. And there is the question I think actually matters most, which is whether the enormous amount of money being spent to build AI infrastructure is going to earn a return. Those three questions have three different answers, and lumping them into "is it a bubble" gets you a worse read than answering them one at a time.

So I want to take them one at a time, with the numbers as they stand in the middle of 2026, and land somewhere honest rather than somewhere dramatic. My short version, before I show the work, is that the market's largest companies are expensive but mostly defensible, the index's concentration is genuinely worrying in a way that has little to do with valuation, and the real fragility is in the build-out economics rather than in anyone's price-to-earnings ratio. The bubble talk is pointed at the stocks. I think the strain is somewhere else.

One caveat before the numbers. The figures below are the best cross-checked readings I could assemble in mid-2026, and many of them, especially the daily valuation multiples and the fast-moving private-company revenue run-rates, drift constantly. I have tried to use broad ranges rather than false decimals, because the argument does not depend on whether Nvidia's forward multiple is 21 or 23, and anyone quoting these to two decimal places is pretending to a precision the data does not have.

## The concentration is real, and it is larger than 2000

Start with the thing that is not really arguable. The market has become extraordinarily concentrated. The group people call the Magnificent Seven, which is Nvidia, Microsoft, Apple, Alphabet, Amazon, Meta, and Tesla, now makes up somewhere around 35 percent of the entire S&P 500 by market value. A decade ago that same group was closer to 12 or 13 percent. The top ten names in the index reached roughly 40 percent of its total weight at the end of 2025, which is a record, and well above the rough 25 to 27 percent the top ten represented at the dot-com peak in early 2000. On the narrow measure that gets the most attention, today's market is more top-heavy than it was at the height of the internet mania, not less.

Nvidia is the single clearest expression of this. It crossed three trillion dollars in market value in June 2024, became the first company to touch four trillion intraday in July 2025, and then the first to close above five trillion at the end of October 2025. At various points in 2026 it has been worth more than seven percent of the entire index by itself, which means one company carries more weight than whole sectors do. When a single stock is that large, the index stops being the diversification people assume they are buying. Torsten Slok at Apollo put it bluntly last year when he argued the top ten companies in the S&P 500 were more overvalued than the top ten were in the 1990s, and that the index is no longer really a diversified index at all. I think that is the right thing to be nervous about, and notice that it is a statement about structure, not about whether any individual company is mispriced.

The return math makes the dependence concrete. In 2025 the S&P 500 returned somewhere around 18 percent including dividends, and roughly 42 percent of that came from the Magnificent Seven. The cap-weighted index has pulled so far ahead of its own equal-weighted version that the gap set records, with 2023 and 2024 ranking as the two worst years for the equal-weight version relative to the standard one since it launched in 2003. What that tells you is that the typical stock has been having a much more ordinary few years than the headline index suggests. The index looks like it is booming because seven companies are booming and the scoring system weights them by size.

Here is the part that complicates the simple bubble story, though. Those top ten companies are about 40 percent of the index's market value but only around 32 percent of its earnings. That is a real premium, but it is a gap of eight points, not the kind of chasm you would expect if this were pure mania. The concentration is extreme and the disconnect between price and profit at the top is meaningful but not absurd. Both of those things are true at once, which is exactly why the one-word answer fails.

## The leaders are expensive, but they are not dot-com expensive

This is where I part company with the loudest version of the bubble argument. On the measures that look at the whole market, valuation is genuinely stretched. The S&P 500's forward price-to-earnings ratio has been sitting in the low 20s, above its five-year and ten-year averages of roughly 20 and 19. The cyclically adjusted CAPE ratio has been hovering near 40, which is the second-highest reading in the history of the series, behind only the 44 it hit in December 1999 and above the roughly 32 it reached before the 1929 crash. The Buffett indicator, total US market value against GDP, has been running around 230 percent, close to a record and something like two standard deviations above its long-term trend. If you want to argue the broad market is priced for disappointment, those numbers are your evidence and they are strong.

But the specific claim that gets made, that the AI leaders are trading like the dot-com leaders did, does not survive contact with the actual multiples. At the 2000 peak, the seven largest stocks traded at something like 66 times forward earnings as a group. The Nasdaq's forward multiple was around 60. Cisco, the Nvidia of that era, carried a price-to-earnings ratio that depending on which earnings base you use ran from roughly 130 to 200, and the stock did not retake its 2000 high until December 2025, a full twenty-five years later. The Nifty Fifty in 1972, the other favorite analogy, averaged around 42 times earnings with the most expensive names north of 90.

Now look at where the AI leaders actually trade in 2026. As a group the Magnificent Seven have been around 28 to 31 times forward earnings, which is expensive against the market but less than half the multiple their predecessors carried in 1999. The individual numbers are more surprising than the group average. Nvidia, the stock at the center of all of it, has a forward price-to-earnings ratio in the low 20s, because its earnings have grown nearly as fast as its price. Meta has been the cheapest of the group at roughly 18 times forward earnings. Microsoft sits around 21 or 22, Alphabet in the high 20s, Amazon around 30, and Apple is the richest of the seven near 32 with the weakest growth to justify it. The valuations that actually rhyme with the dot-com extremes belong to names like Tesla near 180 times, Arm above 120, and Intel above 100, and those are not the companies carrying the AI infrastructure story. The richness in Nvidia shows up not through its earnings multiple but through price-to-sales near 20 and enterprise-value-to-EBITDA around 30, which is a different and more defensible kind of expensive when the revenue is compounding at the rate it has been.

So when someone tells me the AI mega-caps are 2000 all over again, my honest response is that the market as a whole is priced about as richly as it has ever been, but the leaders specifically are nowhere near the multiples that defined that peak. Those are two different claims, and the bears tend to borrow the credibility of the first to make the second, which the second has not earned.

## Where the actual bubble lives

If the strain is not in the leaders' earnings multiples, where is it? I think it is in the spending, and in the financing wrapped around the spending, and this is the part of the picture that genuinely worries me.

The scale of the build-out is hard to hold in your head. The four biggest spenders, which are Microsoft, Alphabet, Amazon, and Meta, put somewhere between 340 and 390 billion dollars into capital expenditure in 2025, up around 60 percent year over year, and their 2026 guidance points to well over 600 billion combined, with some estimates running toward 700 or 800 billion once you include Oracle and count generously. Goldman Sachs has the industry investing more than 500 billion in 2026 and has lifted its cumulative AI-capex outlook to something like 7.6 trillion dollars through 2031. McKinsey models more than five trillion of AI-specific data-center spending by 2030. These are numbers that used to describe national infrastructure programs, and they are being committed by a handful of companies on a few years' timeline.

The problem is not the spending by itself. The problem is the gap between the spending and the revenue it has produced so far. Sequoia framed this two years ago as the "600 billion dollar question," the difference between what the industry was investing in AI compute and the end-user revenue that would have to exist to justify it, and the gap has only widened since. Bain estimated that by 2030 the industry would need roughly two trillion dollars a year in new AI revenue to fund the compute it is planning, and that even crediting every plausible efficiency saving, the world comes up around 800 billion a year short. An MIT study last year found that 95 percent of enterprise generative-AI pilots showed no measurable impact on the profit-and-loss statement. The study has been argued about, and it should be, but the direction it points is consistent with everything else. The money going into the build is running far ahead of the money coming out of the applications.

Some of the financing has started to look circular in a way that should make anyone who lived through the late 1990s uneasy. Nvidia announced a commitment of up to 100 billion dollars to OpenAI, which is among the largest buyers of Nvidia chips, and while that figure has in practice shrunk toward a roughly 30 billion dollar equity stake, the shape of it, a chip supplier helping fund its own customer's purchases, is exactly the pattern that turned the telecom build-out of 1999 into the wreckage of 2001. OpenAI has reportedly committed on the order of 1.4 trillion dollars across compute deals with Nvidia, AMD, Broadcom, Oracle, Microsoft, and others, against an annualized revenue run-rate that was only around 20 billion exiting 2025. Jim Chanos, who shorted Enron, has called the debt piling up in the GPU-backed "neocloud" layer a rerun of Lucent, and the leverage at a company like CoreWeave, with debt many times its equity and most of its revenue coming from a single customer, is the kind of thing that does not matter at all until the moment it matters enormously.

Then there is the accounting question that Michael Burry raised at the end of 2025, which I find more persuasive than I expected to. The hyperscalers have been extending the assumed useful life of their servers and chips, with Meta moving to five and a half years and others reportedly to six. Stretching the depreciation schedule lowers the annual expense and raises reported profit, and Burry's argument is that GPUs in heavy AI workloads wear out economically in something closer to two or three years, which would mean current profits are being flattered by understated depreciation. Nvidia pushed back, pointing out that older chips like the A100 from 2020 still run at high utilization and hold value, and the truth is probably somewhere in between and genuinely unknown. But the fact that the question is live, that a non-trivial slice of reported hyperscaler profit may depend on an aggressive assumption about how long this hardware lasts, is itself a sign of how much weight the build-out is being asked to bear.

Underneath all of it sits a physical constraint that money cannot quickly fix, which is power. The buildout has moved from being capital-constrained to being energy-constrained. The International Energy Agency expects data-center electricity demand to roughly double by 2030 to somewhere near 945 terawatt-hours, with US data centers potentially consuming 7 to 12 percent of the country's electricity by 2028. Transformer and switchgear lead times now run years, interconnection queues stretch longer than that, and a meaningful share of the data-center capacity that has been announced for 2026 may simply slip because there is nowhere to plug it in. You can commit the capital in a quarter. You cannot energize the megawatts on the same schedule.

## Why this still might be fine, and why it might not

I want to be fair to the bull case, because it is stronger than the bears usually admit. The defining feature of 2000 was that the leaders did not make money. The internet stocks Mary Meeker tracked in 1999 carried something like 450 billion dollars of market value on around 21 billion in sales and were collectively losing money. That is not remotely the situation today. Nvidia did about 216 billion dollars in revenue in its fiscal 2026, up 65 percent, at gross margins above 70 percent and a net margin around 56 percent, which is a level of profitability that is almost difficult to believe for a company that sells physical hardware. The Magnificent Seven as a group run net margins around 25 percent, roughly double the rest of the market, and they sit on something like 600 billion dollars of cash. The revenue underneath the AI story is also real and growing fast, with Microsoft's AI business passing a 37 billion dollar annualized run-rate, OpenAI moving from around 13 billion in 2025 revenue toward a 20-billion-plus run-rate, and Anthropic's run-rate climbing through the year at a pace that would have sounded fictional eighteen months ago. When bulls say this is not 2000, the profitability of the leaders is their best evidence, and it is good evidence.

But I do not think the strongest bear points are about the leaders being unprofitable. They are about three things that are all true at the same time. The first is that the group's earnings growth is dangerously dependent on a single name. Strip Nvidia out of the Magnificent Seven and the remaining six were on track to grow first-quarter 2026 earnings at something closer to 6 percent rather than the 23 percent the group posted together. The headline that says Magnificent Seven fundamentals are great increasingly means Nvidia's fundamentals are great and the others are fine, which is a more fragile foundation than the aggregate suggests. The second is that the free cash flow story has reversed. The big four generated less combined free cash flow in 2025 than in 2024, because capex is now eating it, and Meta went free-cash-flow negative for stretches of the year. The comforting line that this build-out is self-funded from rising cash flow is no longer quite true, which is why these companies have started issuing debt to pay for it. The third is that the market has already shown twice how fast this can unwind, once in January 2025 when the DeepSeek efficiency scare erased something like 600 billion dollars of Nvidia's value in a single day, and again in November 2025 when the AI complex sold off hard on exactly these capex-versus-revenue worries, with Nvidia falling roughly 19 percent from its October high.

So the institutions lining up to warn about this are not cranks. The IMF's Kristalina Georgieva said valuations were heading toward levels last seen during the internet bubble. The Bank of England flagged a rising risk of a sharp correction. The Bank for International Settlements noted in early 2026 that broad-market multiples had approached dot-com levels. Jeremy Grantham has called AI potentially the greatest capital-investment bubble of all time. Ray Dalio's framing is the one I find most useful, which is that the conditions of a bubble are visibly present but a bubble does not pop until holders are forced to sell, usually when financial conditions tighten, so the timing is about liquidity rather than valuation. None of these people are predicting the leaders go to zero. They are describing a market that has priced in a great deal of future success and left itself very little room for the build-out to disappoint.

## What I would actually watch

If I had to compress all of this into a position, it would be that the index is expensive, the leaders are expensive but defensible, and the genuine fragility is the distance between AI capital spending and AI revenue, amplified by how much of the index now rides on a few companies and how much of the financing has started to feed on itself. That is not "it is a bubble" and it is not "everything is fine." It is closer to saying the most likely accident is a sharp repricing of the AI build-out, on the order of the 20 to 30 percent drawdown that the centrists model, rather than a 78 percent Nasdaq-style collapse, because the leaders' profits put a floor under them that the 2000 leaders never had. I would not rule out the worse outcome. I would just not treat it as the base case.

The single variable I keep coming back to is the relationship between capex and the revenue attached to it, and specifically whether that gap is narrowing or widening. I wrote a whole piece on reading earnings through that lens, because I think the slope of those two curves tells you more than any valuation snapshot can. A world where capex keeps accelerating and attached revenue accelerates with it is a healthy, expensive market. A world where capex keeps accelerating while attached revenue flattens is the setup for the repricing, and the warning will come not from the price-to-earnings ratio but from a hyperscaler trimming its capex guidance, or an efficiency breakthrough that makes the planned compute look excessive, or a stumble somewhere in the debt-financed neocloud layer that reminds everyone how much leverage got added on the way up.

I am not going to make calls on individual names, and none of this is advice about what anyone should buy or sell. What I will say is that the useful question is not whether the AI rally is a bubble. It is which part of it is fragile and what would have to happen for that fragility to matter. The valuations of the leaders are not the fragile part. The concentration is fragile, the financing is fragile, and above all the bet that hundreds of billions a year of spending turns into commensurate revenue is fragile. Watch that gap rather than the multiple, and the next year gets a lot more readable than the bubble-or-not framing makes it seem.
