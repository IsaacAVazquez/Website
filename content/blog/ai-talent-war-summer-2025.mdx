# The AI Talent War Is Pricing the Thing People Said Was Commoditized

The story everyone told about this summer's hiring spree was about ego and money. Mark Zuckerberg is writing enormous checks, Sam Altman is wounded that his people are leaving, and the whole thing reads as billionaires measuring themselves against each other with other people's careers. That framing is not wrong exactly, and there is real ego in it, but I think it misses what the compensation war is actually revealing. When you strip out the personalities, the offers are a pricing signal, and what they are pricing is scarcity. For two years the common claim was that the models were becoming a commodity and that the real moats would be data, distribution, and product. The talent war is the market saying, in the bluntest language it has, that the scarce inputs are a few hundred people and a very large amount of compute, and that the model itself is downstream of both.

## The size of the offers is the actual data point

It is easy to get numb to the numbers, so it is worth sitting with how strange they are. Meta reportedly landed Apple's foundation models lead with a package worth more than $200 million. Altman has said publicly that his researchers were being offered signing bonuses of up to $100 million. The most extreme figure I have seen reported is a package worth as much as $1.5 billion, spread over six years, dangled at a single researcher who turned it down. I want to be careful with that last one because it is the kind of number that gets inflated in the retelling, but even if the true figure is a fraction of that, it is still an amount you associate with acquiring a company, not hiring a person.

That is the part I keep coming back to. When a firm is willing to pay acquisition prices for individual humans, it is telling you that it cannot get what it needs any other way. You do not offer someone a billion dollars when the thing they know is written up in a paper you can read, or baked into a model you can license, or reproducible by a competent team you already employ. You offer it when the knowledge is scarce, tacit, and concentrated in a small number of heads, and when being second to it is expensive enough that the price starts to look rational. I do not think these companies are being reckless. I think they are pricing a genuine bottleneck honestly, and the honesty is what makes it uncomfortable to watch.

## Meta is buying teams, not just hires

The more revealing move is that Meta is not really recruiting individuals. It is recruiting groups. The clearest example is the raid on Thinking Machines Lab, the startup founded by former OpenAI people. Meta reportedly tried to buy the whole company outright, was turned down, and then went after the founding team one at a time until several of them had signed. Zuckerberg is doing a lot of the recruiting personally, and the destination is a newly stood-up superintelligence lab that is being staffed the way you would assemble a sports roster, by target rather than by posting.

What buying a team says, and this is the part I find genuinely interesting as someone who has watched engineering orgs form and dissolve, is that the valuable unit is not the person. It is the group that has already shipped hard things together. A researcher who spent two years getting a frontier training run to converge alongside four other people carries context that does not transfer through a job description. The muscle memory of how that specific group argues, divides work, and recovers from a failed run is the asset, and you cannot hire it in pieces without risking that you have paid for the parts and lost the thing that made them work. Meta clearly understands this, which is why it went for the intact founding group rather than cherry-picking names.

## OpenAI's counter tells you compute is the other half

If you want confirmation that this is about scarce inputs rather than a single company's spending mood, look at how OpenAI answered. It did not only try to hold onto researchers. It went and hired the infrastructure people, including the architects who built xAI's 200,000-GPU Colossus cluster and a senior engineering leader out of Tesla. That is a tell. The counter to losing model researchers was to reinforce the ability to build and operate enormous clusters, because the two are inseparable. A brilliant research team with no compute is stuck, and a giant cluster with no one who knows how to wring training runs out of it is an expensive warehouse.

So the war is being fought on both fronts at once, for the people who know what to train and for the people who can stand up the machines to train it on, and the fact that both are being bid up tells you neither is abundant. Altman's public pushback, framed around missionaries versus mercenaries and warnings about culture, is the reaction of someone who cannot simply match every offer and has to compete on something else. I have some sympathy for it, because mission does hold teams together in ways money does not, but it is also the argument you make when you have lost the bidding on price.

## The pattern I would pull out of this

The pattern I would pull out of this summer is that the "models are a commodity" story was premature. If the frontier were genuinely commoditized, no one would pay a billion dollars for the people who make it, because you could just buy the output. The comp war is the strongest evidence I have seen that the capability still lives disproportionately in a small population of researchers and in access to compute at a scale only a handful of companies can afford, and that everything downstream, the products and the distribution and the enterprise deals, is still gated on those two inputs.

What I think this means for everyone who is not a hyperscaler is less dramatic than the headlines but more durable. If the scarce inputs are concentrated talent and concentrated compute, then the frontier is going to stay in very few hands for a while, and the interesting work for the rest of us is at the application layer, where the constraint is understanding a real problem well rather than out-bidding Meta for a research team. That is not a consolation prize. It is just a different game, played on the part of the board where scarcity is not measured in nine figures.
