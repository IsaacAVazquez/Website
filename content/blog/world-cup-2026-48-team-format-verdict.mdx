# The 48-Team World Cup Worked, but FIFA Should Not Keep Every Part of It

Before the tournament I thought the 48-team World Cup would move the drama rather than remove it, and after 104 matches I think that was mostly right. The group stage gave strong teams more room to survive a bad opening result, then the new Round of 32 gave those same teams one more elimination match to get wrong. Cape Verde held Spain, reached the knockouts, and pushed Argentina into extra time. Congo advanced and made England work. Curaçao reached its last group match with a path through. Germany and the Netherlands still went home in the first knockout round, while Morocco made another run into the last eight. Expansion gave more countries a real World Cup without preventing Spain, Argentina, France, and England from occupying the final four.

That is enough for me to call the central format a success. I would keep the 48 teams, twelve groups of four, and the Round of 32. The concern that the tournament would become six weeks of mismatches did not hold up, and the concern that best third-placed teams would eliminate group-stage jeopardy was only partly right. The math was harder to follow and some teams could play for a narrow target in their final match, which is exactly why I built [World Cup Pulse](/world-cup-2026), but the uncertainty also kept more groups live deeper into the final round. The [Associated Press tournament review](https://apnews.com/article/world-cup-final-spain-argentina-e45fd30932bb9885812805cafa99ca33) reached the same broad result from the matches, because the new entrants competed and the established powers still rose by the end.

The parts I would change sit around the football. FIFA took a format that already added forty matches and spread it across sixteen cities in three countries, then treated the summer heat as a problem that could be solved with a scheduled interruption. Every match stopped in the 22nd and 67th minutes for a three-minute hydration break, regardless of the temperature, venue, or whether the stadium had a roof. FIFA's own explanation was that the breaks addressed both heat and the load of playing as many as eight matches in 39 days. That concern was real, but a fixed break is a blunt answer. It turned cool-weather matches into television timeouts and gave coaches a formal tactical reset twice per game, while the genuinely dangerous matches still went ahead in conditions that deserved a stronger response.

The heat data is the part FIFA should not smooth over. [An analysis published during the final week](https://www.theguardian.com/football/2026/jul/16/world-cup-heat-players-union) found that nearly one in five matches reached heat and humidity levels at which the global players' union has recommended delays or postponements. What that means is that FIFA had a useful safety intervention but applied it backward. The organization standardized the break, even where the weather did not require one, while refusing to standardize a harder threshold for changing a kickoff time or delaying a match. I would keep mandatory cooling protocols when the conditions cross a defined line, and I would remove the universal stoppage when they do not.

The geography made that problem harder. A three-country World Cup can be a genuine regional event, and the host cities produced full stadiums and different kinds of home crowds, but the distances were closer to a domestic airline network than a tournament cluster. Teams moved between altitude, indoor venues, humid southern cities, and the Pacific coast while playing an extra knockout round. Fans faced the same map without charter flights or federation travel teams. I think the 2026 tournament proved the event can operate at this scale, but operation is a low bar. The next edition that covers a comparable footprint should use tighter regional pods through the early knockout rounds so a team's route is not also a recovery penalty.

Ticketing is where the claim that expansion makes the World Cup more accessible starts to come apart. More countries participated and more people had a team to follow, but [general-sale prices reported by the Associated Press](https://apnews.com/article/world-cup-final-spain-argentina-e45fd30932bb9885812805cafa99ca33) ran from $140 to $2,735 for group matches and reached $8,680 for the final. FIFA later offered some $60 tickets through the participating federations, and supporters still filled stadiums after a shaky opening stretch, but attendance does not make the pricing reasonable. It shows that scarcity let FIFA charge far beyond what most local supporters could pay. A bigger World Cup should create more access in the stands as well as more places in the draw.

The technology also needs a narrower remit. The connected ball and video review produced technically defensible calls on tiny touches and offsides, but Germany, Croatia, and Egypt all had major goals overturned in decisions that left players and supporters arguing about whether the system had improved the match or only made it more exact. VAR should remain because asking referees to guess would make the outcomes less accurate, but the offside system should be designed around a clear sporting advantage rather than the smallest sensor-detected contact that can be found after the celebration. Precision helps only when the rule being measured deserves that precision.

The pattern I would pull out of the first 48-team World Cup is that expansion was not the problem people expected. The football justified the larger field, and the extra knockout round gave the tournament more than it took away. The weaker decisions were about how FIFA packaged that scale, from fixed hydration breaks, to travel, to ticket prices, to review technology that could find a touch more easily than it could explain why the touch mattered. Keep the field. Keep the bracket. Change the operating choices that made a successful format harder on the people playing and watching it.
