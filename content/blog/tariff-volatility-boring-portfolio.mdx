# A Tariff Shock Is a Test of Your Behavior, Not Your Forecast

The tariff news landed this week and markets did what markets do with a sudden policy shock, which is drop first and figure out the details later, and the coverage has settled into the two modes it always uses for a selloff, panic and prediction. One set of takes tells you how bad this could get, and the other tells you exactly what will happen next and what to do about it, and I think both are the wrong thing to consume right now, because a shock like this is not really a test of whether you can forecast the outcome. It is a test of whether you can sit still, and for anyone holding a boring diversified portfolio, sitting still is usually the right move and also the hardest one, not because it is complicated but because it asks you to do almost nothing while every instinct and every headline is telling you to act. The difficulty is entirely psychological, and knowing that in advance is most of the battle.

I should say plainly that this is how I think about my own money rather than advice for yours, since risk tolerance and time horizon change the answer, and what I am describing is a way of reasoning, not a recommendation.

## What a shock actually does to a diversified portfolio, and what it does not

The first thing worth being clear-eyed about is that a broad selloff on a policy shock hits a diversified portfolio too, and pretending otherwise is how people end up disappointed and then reactive. Diversification does not mean you avoid the drawdown, it means you are not exposed to any single company or sector blowing up, and in a broad risk-off move nearly everything falls together for a while regardless of how well spread out you are. What diversification actually buys you is not immunity from the drop, it is a much better chance of participating in the recovery whenever it comes, because you did not concentrate into the specific thing that does not come back. So the honest expectation during a shock is that a diversified portfolio goes down and you feel it, and the case for holding it is about what happens after, not about dodging the fall, and confusing those two is what makes people abandon a sound approach at the worst moment.

## Selling into the fear is the one move that turns a drawdown into a loss

The specific mistake that a shock is engineered to produce is selling after the drop, and it is worth understanding why it is so tempting and so costly. A paper drawdown is not a realized loss, it is a number that can recover, and the only way to convert it into a permanent loss is to sell at the bottom, which is exactly what fear pushes you toward, because holding through the pain feels like inaction in the face of danger. The cruelest part is that the people who sell into the fear usually do not get back in until after the recovery is well underway, so they lock in the loss and miss the rebound, capturing the worst of both directions. I try to remember that the market does not know or care what price I bought at, and that the urge to sell is at its strongest precisely when selling is at its most expensive, and that the feeling of needing to do something is not information about what I should do.

## Rebalancing is the disciplined version of doing something

If the urge to act is overwhelming, and it often is, there is a productive channel for it that is the opposite of panic selling, which is rebalancing. When a shock drives some assets down more than others, your allocation drifts away from your targets, and rebalancing back to those targets means buying more of what fell, which is the disciplined form of buy low, done mechanically rather than on a brave prediction. This is the move that lets you act without acting on fear, because it is following a rule you set when you were calm rather than a feeling you have when you are scared, and it points you toward buying the thing everyone else is selling for reasons of process rather than nerve. I am not talking about backing up the truck on a hunch that this is the bottom, because I have no idea if it is, I am talking about the boring rebalancing discipline that happens to buy weakness as a byproduct of keeping your allocation where you decided it belonged.

## The boring portfolio's whole job is surviving the shock you did not predict

The reason to hold a plain, diversified, boring portfolio in the first place is days like this, and it is worth remembering that when the boringness suddenly feels inadequate. A boring portfolio is not built to win a specific forecast, it is built to survive the shocks you did not see coming, and a tariff move that few people were positioned for is exactly the kind of thing it is designed to absorb. The temptation during a shock is to conclude that boring was a mistake and that you should have been positioned for this, but that is hindsight talking, because you did not know this was coming and neither did anyone else, and a portfolio that only survives the shocks you predicted is not a portfolio, it is a series of bets. The whole point of boring is that it does not need you to have called this, and abandoning it because it did not dodge an unpredictable event is giving up the exact property you were paying for.

The pattern I would pull out of this week is that a tariff shock, like every shock, is a behavior test wearing the costume of a forecasting problem, and the edge is not a better prediction of where this goes, it is a decision about how you will act that you made before the fear arrived. A diversified portfolio will fall and then most likely recover if you let it, selling into the fear is the one reliable way to make the loss permanent, rebalancing is the disciplined way to channel the urge to act, and the boringness you are doubting is the thing built for exactly this. I cannot tell you where the market goes from here and neither can the confident takes, and the good news is that the right response never required knowing, which is what the [structure I hold](/investments) is really for.
