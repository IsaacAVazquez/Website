# The AI Trade Everyone Buried Over the Winter Just Came Back on Earnings

For about six months the consensus was that the AI trade had rolled over. From roughly last October through the selloff in March, the mood turned from euphoria to suspicion, and the suspicion had two specific shapes. One was that the spending was circular, with the same handful of companies funding each other's demand in ways that flattered everyone's numbers. The other was simpler, which was that hundreds of billions of dollars were going into datacenters and the return on it was still mostly a promise. The March drawdown looked like the market finally saying it out loud. So the thing I find most interesting about the spring is that the trade everyone had written off came back, and it didn't come back on a new story or a fresh burst of hype. It came back on earnings, which is the least glamorous and most convincing reason a market can have. I think that changes what the argument is about now, from whether AI is real to whether the whole index is leaning on too few names to carry it.

## The rally that died over the winter got resurrected by earnings, not story

The catalyst for the recovery was Q1 results, and they weren't close. Going into the season, expectations for the Magnificent Seven had been marked down to something like low-double-digit earnings growth, which was itself a concession to the bearish winter mood. What actually printed was blended growth well up into the mid-twenties percent, which is the kind of gap that moves a market on its own. When a group that large beats a lowered bar by that much, you don't need a narrative to explain the rally, because the numbers are the narrative. I'd stress how much that matters relative to the winter's worries. The circular-financing critique and the where-is-the-payoff critique are both, at bottom, claims that the reported strength isn't real. A quarter where the strength shows up in actual earnings, against estimates that had already been cut, is the most direct rebuttal available, and the market took it that way and repriced the group back up. That doesn't prove the bears were wrong about everything, but it does mean they lost the argument that mattered most this quarter, which was whether the fundamentals were quietly deteriorating underneath the spending.

## The recovery leaned even harder on a handful of names

Here's where I get more cautious, because the good news and the risk are the same fact. The Magnificent Seven now sits at around a third of the entire S&P 500, which means the index and this small group of companies have become close to the same trade. Alphabet has been the standout, sitting as the best performer in the group on the year, and Nvidia has climbed back to being the single most valuable company in the market. Those are genuinely strong businesses and I'm not being cute about that. But a market where one third of the value, and much more than a third of the recent movement, sits in seven names is a market that goes up together and comes down together. The earnings that resurrected the trade also deepened this concentration, because the money that flowed back in flowed into the same handful of tickers that led it out. So the index is healthier and more fragile at the same time. If you own a broad fund, you own a lot more of these seven companies than you probably think, and the diversification you assume you have is thinner than the label suggests. I keep a rough version of this exposure math in my own [investments](/investments) view for exactly that reason, because the headline index number hides how much of your outcome now rides on a few earnings prints.

## Nvidia's print is the swing vote, and it's a couple of weeks out

Which brings me to the near-term thing everyone is actually waiting on. Nvidia reports in roughly two weeks, around the twentieth, and it has become the single most-watched number in the market for a reason. It sits at the center of the whole capex story, since it's the company most directly selling the shovels, and the Magnificent Seven as a group are guiding to well over half a trillion dollars of AI spending this year, with some estimates running considerably higher. That spending is Nvidia's revenue, which is exactly the loop the bears call circular. So the print is a genuine swing vote. A clean beat with strong guidance confirms the payoff thesis and probably extends the spring recovery, because it says the capex is translating into real demand rather than inventory. A soft number or cautious guide, on the other hand, hands the bears their opening back and revives every circular-financing worry from the winter in a single afternoon. I don't have an edge on which way it goes, and I'd be suspicious of anyone who claims to. What I'd watch, more than the headline, is the relationship between capex and the revenue it's supposed to be producing, because that ratio is the actual thing in question and a beat that comes with even bigger spending commitments isn't obviously reassuring.

The pattern I'd pull out of this is that the market un-killed the AI trade the honest way, on earnings instead of hope, and then immediately made itself more dependent on the same few companies to keep doing it. The recovery is real and it's better-founded than the fall implied, but the thing that saved the index is also the thing that concentrates its risk, and those don't cancel out. So I'm reading the spring as vindication with an asterisk. The fundamentals answered the winter's doubts for now, and the price of that answer is that an unusually large share of everyone's portfolio now rides on whether a handful of companies, Nvidia first among them, keep printing numbers that justify the spending. I'd rather hold that tension clearly than pick a side of it too early.
