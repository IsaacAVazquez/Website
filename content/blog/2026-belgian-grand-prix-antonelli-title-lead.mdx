# Antonelli Won at Spa and Made Russell's Margin for Error Disappear

Two weeks ago Kimi Antonelli left Silverstone with a broken car, no points, and a championship lead that had fallen to twenty-five. He leaves Spa with his sixth win of the year, a fifty-point advantage over George Russell, and the clearest evidence yet that the brief tightening of this title race came from Mercedes reliability rather than a change in who has been fastest. Antonelli started on pole, lost the lead through the timing of a virtual safety car, passed Charles Leclerc back with ten laps remaining, and drove away. Russell spent almost the entire afternoon in the gravel after a first-lap collision.

The result sheet says Antonelli, Leclerc, and Max Verstappen, which looks orderly enough for a race that turned on two incidents. The first came when the virtual safety car gave Leclerc a cheaper stop and moved the Ferrari ahead of Antonelli. That forced the championship leader to win the race on track rather than manage it from the front, and he did. He stayed close enough to avoid burning through the tires, waited until the closing stint, then passed Leclerc with ten laps left and pulled clear. [Formula 1's official classification](https://www.formula1.com/en/results/2026/races/1290/belgium/race-result) records the sixth victory of Antonelli's season and career, which already puts the 19-year-old level with Riccardo Patrese for the second-most wins by an Italian driver.

The second incident happened on the opening lap and changed the standings more than the fight for the win. Lewis Hamilton pushed Russell into a spin, Russell could not get the Mercedes out of the gravel, and the driver who had spent the previous month taking chunks out of Antonelli's lead scored nothing. Hamilton recovered to fourth and moved to 159 points, five ahead of Russell and 45 behind Antonelli. Russell is now third on 154, fifty back, which is a brutal swing from the twenty-five-point gap after Silverstone and the kind of result a title challenger cannot absorb many times.

I do not think that means Russell is suddenly out of the championship. There is too much season left and Mercedes have already shown how quickly one mechanical failure can move the gap. Antonelli retired from a likely podium in Barcelona and from a likely win at Silverstone, while Russell lost a likely win in Canada to a power unit failure. The fastest car has also been the thing keeping both drivers within reach of each other. One bad weekend in Spa does not settle that pattern, and Hamilton has now moved between them in the standings without Ferrari having the consistently fastest package.

What Spa changes is the burden of proof inside Mercedes. When the lead fell to twenty-five, the team could treat both drivers as equal championship bets and let the track decide it. At fifty points, with Hamilton closer to Russell than Russell is to Antonelli, the default has to shift toward protecting Antonelli's campaign from more preventable failures. The shift should start with reliability and resource decisions, without reaching for team orders in July, because the teenager has built the larger margin and has won six times while the team's own car has been his most persistent rival.

Leclerc's second place matters for a different reason. Ferrari have now won two of the last four races through Hamilton in Barcelona and Leclerc at Silverstone, then put Leclerc between the Mercedes drivers at Spa. He briefly led because of the virtual safety car and made Antonelli pass him, but he did not have the late-race pace to keep the Mercedes behind. That is progress, and it is also the limit of the progress. Ferrari are close enough to decide which Mercedes driver loses points on a given Sunday, but they have not yet shown they can set the terms of the championship across a run of races.

The pattern I would pull out of Belgium is that the title fight was genuinely getting tighter, but it was tightening around Antonelli's failures. Spa gave him a clean enough car to answer on pace, and he turned a compromised strategy into a controlled win. Russell can still recover the ground, Hamilton can still stay in the conversation, and another Mercedes retirement can change the arithmetic again. The position after Spa is simpler than it was after Silverstone, though. Antonelli has the speed, the wins, and the margin, and Mercedes now has to make sure its own car stops being the reason any of those advantages disappear.
