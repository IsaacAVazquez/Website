# World Cup 2026 Countdown, No. 2: France Has the Best Squad and the Worst Draw

At number two in my [2026 World Cup countdown](/writing/world-cup-2026-top-ten-contenders) is the team that gave me the hardest decision on the entire list, and I want to be upfront about it. On raw talent, France might be my number one. They sit top of the FIFA ranking, they have the deepest squad in world football by a comfortable margin, and they have been to the last two World Cup finals. The only reason they are second instead of first is a draw so unkind that I could not in good conscience rank them ahead of a Spain side with a clear road. The talent says first. The bracket says second. I went with the bracket, and I will explain why.

## A decade at the summit

France's recent World Cup history is the best of any team in this field. They won the tournament in 2018 and were the runners-up in 2022, losing the final to Argentina only on penalties after one of the great individual performances in Kylian Mbappé's hat-trick. Before that they won in 1998 and reached the final of Euro 2016. For a decade, France has been either the best team in the world or a penalty kick away from it, and they have done it while continuously regenerating talent, losing world-class players to age and simply replacing them with more world-class players. That conveyor belt is the single most impressive thing about French football, and it is why they are a contender every single tournament regardless of who retired since the last one.

## The deepest squad at the tournament

Didier Deschamps named his squad on May 14, and he has confirmed this will be his final tournament in charge after an extraordinarily successful run, which adds a send-off narrative to go with everything else. Kylian Mbappé captains the side and is chasing the all-time France scoring record, and he remains one of the two or three best players on the planet, the kind of forward who can win a knockout game in a single sprint.

What makes France frightening, though, is not just Mbappé, it is everything around him. Ousmane Dembélé arrives as the reigning Ballon d'Or winner, Michael Olise has grown into one of the best wingers in Europe, and the attacking depth beyond them is almost unfair, with Bradley Barcola, Rayan Cherki, Désiré Doué and Marcus Thuram all available. Deschamps left out Eduardo Camavinga, a player good enough to start for most nations here, after a poor club season, which tells you how much quality he had to choose from. No team at this World Cup can match the sheer volume of elite attacking talent France can put on the pitch and bring off the bench.

## The Group of Death

And now the reason they are second. France drew Group I with Senegal, Norway and Iraq, and by the underlying numbers it is the Group of Death, the hardest group in the entire tournament. It is the only group with three top-twenty-five teams in it. Senegal is one of the best sides in Africa and a genuinely dangerous opponent. Norway arrives with Erling Haaland in the form of his life, fresh off a perfect qualifying campaign, and is the kind of team that can beat anyone on a given night. France should still advance, and probably should still win the group, but second place is a live possibility and even an early exit is not the fantasy it would be for a top seed in an easier group.

This matters enormously, because a hard group does two things. It raises the risk of a slip before France ever reaches the knockouts, and it forces them to spend real energy and intensity in games where the soft-group teams are cruising. Two of the best group-stage games of the whole tournament, France against Senegal and a Haaland-against-Mbappé meeting with Norway, are happening in week one, and that is a brutal way to start a World Cup.

## The betting angle

The market treats this as a two-horse race at the top, with France a co-favorite around +475 to +500 alongside Spain, and the best price at +500. The no-vig prediction markets like Kalshi and Polymarket have actually had France narrowly ahead of Spain since late April, which tells you the sharpest money sees them as genuinely the strongest team. In the staged markets they sit around +240 to reach the final. I read these prices as analysis rather than advice, and they move quickly, but the signal is consistent, the betting world rates France's squad right at the very top even while pricing in the difficult draw.

## Where I land

France at two is a ranking I could defend either direction, and if you told me they win the whole thing I would simply nod, because the talent is championship-grade and Deschamps knows exactly how to win these tournaments. The reason they are not my number one is that a countdown of likely outcomes has to account for the path, and France's path is a minefield from the opening match while the team above them got a clear road. Drawing the Group of Death does not make France a worse team. It makes their route to the trophy harder than the one team I have ranked ahead of them, and at this level, on margins this thin, the draw is the tiebreaker.

That is my number two. At number one is the team I believe is the most complete in the tournament, the reigning European champions, with the friendliest path of any contender and a young core that already plays like it has done this before. [Spain](/writing/world-cup-2026-contenders-1-spain) closes the countdown.
