# Build vs Buy for Agentic AI: When Startups Should Roll Their Own

The build versus buy conversation around agentic AI is getting worse at the same time it is getting more important. A lot of advice in this category comes from people who are arguing from identity rather than from product constraints. One camp treats frameworks as a sign that you are moving fast and staying practical. The other treats custom infrastructure as proof that you are building something real. I do not think either instinct is very helpful on its own.

My default view is that most startups should buy more than they think at the beginning and own more than they expect by the time the workflow actually matters. That sounds like a dodge, but it is not. It is just a recognition that the constraints change as the product gets real. Early on, the biggest risk is that you spend months building infrastructure around a workflow users do not care about. Later, the biggest risk is that you inherit abstractions that no longer fit the product you proved people want.

## Why I would start by buying

At the beginning, the question is usually product risk, not infrastructure purity. Does the workflow create value. Do users actually want the system to take the steps you think they want automated. Can you make the experience trustworthy enough that anyone will use it repeatedly. Those are the things I would want to learn quickly, and a framework or platform is usually the fastest way to learn them.

That does not mean frameworks are perfect. It means their flaws are often cheaper than building the wrong thing. If the workflow turns out to be weak, I would much rather discover that while standing on somebody else's abstractions than after spending a quarter building my own orchestration layer, state model, retry logic, and evaluation plumbing. Speed of learning matters more than elegance when the product still might be wrong.

I also think teams often underestimate how much of the product value lives above the orchestration layer anyway. Users care about whether the system fits their workflow, whether it is transparent, whether the outputs are useful, and whether the handoffs are clean. Very little of that value comes from having a lovingly handcrafted agent runtime on day one. A lot of it comes from making good product decisions while moving fast enough to get feedback.

## When the custom path starts making sense

The case for building usually shows up after the product earns the right to be optimized. It appears when context handling becomes specific enough that the framework assumptions fight you. It appears when audit requirements, privacy boundaries, or customer isolation rules stop being optional. It appears when unit economics start to matter and the framework overhead is no longer a rounding error. It appears when reliability work stops being edge-case cleanup and starts becoming the core of the product.

That is usually the point when teams realize the real question was never build or buy in the abstract. The real question was which decisions they needed to own. If your differentiation depends on how memory is managed, how tool calls are sequenced, how errors are recovered, or how customer data is partitioned, then those parts probably should not stay buried inside a generic layer you do not control. If your differentiation is mostly workflow design and domain context, a framework may keep being good enough longer than the engineers want to admit.

I think the biggest mistake on the build side is romanticizing ownership. Custom infrastructure has a maintenance cost that does not disappear because the architecture diagram looks impressive. The biggest mistake on the buy side is pretending migration will be painless once the framework no longer fits. It usually is not. Both paths create future bills. The right move is the one that buys the right learning now without trapping the product later.

So if I were advising an early-stage team, I would ask a very plain question. What part of this system must be yours for the product to work the way you intend. If the honest answer is "not much yet," buy aggressively and learn fast. If the answer starts becoming specific and expensive in the middle of growth, that is when the custom path becomes rational. Not because custom is more serious, but because the product finally has something serious enough to protect.
