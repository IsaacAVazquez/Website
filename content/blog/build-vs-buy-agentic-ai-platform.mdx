# Build vs Buy for Agentic AI: When Startups Should Roll Their Own

The build vs buy question comes up early in every agentic AI project, and the answer has gotten more complicated in the last 12 months. The platforms have matured, which makes buying more appealing. But the reasons to build your own have also become clearer, particularly as teams that started with frameworks have run into their limitations in production.

I want to walk through the actual trade-offs, because the advice circulating tends to collapse into either "always use a framework" or "roll your own for anything real," and both versions are wrong.

## What you are actually deciding

The build vs buy question in agentic AI is not a single decision. It has at least three layers: the model layer, the orchestration layer, and the application layer.

At the model layer, almost every team is buying. Building foundation models is a multi-billion-dollar endeavor that is not within scope for the vast majority of companies. Fine-tuning is within scope for some, and inference infrastructure decisions matter for latency and cost, but the model itself is bought.

At the orchestration layer, the question is real. Frameworks like LangChain, LlamaIndex, and others provide abstractions for building agentic workflows: memory management, tool calling, multi-agent coordination, state management. The question is whether these abstractions fit your use case or fight it.

At the application layer, you are always building. The actual product experience, the UX decisions, the escalation logic, the feedback loops: none of this comes from a framework. This is where most of the product differentiation lives.

## When to use a framework

Frameworks make sense when your use case is close to the patterns the framework was designed for, when speed of initial development matters more than long-term control, and when the team does not have deep experience with the lower-level mechanics of managing agent state and context.

For a proof of concept or an internal tool where production reliability is not the primary concern, a framework is almost always the right starting point. It lets you validate the workflow design without spending engineering time on infrastructure decisions you have not yet learned enough to make well.

For a product in a category where the framework has active development and strong community support for your specific use case, the buy decision also makes sense for longer than people expect. The frameworks have improved substantially and the common failure modes are better documented.

## When to build your own

The reasons to build your own orchestration tend to emerge from direct experience rather than from a priori reasoning. But they cluster around a few patterns.

Control over context management is the most common one. Frameworks make assumptions about how context is managed across agent interactions. Those assumptions work for the cases the framework was designed around. When your workflow has unusual memory requirements, unusual context window constraints, or unusual patterns for how information needs to flow between agents, the framework abstractions start fighting you rather than helping you. At that point, the cost of working around the framework exceeds the cost of building the specific thing you actually need.

Data handling and security requirements are the second common driver. Frameworks route data through their abstractions in ways that may not be compatible with strict data residency requirements, audit logging requirements, or customer data isolation requirements. A healthcare company or a financial services company building agentic workflows may find that the framework's data handling is non-negotiable in the wrong direction, and the cost of instrumenting around it is higher than building the relevant pieces themselves.

Performance at scale is the third. Frameworks add overhead. For most use cases, that overhead is acceptable. For high-volume workflows where latency directly affects user experience or cost, the overhead becomes a real constraint.

## The stage question

The right answer to build vs buy changes at different company stages, and I think this is where the generic advice breaks down most completely.

At early stage, before you have validated that the workflow design produces real value for users, the right answer is almost always to use a framework or even a higher-level platform. The risk is not technical. It is product. Getting to a working prototype quickly enough to learn from users is more valuable than owning the infrastructure you learn from.

At growth stage, when you know the workflow produces value and you are scaling usage, the constraints start appearing. Performance requirements tighten, cost per interaction matters, and the framework abstractions that were good enough for validation start showing their limitations. This is when the migration conversation typically starts.

At scale, the teams that have built their own orchestration are generally glad they did, and the teams that are still on unmodified frameworks are usually managing workarounds. The exception is the teams that started with frameworks and built sufficient abstraction layers above them that the framework is hidden behind interfaces they control.

## The honest version

I will say directly: most teams underestimate the ongoing maintenance cost of building their own agentic infrastructure, and most teams underestimate the eventual migration cost of staying on a framework that does not fit their use case. Both of these costs are real, and which one you should bear depends on your specific situation rather than on a general principle.

The useful question is not "framework or custom" but "which decisions do I need to own, and which can I delegate to the platform without significant risk?" Answering that question specifically is harder than choosing a general strategy, but it is the question that will determine whether you make the right call.
