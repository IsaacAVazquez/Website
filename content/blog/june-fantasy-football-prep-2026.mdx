# The Fantasy Football Work Worth Doing in June, and the Work That Isn't

Every June, a familiar argument resurfaces in fantasy circles. One camp says early preparation is how leagues get won, and points to the best ball degenerates who have been drafting since March. The other camp says anything you study before training camp is wasted effort, because depth charts in June are fan fiction and half your carefully formed opinions will be invalidated by one August beat report. I have been on both sides of this over the years. I have walked into Labor Day drafts cold and regretted it, and I have memorized June rankings that were worthless by my draft date. What I eventually figured out is that both camps are answering the wrong question. The question is not whether to prepare in June. It is which preparation expires and which compounds.

Most of what people call draft prep expires. A set of rankings memorized in June is a snapshot of a player pool that does not exist yet. Rookies have not seen a practice field in pads, coaching staffs have not tipped their usage plans, the July trade or the camp hamstring has not happened. Worse than the staleness is the anchoring. Opinions formed early harden, and the manager who decided in June that a player is a value will keep believing it in September after the situation that created the value has evaporated. I have done this. The early conviction felt like an edge and functioned as a liability, because I was drafting against my memory of the offseason instead of the offseason that actually occurred.

But some work genuinely compounds, and it is the work almost nobody does because it looks less like fantasy football and more like homework. Structural research, the kind whose conclusions do not depend on August news. Offensive line changes and what they imply for run games. Coordinator turnover and what each new scheme has historically meant for target distribution. Team pace and play volume trends, which are stickier year over year than most managers assume. Vacated targets and carries, which are arithmetic rather than opinion. None of this tells you whom to take in round three. All of it makes you faster and sharper at evaluating the August news when it arrives, because you already know the context the news lands in. The manager who knows a team lost 180 targets does not need a podcast to tell him what the rookie receiver's camp hype means. He can price it himself.

June is also the right time for format math, which has the convenient property of never expiring. If you read my piece on RB versus WR breakeven points across scoring systems, that analysis does not care what month it is, and neither does the work of actually understanding your own league's settings. Every year, managers in superflex leagues draft quarterbacks like they are in one-QB formats and donate value to the rest of the room for three rounds. Every year, managers in half PPR import full PPR rankings and overpay for target hogs by a round. Sitting down once in June with your league's exact scoring and roster rules and working out where the positional value actually sits is two hours of effort that pays at the draft regardless of anything that happens between now and then.

The other June activity I will defend is watching ADP move, with an important distinction. Not studying ADP to learn it, because June ADP itself is as perishable as June rankings. Watching it move, because the movement is the information. When a player climbs two rounds between June and August on no news, that tells you something about narrative momentum you would never detect from a single late-August snapshot, and fading narrative momentum is one of the few durable edges left in home leagues. Best ball drafts make this visible in a way redraft mocks never did, since real money creates real price discovery all summer. I do a handful of cheap best ball drafts in June and July less to win them than to feel where the market is drifting, which players the room consistently wants a round early, which ones keep falling past where the projections say they should. That feel survives into draft season even as the specific prices change.

So my June split looks like this, and it is intentionally modest. Structural research on a few hours a week, because it compounds. Format math once, because it is permanent. A few best ball drafts as market reconnaissance, not conviction-building. And a deliberate refusal to write rankings or fall in love with anyone, because every conviction formed in June is a debt that comes due in August when I have to decide whether the situation changed or whether I am just anchored. The actual rankings work, the tier construction, the draft-day plan, all of that waits for camp, when the information it depends on starts to exist.

The honest summary is that June preparation is real but it is not the preparation people picture. You are not learning the answers early. The answers do not exist yet. You are building the machine that will process the answers quickly when they arrive, and the managers who confuse those two activities end up either unprepared in September or, worse, confidently prepared for a season that never happened.
