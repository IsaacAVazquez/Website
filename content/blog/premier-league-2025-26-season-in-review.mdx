# The 2025-26 Premier League Season in Review: What It Was Actually About

I have been checking in on this season all year, writing down what I thought at each stage rather than waiting for hindsight to make me look smart, and now that it is over I want to pull the whole thing together and say what I think it was actually about. Every season has a headline, and the headline here is Arsenal ending a 22-year wait. But the more interesting version of the story is the one underneath the headline, about how this title was built, what happened to the teams that were supposed to challenge for it, and how the shape of the whole league shifted in ways that will matter beyond this one year.

The through-line, the thing I kept coming back to from October onward, is that the title went to the team with the highest floor rather than the highest ceiling. Arsenal won the league on 85 points, seven clear of Manchester City, and they won it on defense. David Raya's Golden Glove, the long runs of clean sheets, the back line that held its shape through the winter congestion that usually breaks title challengers, all of it was the same story told in different months. I trusted that lead all season for exactly that reason, because a team built on not conceding does not depend on anyone staying hot, and the one time the formula was genuinely threatened, in January when the goals dried up and the lead shrank to almost nothing, Arsenal answered in April with a run of wins without conceding and pulled clear. What changed for a club that had spent years finishing close was not the talent, it was the nerve, and I think that is the truest sentence I can write about the season.

## The teams that were supposed to challenge

Manchester City did not collapse, which is worth saying plainly, because losing a title race and throwing one away are different things. City ran their usual season, stayed in range, and made their move at the right moment, and in most years the leader cracks and that is enough. This year the leader did not crack, and there was not much City could do about a rival that refused to blink. Finishing second on 78 points is not a failure, it is the ordinary cost of running into a team having the season Arsenal had.

Liverpool are the harder story. They came into the year as defending champions, made the early running exactly as champions should, and then unraveled to a fifth-place finish that I still find a little startling. A title defense that turns into a scramble to hold a top-five place is the kind of fall that raises real questions about a club, and I spent the middle months of the season watching a reigning champion quietly become a team fighting for a Champions League spot rather than defending anything. Fifth is not a disaster in isolation, but measured against where they started and what they were defending, it is the most disappointing season any big club had.

Manchester United are the counterpoint, and their season is the one I would point to if someone asked how much difference a single player can make. A club that finished fifteenth a year earlier climbed to third, and the engine of it was Bruno Fernandes producing the best creative season the league has ever recorded, 21 assists to break a record shared by Henry and De Bruyne, on the way to Player of the Season. I wrote in March that this was close to a single-handed transformation and that the floor under United was lower than their position suggested, and I would stand by both halves of that. It was a wonderful individual season carrying a team a very long way, and it was fragile in a way that a title-winning project is not.

## The shift that will outlast this season

The development I think matters most beyond 2025-26 is the one at the bottom of the table, where the promoted clubs finally broke the pattern of going straight back down. Sunderland finished seventh and qualified for Europe, Leeds stayed up, and only Burnley of the promoted three went back down, alongside two established clubs in Wolves and West Ham. For two seasons the gap between the divisions had looked permanent, with all three promoted teams relegated each year, and this season is the evidence that it is not as fixed as it seemed. When the promoted clubs can actually compete, the jeopardy does not disappear, it moves onto the established mid-table clubs that used to feel safe, and West Ham missing survival by two points is the sharpest illustration of that. If this holds, it changes what promotion means and what safety means, and that is a bigger structural story than any one title.

The pattern I would pull out of the whole season is that the league rewarded substance over spectacle at both ends of the table. The title went to the best-defending team rather than the best-attacking one, the individual award went to the best individual season rather than the champion, and the clubs that survived at the bottom were the ones that recruited and organized well rather than the ones with the biggest names.
