# The Off-Year Elections Are a Read on Cost of Living, Not a Referendum

There's a ritual that comes with every off-year election, and it starts before the polls even close. By Wednesday morning we'll have a stack of confident takes explaining what New Jersey, Virginia, and New York City "mean" for the country, and most of them will read Tuesday as a referendum on the president. I think that framing is too big for what the evidence can carry. Off-year electorates are small, older, and more politically engaged than the ones that turn out for a midterm or a presidential year, so reading a national mood off them is a little like describing a whole book from the couple of pages that happened to fall open. What I think these races actually tell you, if you pay attention to what the candidates chose to run on rather than just who ends up winning, is narrower and more useful than a referendum. Across a democratic socialist in New York, a former Navy pilot in New Jersey, and a former CIA officer in Virginia, the throughline is cost of living, and that convergence is the thing I'll be watching for when the returns come in.

I want to be upfront that this publishes before any votes are counted, so I'm not going to pretend to know the outcomes. What I can do is say what I think would count as a real signal and what I think would be noise.

## The New York mayor's race is a cost-of-living campaign wearing an ideological label

The coverage of the New York mayor's race has fixated on the word socialist, because Zohran Mamdani is 34 and runs as a democratic socialist against Andrew Cuomo and Curtis Sliwa, and that's the more dramatic story. But if you actually read the platform, it's an affordability platform first. Free buses, universal childcare, and a freeze on rent for stabilized apartments, paid for by higher taxes on high earners and corporations. You can think the math is sound or think it doesn't add up, and reasonable people land in both places, but the campaign itself is a bet that voters will reward whoever speaks most directly to the price of living in the city day to day. What I'll be watching isn't only whether that bet pays off, but whether the affordability message is what drives turnout, because that's the part that generalizes beyond New York.

## New Jersey and Virginia are running the same theme in a different accent

New Jersey and Virginia are where the same theme shows up in a much more moderate register, which is what makes them useful. In New Jersey, Mikie Sherrill has run hard on electricity costs, which are up around 22% in the state this year, and on a promise to freeze rates, against Jack Ciattarelli. In Virginia, Abigail Spanberger has centered housing, energy, and healthcare costs against Winsome Earle-Sears. These are two candidates who are nothing like Mamdani ideologically, running in two states that are nothing like New York City, and they picked the same organizing theme. That's the part I find telling. When candidates across that wide a range independently decide that cost of living is the ground worth fighting on, the choice itself is data, somewhat separate from who wins. What I'd watch on Tuesday is whether the affordability-forward candidates run ahead of or behind the 2024 baseline in their states, because the margin relative to that baseline says more than the win or loss does.

## California's Prop 50 is the ballot item that isn't about affordability

California's Proposition 50 is the counter-case worth naming, because it's the marquee item on the ballot that has nothing to do with kitchen-table costs. It's a mid-decade redistricting fight, a question about political power and process, and it'll get read as a national maneuver rather than a statement about anyone's grocery bill. I mention it because I don't want to force every result into one story. Not everything on Tuesday is about cost of living. But the fact that the candidate races converged so hard on affordability while the pure power play sits off to the side actually sharpens the point rather than muddying it.

## What I'd count as a signal, and what I'd treat as noise

Here's the honest version of what I'll conclude and what I won't. If Democrats carry New Jersey and Virginia and the New York race goes to the affordability candidate, the referendum crowd will call it a repudiation of the president, and I'd resist that read, because these electorates are too small and too self-selected to carry it. The cleaner signal is in the margins relative to 2024 and in who actually showed up. If affordability-forward candidates outperform their polling across very different states and ideologies, that tells me 2026 gets fought on cost of living and that the party able to own that issue has an edge. If those same candidates underperform their own numbers, the signal runs the other way, that the cost-of-living message isn't converting into votes the way every campaign currently assumes it will.

The pattern I'd pull out of this, regardless of how Tuesday breaks, is that the candidates have already told us where the fight is. Across an unusually wide ideological range, from a self-described socialist to a couple of pragmatic moderates, they independently decided that affordability is the terrain, and that convergence is a more reliable leading indicator of the 2026 mood than any single result will be. The winners will get the headlines on Wednesday, but the thing I'll actually file away is that the whole field, left to right, is already running on the cost of living.
