# The Economy Looks Fine Because a Few Companies Are Spending Enormously

The consensus read on the economy right now is that it's resilient. Growth is holding up, the headline indexes are near records, and the recession that got forecast repeatedly over the past couple of years still hasn't shown up. I think that read is technically true and pretty misleading, and the gap between those two things is the most important thing to understand about where we are in early 2026. A strikingly large share of the growth that makes the economy look fine is a handful of hyperscalers spending unprecedented sums to build data centers, and underneath that spending the parts of the economy most people actually live in, from the job market to housing, have been softening. An expansion that's held up by the capital budgets of five or six companies is a fragile kind of fine, and I don't think the top-line number is telling you what most people assume it's telling you.

## The top-line is being carried by data-center construction

The thing that's easy to miss in a decent growth print is how concentrated the source of the growth has become. Analysts have been flagging for a while now that AI and data-center investment has accounted for an outsized share of recent growth, with estimates running toward it being responsible for the majority of the expansion in the most recent quarters, and projections that it adds something on the order of two and a half percentage points to growth this year. I'd hold those specific figures loosely, because the estimates vary and the full data lags, but the direction is not really in dispute. Strip out the data-center build-out and the picture underneath is much flatter than the headline suggests. That's a very different economy from one where growth is broad, and it matters because concentration is fragility. When the growth comes from one source, it's only as durable as that one source's willingness to keep spending.

## The spending creates dollars of GDP but very few jobs

What makes this build-out unusual, and what makes the divergence I'm describing possible in the first place, is that data-center investment converts enormous amounts of capital into GDP with unusually little labor attached. The number of jobs created per million dollars of data-center spending is far below what you'd get from most other kinds of investment, so the money shows up in the growth statistics without showing up as broad hiring. Goldman has pointed out that the bottleneck on the build-out is even shifting from power toward skilled trades, with something like 600,000 trade jobs open against only around 150,000 apprentices entering the field a year, which tells you how little of this spending flows through to the labor market. So you can get a strong growth number and a weak jobs picture at the same time, not as a contradiction, but as a direct consequence of what kind of spending is doing the work.

## Underneath the capex, labor and housing have been cooling

While the top-line holds, the parts of the economy people experience directly have been going the other way. Hiring has been slowing into early 2026, with the labor market cooling in a way that the aggregate growth figure obscures, and housing affordability is still strained enough that a lot of would-be buyers remain locked out, with sales soft. None of that is catastrophic on its own, and I want to be careful not to narrate a downturn that hasn't arrived. But the lived economy for most households, the one made of paychecks and rent and whether it's possible to buy a house, is not the boom that the index charts imply, and that gap is exactly what a growth number carried by capital spending will hide. This is the kind of divergence I keep an eye on in the [investing dashboard](/investments) I maintain, because the top-line and the household-level picture have rarely felt this far apart.

## What would confirm this thesis, and what would break it

I try to state what would prove me wrong, so here's the honest test. The thesis gets confirmed if capex keeps climbing while wages, hiring, and housing stay soft, and especially if any pause in the data-center spending shows up almost immediately as a growth air-pocket, because that would demonstrate how much the whole number was leaning on it. It gets broken in one of two good ways. Either the real economy firms up on its own, with the consumer and the labor market and housing re-accelerating so the growth broadens out and no longer depends on a few capital budgets, or the capex actually starts producing the broad productivity gains it's supposed to, diffusing out beyond the companies doing the building so the spending justifies itself in output rather than just in growth accounting. I'm genuinely watching for the second one, because that's the version where this ends well, and I haven't seen convincing evidence of it yet.

The pattern I'd pull out of this is that "the economy is fine" and "the economy is fragile" are both true right now, and they're actually the same sentence, because it's fine on account of the spending and fragile on account of depending on it. A healthy expansion tends to broaden over time, spreading from wherever it started into wages and hiring and the rest of the real economy, and this one has done the opposite and narrowed down to a single engine. That doesn't mean a downturn is coming next quarter, and I'm not predicting one. It means the top-line has stopped being a good summary of how most people are doing, and the thing I'd actually watch isn't the index at all, it's whether hiring and housing stabilize before the capex cycle slows, because that sequence is what decides whether this holds together or comes apart.
