# The Companies and Watches That Shaped Modern Horology

In a companion piece I traced [the history of horology](/writing/a-history-of-horology) as a story about oscillators, the long search for a steadier beat to count against. That arc explains how clocks got accurate. It does not explain why a steel watch with a movement no more precise than a cheap quartz module can sell for the price of a house. To understand that, you have to look at the houses and the specific watches that turned timekeeping into something people care about far past its function. What I find fascinating is that almost every era of modern watchmaking was defined less by a new oscillator than by a company answering a particular question of its moment, and the answers became the objects we still recognize on sight.

## The pocket-watch era and the race for complications

Before timekeeping moved to the wrist, the prestige of a watch was measured by what else it could do besides tell the time. This was the age of the complication, the additional mechanical functions layered onto the movement, and the houses that mastered them in the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries still anchor the top of the market today. Vacheron Constantin, founded in Geneva in 1755 and generally considered the oldest watch manufacturer in continuous operation, comes out of this world, as does Blancpain, which dates its founding to 1735. Patek Philippe, established in 1839, built its entire identity on the hardest complications, the perpetual calendar that tracks leap years on its own, the minute repeater that chimes the time on demand, and the split-seconds chronograph.

The single name that towers over this period is Abraham-Louis Breguet, who founded his house in 1775 and invented so much of the vocabulary that the rest of the craft is still using his ideas. The tourbillon, patented in 1801, is the most famous. It puts the balance and escapement inside a rotating cage so that gravity, which pulls unevenly on a balance wheel sitting in a pocket all day, averages out across the rotation. It was a genuine engineering answer to a real error source, and it is telling that today it survives almost entirely as a display of skill rather than a necessity, because the wristwatch it was never designed for keeps changing position naturally.

What is easy to forget is that the most consequential horology of this era was not Swiss luxury but American industry. Firms like Waltham and Elgin pioneered the mass production of interchangeable watch parts in the mid-nineteenth century, turning the watch from a handmade object into something a working person could own. The pressure that forced real standardization was the railroad. After a collision in Kipton, Ohio in 1891 was traced to a conductor's stopped watch, the American railroads adopted strict timepiece standards, and the railroad-grade pocket watch became the benchmark for everyday accuracy. I think this is the quiet turning point where precise time stopped being a luxury and became infrastructure.

## The wrist, and the watches that got there first

For most of its history the serious watch lived in a pocket, and the wristwatch was dismissed as jewelry for women. Two things changed that. The first was a specific commission. In 1904 the aviation pioneer Alberto Santos-Dumont told his friend Louis Cartier that he could not check a pocket watch while flying, and Cartier built him a flat, square watch to wear on the wrist. The Cartier Santos is one of the first purpose-built men's wristwatches, and Cartier followed it in 1917 with the Tank, whose case was inspired by the tracks of the armored vehicles on the Western Front. The Tank is, to me, the clearest proof that a watch can become a permanent design object, because it has barely changed in over a century and still looks modern.

The second force was the First World War itself. Soldiers needed to read the time and coordinate without fumbling for a pocket watch, so they strapped them to their wrists, and the habit came home with them. Once the wristwatch was respectable, the next problem was that it was fragile and exposed. Rolex, founded by Hans Wilsdorf in 1905, made solving that its mission. The Oyster case of 1926 was the first genuinely waterproof wristwatch, and Wilsdorf proved it in 1927 by having the swimmer Mercedes Gleitze wear one across the English Channel. In 1931 Rolex added the Perpetual rotor, a self-winding mechanism using a weight that swings full circle with the motion of your arm, building on John Harwood's earlier automatic design and turning it into the system nearly every mechanical watch uses now.

## The tool-watch generation

The era I find most appealing is the postwar stretch from the late 1940s into the 1960s, when watches were engineered as serious instruments for specific jobs. Diving, flying, racing, and eventually spaceflight each got a purpose-built watch, and those designs are now the most recognizable objects in the entire category. The dive watch arrived in 1953 from two directions at once, with Blancpain's Fifty Fathoms and the Rolex Submariner both built to stay readable and watertight at depth, with rotating bezels to track elapsed dive time. That same year the Rolex Explorer rode along on the first summit of Everest. In 1954 the Rolex GMT-Master gave Pan Am pilots a second time zone for the new era of long-haul jet travel.

The watch that earned the most serious credential of all was the Omega Speedmaster. Omega, founded in 1848, introduced the Speedmaster as a racing chronograph in 1957, but its place in history came from NASA, which put it through a brutal qualification gauntlet and certified it for crewed spaceflight. When Buzz Aldrin stepped onto the lunar surface in 1969, a Speedmaster was on his wrist, and it has been the Moonwatch ever since. There is something I genuinely love about the fact that the watch humanity wore to the Moon was a manual-wind chronograph you could still buy in a shop, chosen because it survived the testing rather than because it was exotic.

This was also the golden age of the chronograph, and it produced one of horology's great sprints. In 1969 three different efforts raced to build the first automatic chronograph, with Zenith launching its high-frequency El Primero, a Heuer-led consortium releasing the Calibre 11, and Seiko shipping its own automatic chronograph in Japan. Out of that moment came the Heuer Monaco, the square-cased chronograph Steve McQueen wore in the film Le Mans, which is now inseparable from the brand that became TAG Heuer.

## The quartz revolution and the crisis it triggered

Everything above was built on the mechanical movement, and at the end of that same year, 1969, Seiko made most of it obsolete. The Seiko Astron was the first quartz wristwatch, accurate to a handful of seconds a month and far beyond the reach of any mechanical watch at any price. Seiko, founded in 1881, had quietly bet on electronics, and the bet paid off in a way that nearly destroyed an entire country's industry. The years that followed are known as the quartz crisis, and the Swiss watch sector, which had assumed mechanical superiority was permanent, was gutted as cheap and accurate quartz watches poured out of Japan.

The Swiss response is one of my favorite business stories in any industry. Rather than try to out-engineer quartz on price, two answers emerged. One was to embrace it. The Swatch, launched in 1983, was a cheerful, inexpensive plastic quartz watch that reframed the product as a fashion accessory you bought several of, and the group behind it, led by Nicolas Hayek, generated the cash that ultimately kept Swiss watchmaking alive. The other answer came from Japan again, where Casio's G-Shock, also from 1983, took the durability idea to its extreme with a digital watch engineered to survive being dropped, which created an entire category of tough watches that still thrives.

## The luxury sports watch and the mechanical renaissance

The most counterintuitive move of the quartz era actually predates the worst of the crisis, and it is the reason mechanical watchmaking survived as a luxury rather than dying as a technology. In 1972 Audemars Piguet, a house founded in 1875, asked the designer Gérald Genta to create something new, and he drew the Royal Oak overnight. It was a luxury sports watch made of steel, with an octagonal bezel held by exposed screws, priced higher than many gold watches of the time. The idea that steel could be more desirable than precious metal was heretical, and it worked. Genta repeated the trick in 1976 with the Patek Philippe Nautilus, and those two watches essentially invented the integrated-bracelet luxury sports category that dominates the high end today.

That repositioning is what let the craft come back. Once a mechanical watch was understood as an object of art and identity rather than a tool for telling time, accuracy stopped being the selling point and finishing, heritage, and design took over. The clearest symbol of the revival is A. Lange & Söhne, the great German house from Glashütte founded in 1845, which vanished behind the Iron Curtain after the Second World War and was refounded by Walter Lange in 1990 after reunification. Its 1994 Lange 1, with its off-center dial and oversized date, announced that serious watchmaking was alive again. Even the technical innovation of this era is craft-driven, like the co-axial escapement that the independent watchmaker George Daniels spent decades developing to reduce friction and that Omega finally industrialized in 1999, or Seiko's Spring Drive, which by the late 1990s blended a mechanical mainspring with quartz-level regulation to produce a perfectly gliding seconds hand.

## The smart generation

The newest chapter rhymes with the quartz crisis but has played out differently. The Apple Watch, introduced in 2015, became within a few years the best-selling watch on Earth, and by unit volume Apple now ships more watches than the entire Swiss industry combined. By every functional measure it is the most capable thing you can wear on your wrist, the way a quartz module once was against a mechanical movement.

What did not happen is the part I find most revealing. The mechanical watch did not collapse a second time. If anything, the rise of the smartwatch sharpened the appeal of the mechanical one, because choosing a hand-wound movement in a world of notifications is now an obvious statement about how you want to relate to time. The two products stopped competing on the same axis entirely. That is the through-line across every generation in this story. The watch keeps surviving the thing that should have killed it by changing what it is for, from a pocket instrument to a wrist tool to a piece of engineering you wear precisely because you do not need it to keep time. The oscillators got better and then they stopped mattering, and the objects we built around them turned out to be the part that lasted.
