# World Cup 2026 Countdown, No. 7: Germany Got the Easiest Road in the Bracket

Number seven in my [2026 World Cup countdown](/writing/world-cup-2026-top-ten-contenders) is the placement I expect to take the most heat for, and I understand why. Germany has spent the last two World Cups doing something a four-time champion is not supposed to do, going home after three games. Ranking them seventh, above a Netherlands side with arguably more pedigree right now, looks like nostalgia. It is not. It is a bet on two things that are very much about 2026 rather than the trophies in the cabinet, a generational attacking talent and the single softest draw of any serious contender in this tournament.

## A proud program that fell off a cliff

Let me be fair to the skeptics first, because their case is strong. Germany won this tournament in 2014 and looked like a machine doing it. Then the floor gave way. They crashed out of the 2018 World Cup in the group stage as defending champions, did the exact same thing in 2022, and lost a home European Championship quarterfinal to Spain in 2024. That is not a blip. That is a six-year identity crisis at the highest level, and any honest ranking has to price it in. When I put Germany seventh, I am not telling you they have fixed everything. I am telling you the combination of talent and circumstance is good enough to overcome a team that still has real questions about whether it can win a knockout game under pressure.

## The reason to believe is two players

Julian Nagelsmann named his squad on May 21, and the heart of the argument for Germany is the pairing of Jamal Musiala and Florian Wirtz. Musiala has recovered from the broken leg he suffered at the Club World Cup last summer, and when he is at his best he is one of the very few players in this tournament who can beat several defenders in a phone box and create something from nothing. Wirtz arrives for his first World Cup as one of the best attacking midfielders in the world after his move to Liverpool. Between them they give Germany a creative axis that almost no other team can match, and creativity in the final third is exactly what wins the tight games that knocked Germany out in the past.

Joshua Kimmich captains the side and remains the connective tissue in midfield. The headline of the squad announcement was the surprise recall of Manuel Neuer at forty years old, a decision forced partly by Ter Stegen's injury, and it is a gamble. Kai Havertz is likely to operate as a false nine, which is less a tactical masterstroke than an admission that Germany does not have a top-tier orthodox striker. So the squad is not flawless. But the top end of the attacking talent is genuinely elite, and that is what I am ranking.

## The draw is the whole argument

Here is the part that pushes Germany from interesting to top-seven. They drew Group E with Curaçao, Ivory Coast and Ecuador, and by the underlying numbers it is the softest group any contender received. Germany's power rating dwarfs the rest of the group, and a clean three-win march into the knockouts is very much on the table. Just as importantly, Germany sits on a side of the bracket where the first genuinely hard test does not arrive until the quarterfinals.

I keep coming back to this because it matters more than people think. A flawed team with a young, brilliant attack and an easy road is a very different proposition than the same team in a brutal group. Germany gets to build rhythm and confidence against beatable opponents, exactly the thing a fragile side needs, before it has to prove itself. That is a real edge, and it is why I am willing to look past two group-stage exits and rank them ahead of teams with steadier recent records.

## The betting angle

The market has Germany around +1400, which positions them as the bridge between the elite tier and the mid-pack. I think that is about right on the raw price, but the value here is not really in the headline number, it is in the path. A team priced at +1400 with a near-guaranteed run to the quarterfinals is a more interesting proposition than a team at the same price staring down a group of death, and that is the case for Germany doing better than its odds suggest if Musiala stays healthy. As always I am treating these numbers as analysis rather than advice, and they will move, so check a current board before reading too much into any single figure.

The risk the market is pricing, correctly, is that this is still a team that has not proven it can win when the games get tight, and an easy group does nothing to answer that question until the quarterfinals arrive.

## Where I land

Germany at seven is a bet that talent plus circumstance can paper over a genuine psychological wound. I do not trust this team the way I trust the six ahead of it, and if you told me they went out in the round of sixteen to a well-organized opponent I would not be shocked, because we have seen this group fold before. But I cannot ignore that Musiala and Wirtz give them a higher attacking ceiling than anyone ranked just below them, and that the bracket handed them the gentlest possible route to use it. Soft draws are worth real placement at a World Cup, and Germany got the softest one.

That is my number seven. At number six is a team that drew the opposite of Germany's easy road, landing in the second-toughest group in the tournament, yet is rated higher than this Germany side by almost every model because the midfield might be the best at the World Cup. [Portugal](/writing/world-cup-2026-contenders-6-portugal), and Cristiano Ronaldo's last shot at the one trophy missing, is next.
