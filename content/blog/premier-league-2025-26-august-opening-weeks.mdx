# Premier League 2025-26, the Opening Weeks: Don't Trust the August Table

I want to start a season-long habit this year, which is checking in on the Premier League every so often and writing down what I actually think is happening rather than what the table says is happening. I built [Premier League Pulse](/premier-league) partly so I could look at the standings, the recent fixtures, and a club's form in one place, and the opening weeks of 2025-26 are a good reminder of why that matters. The August table is the least trustworthy table of the whole year, and every season a few people talk themselves into believing it.

The reason August lies is that the sample is tiny and the fixtures are lopsided. Three or four games in, a club that drew the two toughest opponents on the calendar can look like it is in trouble when it is playing fine, and a club that caught two of the promoted sides early can look like a contender when it has not really been tested. So I try to separate the results I believe from the results I am setting aside until the schedule evens out. What I found useful in these first weeks was to keep asking whether a club's position was a reflection of how it was playing or a reflection of who it happened to play.

## Liverpool started like defending champions

The clearest thing in the opening weeks was Liverpool. They came into the season as the defending champions, and they carried themselves that way, getting out to a fast start and sitting at or near the top through the early rounds. I think that is worth taking mostly at face value, because a fast start from a team that already knows how to win the league is different from a fast start by a team that has never done it. The habits are already there. When Liverpool go on a run, the question is usually not whether they are good enough but whether they can sustain it across the congestion later in the year, and it was far too early in August to answer that.

What I would flag, even while giving Liverpool credit, is that leading in August is the easiest lead to hold and the easiest to lose. It tells you a team is in form. It does not tell you the team is going to be there in May. I made a note to come back to that one.

## The promoted three looked better than promoted teams usually look

The story I found most interesting in the opening weeks was the three promoted clubs, Leeds United, Burnley, and Sunderland. The recent history here is brutal, since all three promoted teams went straight back down in each of the two previous seasons, and the smart expectation coming in was more of the same. What I saw early was different. All three of them looked like they belonged, and Sunderland in particular started picking up points at a rate that nobody who watched the Championship play-offs would have predicted for them.

I am cautious about reading too much into it this early, because the promoted-team collapse usually does not happen in August, it happens in the winter when the fixtures pile up and the squad depth runs thin. But the early signal was real enough that I moved the promoted clubs from the "probably doomed" column to the "let us actually watch this" column, which is more than I expected to be doing four weeks in.

## Arsenal did the quiet thing they do

Arsenal did not dominate the opening-weeks conversation, and I think that is exactly the point. They went about their business, stayed near the top without making a lot of noise, and gave the early attention to Liverpool. Arsenal have been the team that finishes close for a couple of years now, and the honest question about them coming into this season was never talent, it was whether they could turn all those second-place finishes into actually being in front when it counted. You do not answer that in August either, but a quiet, steady, unbothered start is the kind of thing that tends to matter more in hindsight than it feels like at the time.

The pattern I would pull out of the opening weeks is that the two clubs I ended up watching most closely were the loud one and the quiet one, Liverpool making the early running and Arsenal sitting just behind without any drama. The promoted teams were the genuine surprise, and the rest of the noise, the panic about a big club that had played two hard games and the excitement about a club that had played two easy ones, I mostly set aside. August is for separating signal from noise, and this August had more signal than most. I will check back in when the schedule has had a chance to tell the truth.
