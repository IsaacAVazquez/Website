# Using AI for Quant Cases and Financial Models Without Cheating

I came into my MBA from a QA and accounting background, which means I have spent more time than most people thinking about what it means to verify that a model is correct. Not just whether the math checks out, but whether the structure reflects the actual business dynamics, whether the assumptions are defensible, and whether the outputs mean what they appear to mean. That background shapes how I use AI in financial modeling and case prep, because the failure mode I worry about most is not making arithmetic errors. It is producing something that sounds rigorous but is not.

AI can help you build a financial model faster than you could build it yourself. It can check your logic in ways that would take twice as long to do manually. The skill is knowing where that help ends and where your thinking has to take over. That boundary is specific enough to describe.

## What quant cases actually test

Before getting into the AI workflow, it is worth being clear about what a quant case or financial modeling exercise is evaluating. The interviewer is not primarily interested in whether you can calculate the right number. Calculators exist. They are interested in whether you understand which number to calculate, why it matters, and what assumptions it depends on.

A market sizing question is not really about getting to 47 million users. It is about structuring a segmentation approach that is defensible, being transparent about your assumptions, and being able to explain what would change the answer. A financial model for a case is not primarily about the Excel mechanics. It is about understanding which drivers move the output and which can be treated as fixed.

This matters for how you use AI, because if you use it to generate the structure of your analysis, you have outsourced the part that is being evaluated. You will have an answer without understanding the reasoning behind it, which becomes obvious the moment an interviewer asks a follow-up question.

## Where AI accelerates the model construction

The parts of financial modeling that AI genuinely helps with are the mechanical parts that take time but do not require the judgment calls that matter.

When I know what I am trying to model, I use Claude to help me build the formula logic faster than I could from scratch. I describe the model structure I want: revenue built up from monthly active users times conversion rate times average order value, costs broken into fixed and variable components, a simple three-year projection. Claude translates that structure into formula logic and flags things I might have missed, like whether I want gross margin before or after fulfillment costs, or whether I need to account for seasonality in the monthly figures.

The verification use case is where AI saves me the most time in practice. I build a model, then describe the key formulas and relationships to Claude and ask it to find the three most likely places where my logic might be wrong or my assumptions might not hold. It almost always surfaces something. Not because I am careless, but because checking your own model is hard, and having a second pass that looks for structural errors rather than just arithmetic errors is genuinely useful.

I also use it for scenario setup. Once I have a base case built, I describe the model to Claude and ask what the two or three most important variables are for sensitivity analysis. The answer is usually intuitive once it is stated, but having it framed explicitly helps me decide which scenarios to run.

## The hypothesis question is yours

The place where AI cannot help is forming the initial hypothesis about what you are trying to model and why.

In a case context, that means deciding what the key driver of the business is, which levers the acquirer actually has, and what question the financial model is meant to answer. Those are judgment calls that require understanding the industry, the company's position, and the strategic context. A model that is technically correct but answers the wrong question is worse than a rough model that addresses the right one, because it is harder to catch.

In a financial modeling context for an actual business problem, it means deciding what assumptions to bake in and what to treat as a variable. Revenue growth assumptions, churn rates, capital intensity, market penetration trajectories: these reflect a point of view about how the business works, and that point of view has to come from understanding the business. AI does not have that understanding. I do, or I need to develop it before I can build a model that means anything.

I have made the mistake of asking AI to suggest the assumptions for a model before I had formed my own view, and the result was a model that reflected generic industry benchmarks rather than the specific situation. It looked defensible because the numbers were in range. It was not actually connected to the business I was supposed to be analyzing.

## Checking whether a result makes sense

One of the most useful things AI does in modeling work is help me think through whether an output is reasonable. Not by telling me the answer, but by stress-testing the logic.

After building a model, I describe the key output and the assumptions behind it: "This model implies the company needs to grow 40% year-over-year for five years to justify the acquisition price. Here are the assumptions driving that. What are the reasons this might be wrong or unrealistic?" The response usually surfaces two or three factors I did not adequately weight.

This is not the same as AI validating the model. It is AI helping me think more rigorously about whether the model is telling me something real. The judgment about whether to believe the output is still mine. But the process of questioning the output is faster with a thinking partner, even an artificial one.

## How to talk about this in interviews

The way MBA recruiting conversations go wrong on AI is when candidates describe AI as doing the analysis rather than supporting it. "I used AI to build the model" is a red flag. "I built the model structure and used AI to check the formula logic and stress-test the assumptions" is a description of an informed workflow.

What interviewers actually want to know when they ask about AI use is whether you understand the difference between using AI as a tool and relying on it as a substitute for thinking. The test for yourself is simple: if the AI went away mid-analysis, could you explain what you were doing and why? If yes, you are using it correctly. If the answer is that you would not know where to start, you have outsourced too much.

My honest accounting is that my modeling has gotten faster and more thorough since I started using AI in this way. Faster because the mechanical parts take less time. More thorough because I can run more rigorous checking than I could sustain manually. What has not changed is that I have to understand the business problem well enough to structure the analysis before AI can help at all. That requirement is the same as it always was, and it is the part that matters.
