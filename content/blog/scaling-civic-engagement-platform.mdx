# Scaling a Civic Engagement Platform from 5M to 60M+ Users Without Slowing Releases

Election software has fixed deadlines and uneven traffic. Most product reliability stories let you choose between scaling carefully and shipping quickly. In civic tech, the calendar takes that choice off the table. The platform has to grow, the new features have to land, and the cycle still ends on a specific Tuesday in November regardless of what shape the product is in.

I led quality and reliability work for a civic engagement platform during the 2022 midterm cycle, when usage went from around 5M users to more than 60M. The job was not to hold the line. It was to keep the team's confidence in releases high enough that scale never felt like the reason to slow down.

## What Was Really At Stake

Reliability issues during election periods do something specific. They damage client trust at the same moment voter outreach is supposed to be working. A platform that fails on the last Saturday before an election does not get a chance to apologize.

The pressure on the team showed up in three places. Manual test coverage could not keep up with rapid feature development. Critical user paths needed stronger protection before election-day traffic. And release cycles had to get faster without lowering confidence, because slowing down would have meant fewer of the customer-facing improvements campaign teams were depending on.

## How The Strategy Got Built

The first move was to map the highest-risk voter outreach workflows and platform edge cases honestly. Not the full surface, just the parts where a failure during peak traffic would actually break a customer's day. That mapping shaped where automation went first and where manual review was still worth the time.

I then expanded automated coverage across those critical product paths and created release reporting that made readiness and risk easier for stakeholders to inspect. The goal of the reporting was not status updates. It was making a release decision easier to make. If a stakeholder could not look at the dashboard and understand whether to ship, the dashboard was not doing its job.

Two principles ended up guiding the rest of the work. First, prioritize critical election-day flows before lower-risk coverage expansion. Broad shallow coverage feels like progress, but it does not protect the workflows that actually matter when the cycle peaks. Second, focus reporting on signals that could change a release decision. Anything else is decoration.

## What Changed

Usage scaled from 5M to more than 60M users. Uptime held at 99.9% during the cycle. Release velocity improved by 30%. Pre-production bug detection reached 95%, which meant the work to catch issues was happening before customers ever saw them.

The deeper change was operational. The team stopped arguing about whether a release was safe and started arguing about which of the remaining tradeoffs was worth making. That is a much healthier place for a release conversation to live, and it is the version of the team that survived peak election traffic without a major incident.

## What I Would Bring Forward

High-scale reliability work has to start with the workflows users cannot afford to lose. There is always a temptation to chase coverage breadth or vanity metrics, especially under pressure, because it feels productive. The teams that hold up under real load are the ones who knew, ahead of time, which two or three workflows were the entire game.

A faster release process only works when confidence signals are visible and trusted. If the team has to debate the meaning of every metric, the metrics are not the bottleneck. The visibility is.
