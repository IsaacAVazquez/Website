# How I'd Evaluate an Agentic AI Product as a PM (Interview-Ready Framework)

When a PM interview turns to agentic AI, the candidates who struggle usually make the same mistake. They reach for a general product framework and start talking about growth, retention, engagement, and competitive landscape as if the product were just another SaaS surface with a language model inside it. None of those things are irrelevant. They just are not the first things I care about.

If I am evaluating a candidate on an agentic AI question, I want to know whether they understand what is uniquely fragile about products that can act, not just talk. I want to hear whether they question the task fit, whether they understand trust as a design problem, and whether they know how to talk about failure without getting vague. Those are the signals that tell me the person has actually thought about this category and is not just swapping AI terminology into a standard PM answer.

## The first questions I would ask

The first question is whether the task even deserves an agentic product shape. That is the simplest test and the one people skip most often. Does the job require multiple steps, tool use, longer-running execution, or action on the user's behalf. Or is it really just a conversational assistance problem wearing more ambitious language. Candidates who ask that question early usually understand scope better than candidates who take the agentic premise as given.

The second question is what the user is being asked to trust. I think this is the center of the evaluation. If the system can act, then predictability, transparency, and control matter immediately. Can the user tell what the system is doing. Can they stop it. Can they understand why a result happened. Can they recover when the agent took the wrong branch. If a candidate cannot talk concretely about that, the rest of the analysis usually sounds polished but hollow.

The third question is how the product fails. I care a lot less about whether someone can recite a metrics stack than whether they understand the difference between an obvious failure and a silent one. An agent that visibly fails is annoying. An agent that quietly makes the wrong move and leaves the user thinking it succeeded is much more dangerous. When a candidate starts talking about loops, bad actions, weak escalations, invisible errors, and audit trails, I pay attention. That person is closer to the actual problem.

## What I would listen for in a strong answer

A strong answer usually gets more specific as it goes. On metrics, for example, I would want to hear something beyond standard retention language. I want to hear task completion quality, escalation timing, override rate, and where human correction is showing up. I want to hear that the candidate knows a zero escalation rate is not automatically good and that a high completion rate may hide low-quality completions. Those are category-specific instincts, and they matter.

I also want to hear a view on differentiation that is more durable than "the model is strong." That answer was weak a year ago and it is weaker now. What matters more is workflow context, proprietary data, tool access, integration depth, and whether the product is learning from repeated use in a way a generic agent cannot. Candidates who say that plainly usually understand that the product moat is not the same thing as the model vendor's moat.

The interview-ready version of all of this is not a memorized checklist. It is a way of showing how you think. Start by questioning task fit. Move quickly into trust and failure design. Use metrics that actually reflect agent behavior. End with what the product owns that is hard to copy. If I heard that shape from a candidate, I would take it seriously, because it tells me they know where these products succeed and where they get exposed.

That is also more or less how I evaluate real agentic products. The interview framing is different, but the substance is the same. I am not looking for a clever framework name. I am looking for evidence that the person understands the product surface, the failure surface, and the human trust problem well enough to make good decisions once the product leaves the demo.
