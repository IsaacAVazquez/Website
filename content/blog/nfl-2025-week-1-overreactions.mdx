# NFL Week 1 Is the Most Overreacted-To Week of the Year, and the Data Says Buy Usage, Sell Box Scores

There is a specific kind of chaos that hits fantasy leagues on the Monday after the first Sunday, and it is worth naming before you get swept into it. One week of real games has landed after a whole offseason of projections, and it feels like the fog just lifted, so people start trading, dropping, and panicking as if the season is now knowable. I think this is the single most reliable way to hurt your team early, because the Week 1 sample is real, which makes it seductive, and it is also tiny, which makes most of what you are reacting to noise. The useful move is not to ignore Week 1, since it does contain information, but to know which parts of it stabilize fast enough to act on and which parts are variance that will regress by Week 4.

The shorthand I use is that you should buy usage and sell box scores, and almost every Week 1 decision gets easier once you separate those two things.

## Snap share and target share are real, and they tell you fast

The most valuable thing Week 1 gives you is a look at how a coaching staff actually deploys players when the games count, which is different from what they said in August and different from the preseason. Snap share, route participation, and target share stabilize quickly, meaning even one game of a player running a full route tree or commanding a large share of the targets is genuine signal, because those are decisions about role, and role is sticky. When I see a back playing on passing downs he was not expected to play, or a receiver leading his team in targets in a way that was not priced in, I treat that as information I can act on now, because usage in Week 1 is a reasonable predictor of usage in Week 5. This is the category where being early is an edge.

## Touchdowns and efficiency are variance, and chasing them is the trap

The part of the box score that fools people is the part that is mostly luck over one game, which is touchdowns and yards-per-touch efficiency. A player can score two touchdowns on limited work and look like a league winner, or catch a long one that inflates his yardage, and none of that tells you much about the role that produces sustainable points. I try to look past the fantasy total to the underlying opportunity, because a quiet fifteen-touch, six-target game with no scores is often a better sign than a loud two-touchdown game built on five touches, and the market reacts to the totals rather than the touches. The players who scored big on thin usage are the ones I am trying to sell to a leaguemate who only saw the final line, and the players who produced little on heavy usage are the ones I am trying to buy.

## The waiver wire is where the overreaction gets expensive

Nowhere does Week 1 panic cost more than the waiver wire, where managers blow a large chunk of their season-long free agent budget on a player who spiked once. Sometimes the spike is real and backed by a role change, and then it is worth paying up, but often it is a one-week outlier on a depth player who will fade the moment the starter is healthy or the game script normalizes. I try to spend waiver budget on opportunity, meaning a player who just inherited a role through injury or a depth-chart move, and to sit on my hands when the only case for a pickup is a single good fantasy line. Budget spent in Week 2 chasing a Week 1 ghost is budget you do not have in November when a real league-winner hits the wire.

## Dropping a stud after one quiet week is how you lose the season in September

The mirror image of chasing a spike is dumping a player you drafted highly because he had a bad opener, and it is just as costly. One quiet game from a talented player in a good role is close to meaningless, and the manager who panics and moves him for pennies, or worse drops him, is trading a season of expected production for a single data point. I gave those players a high draft price for reasons that one Sunday almost never invalidates, and unless the quiet game came with a real role change, a benching or a committee I did not expect, I am holding and buying more if a nervous manager will sell.

The pattern I would pull out of Week 1 is that it is a usage report, not a results report, and the managers who gain ground in September are the ones who read the snaps and targets and ignore the touchdowns. Buy the role, sell the box score, keep your waiver budget for real opportunity, and do not let one Sunday talk you out of a whole offseason of reasoning.
