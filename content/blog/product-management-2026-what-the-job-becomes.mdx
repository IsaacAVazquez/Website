# The Product Manager Job in 2026 Is Compressing Toward the Part That Doesn't Delegate

At the start of the year the product management takes split into two camps that are both, I think, missing the same thing. One camp says AI is going to replace product managers, and the other says PMs need to learn to code and prompt their way into relevance, and both are arguing about tools when the actual change is about which parts of the job survive. What I see happening, from using these tools daily and watching how the work shifts, is that agents are absorbing the execution layer of product management, the writing and the synthesis and the first-draft analysis, and what is left when that layer thins out is the part of the job that was always the point and was often crowded out by the busywork. The role is compressing toward judgment, taste, and the ability to tell whether something is good, and that is a different job than the artifact-production job a lot of PM guides still describe.

What I think this means is that the question is not whether AI replaces PMs, it is which half of the PM job AI does, and the answer is the half that was never the hard part.

## The execution layer is genuinely getting automated, and that is fine

I want to be concrete about what is actually moving, because vague claims about AI changing everything are easy to dismiss. The parts of my job that AI now does well are the mechanical ones, drafting a spec from a rough decision, synthesizing a stack of user interviews into themes, pulling together a competitive teardown, turning a messy set of notes into a structured document. These used to eat real hours, and handing them off is a straightforward win, not a threat, because none of them was ever where the value of the job actually sat. A PM who spent most of the week producing documents was spending most of the week on the delegable part, and having that part get faster is good, as long as you are clear that the documents were the output of the thinking and not the thinking itself. The risk is confusing the two, and treating the artifacts as the job when they were always just the residue of it.

## What is left is judgment, and judgment does not compress the same way

When the artifact production thins out, what remains is the set of things that do not delegate, and they cluster around judgment. Deciding what is worth building and what to say no to, reading a situation that the data does not fully capture, weighing tradeoffs between stakeholders who all have a reasonable case, choosing the bet the specific context calls for rather than the generic best practice, none of these is a task with a checkable answer that a model can just produce. They require holding the goals, the constraints, and the values of the organization at once and making a call you will be accountable for, and that accountability is exactly what the model cannot take on. This is the part of the job that was always the actual job, and it is becoming a larger share of the role precisely because the surrounding work is shrinking, which means the PMs who were coasting on output and thin on judgment are the ones this shift exposes.

## Evaluation is becoming a core PM skill, borrowed from QA

There is a new skill I think is quietly becoming central, which is evaluation, the ability to look at output, whether from a model or a team or a product, and reliably tell whether it is good. When a model can produce a plausible spec, a plausible analysis, a plausible plan, the scarce skill is no longer producing those things, it is judging them, catching where the plausible answer is subtly wrong, knowing what good looks like well enough to reject good-enough. I come at this from a QA background, so it feels familiar, because this is what testing always was, the discipline of checking whether something actually meets the bar rather than whether it looks like it does. A PM in 2026 is doing a lot of evaluation, of AI output and of everything else, and being good at that, at having a calibrated sense of quality and the willingness to send something back, is becoming as important as any traditional PM skill.

## The career path is the part I am genuinely unsure about

The place I do not have a clean answer is the career pipeline, and I would rather name the uncertainty than pretend past it. If the execution layer of the job is what junior PMs traditionally did to learn the craft, and that layer is getting automated, then the path by which someone builds the judgment that the senior job requires gets murkier, in the same way it is getting murkier for junior engineers. You cannot develop taste for what is worth building without reps, and if the reps are the thing being automated away, the field has a real question about how the next generation acquires the judgment the role increasingly depends on. I do not think the answer is to protect the busywork for training's sake, but I also do not think anyone has solved how you grow judgment when the ladder's bottom rungs are the parts a model now does, and I would be suspicious of anyone claiming they have.

The pattern I would pull out of this is that the product management job is moving from producing artifacts to making decisions and judging quality, and the tools are accelerating that move rather than causing it, because the artifacts were never the job. The execution layer is getting automated and that is a good thing to embrace, the judgment layer is what remains and what actually matters, and evaluation is becoming a core skill worth deliberately building. The open question is how the field grows the next set of judgment now that the tasks people learned it on are the tasks getting automated, and I would trust the people wrestling honestly with that question more than the ones confidently predicting either replacement or business as usual.
