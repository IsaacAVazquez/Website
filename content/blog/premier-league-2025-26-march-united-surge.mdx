# Premier League 2025-26 in March: Manchester United's Surge and Bruno Fernandes as the Engine

I spent last month arguing that the run-in is really four races stacked on top of each other, and in March the one that told the best story was the fight for the Champions League places, specifically because of Manchester United. A club that finished fifteenth a season ago spent March climbing into the top few, and a turnaround of that size in a single year is rare enough that it is worth stopping to ask what actually caused it. My answer, and I do not think it is a close call, is that most of it runs through one player.

Bruno Fernandes has been the engine of United's season in a way that goes past the usual "their best player has been good" observation. He has been creating goals at a rate that is genuinely historic, the kind of assist numbers that put a player in the conversation with the best individual creative seasons the league has ever had, and he has been doing it while carrying the captaincy of a club that was in disarray not long ago. When I watch United now, the shape of almost everything dangerous they do traces back to him, and that is both the reason they have climbed and the thing I would be nervous about if I supported them, because a team that depends this heavily on one man is a team that is one injury away from a very different season.

## A single-handed transformation is a specific and fragile thing

What I find interesting about United's rise is how concentrated it is. Plenty of good teams are good because a system makes ordinary players play above themselves, and those teams tend to be stable because the quality is distributed. United in 2025-26 are the other kind, a team lifted a long way by one player operating at the peak of his powers, and that is a more fragile foundation even when the results are excellent. It has worked this season because Fernandes has stayed fit and stayed at that level for months on end, but the honest read is that the floor under this United team is a lot lower than their current position suggests, and the gap between where they are and where they would be without him is enormous.

I do not want to undersell what the club has done around him, because climbing from fifteenth to the edge of the Champions League places is not something one player does entirely alone, and United clearly found some stability in the second half of the season after a start that was going nowhere. But when I try to explain the size of the jump, I keep coming back to the same place. A team does not usually move twelve places in a year on the back of gradual, collective improvement. It moves that far when something changes sharply, and the thing that changed sharply here was one player deciding to have the season of his life.

## The title race kept its nerve while the noise moved elsewhere

Above United, the title race stayed roughly where January left it, a two-team question between Arsenal and Manchester City that neither one was running away with. What I noticed in March is that the noise had moved away from the top, partly because United's surge was more fun to talk about and partly because Arsenal and City were both in the grind-it-out phase where a title is defended one tense 1-0 at a time rather than announced with a statement result. That suits Arsenal, whose whole identity this season has been the high floor and the low drama, and I read the relative quiet at the top as a mild point in their favor, because a team that wants to protect a lead would rather the race be boring than wild.

The pattern I would pull out of March is that the most dramatic movement in the table was not at the top at all, it was a fallen giant hauling itself back up the standings on the shoulders of one exceptional individual season. Manchester United's climb into the Champions League places is the kind of story that makes a season, and Bruno Fernandes's year is going to be in the awards conversation no matter where United finish, because individual brilliance at that sustained a level is exactly what those awards are supposed to reward. The title, meanwhile, kept its powder dry, two teams trading tense wins and waiting for April to force the issue. April always forces the issue.
