# Roadmapping with AI: Turning Raw Feedback into Quarterly Bets

I think most roadmap conversations go wrong before prioritization even starts. Teams act as if the hard part is ranking opportunities, but the harder problem is turning raw feedback into something coherent enough to rank in the first place. Interviews, support tickets, sales asks, executive nudges, churn notes, and product analytics all arrive in different forms and carry different kinds of truth. If you flatten that mess too early, the roadmap meeting becomes a fight between whoever tells the cleanest story, not whoever has the strongest evidence.

AI has been genuinely useful for me in this part of the process, but not because it decides what to build. It helps me do the preparation work that usually gets compressed under time pressure. It can cluster, summarize, and expose holes faster than I can. The decision still stays with me. That distinction matters, because once people start treating a model like a prioritization oracle, the quality of the roadmap usually drops rather than improves.

## What I ask it to do before the roadmap meeting

The first thing I do is separate sources instead of merging them. I want user interviews in one place, support themes in another, sales feedback in another, and internal asks somewhere I can see clearly without letting them dominate the rest. I also want the reliability of the source called out, because not all signal deserves equal weight. One memorable anecdote from a senior stakeholder is not the same as a repeating pattern from users, even if it enters the room with more force.

Once the raw material is organized, I use AI to cluster problems rather than solutions. That has become an important rule for me. If I ask for solution ideas too early, the obvious solutions anchor the rest of the discussion and the team starts debating implementation before we have agreed on the underlying problem. When I keep the first pass focused on problem statements, the output is much more useful. I get a smaller set of recurring issues, direct quotes or evidence attached to them, and a clearer sense of where multiple sources are pointing at the same thing from different angles.

That step saves real time. It turns days of rereading into an hour or two of cleanup and review. But the system still needs a human editor. I almost always collapse a few clusters that should be one, split one that got merged too aggressively, and throw out some noise that the model interpreted as a theme simply because the wording repeated. I do not think that means the tool failed. I think it means clustering is a collaboration step, not a one-click truth machine.

## Where the real product judgment still happens

The part AI cannot own is deciding what matters now. A roadmap is not a report on the top five pains users mentioned. It is a set of bets shaped by strategy, timing, technical constraints, organizational capacity, and what the company is willing to defer. Models are not good at that because the most important variables are often the least explicit. They do not know the executive commitment you already made, the dependency that will not land this quarter, or the competitive pressure that changes how you should weight a problem even if users mention it less often.

I have seen PMs ask a model to score the opportunities and recommend the priority order. The answers usually sound reasonable, which is exactly why I do not trust them much. They are built from visible evidence and generic logic. Roadmapping is full of invisible constraints and asymmetric information. The better use of AI is to get the evidence into shape faster, write clearer option descriptions, and surface the places where you are relying on assumptions instead of actual signal.

That last use is underrated. If I ask the model what information would most change my confidence in a proposed bet, it often points me toward the missing piece I should go get before the meeting. Maybe I need one more user conversation, a volume cut from support, or a better sense of implementation cost. That is valuable because it sharpens my own judgment instead of pretending to replace it.

So my view is straightforward. AI is excellent at compressing the messy middle between raw feedback and a clean set of options. It is not especially good at deciding which bet belongs on the roadmap once the politics, sequencing, and strategic context get real. I use it heavily for the first part and keep a very tight grip on the second. That has felt like the right division of labor.
