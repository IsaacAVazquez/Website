# Treating Pricing as Product Work, Not a Finance-Only Exercise

Most pricing decisions get made on the wrong side of the building. Finance owns the model, sales owns the negotiation, and product gets pulled in at the end to validate that the packaging story is coherent. By that point the structure is already locked, and product's role becomes either rubber-stamping a deck or arguing for changes that finance has to redo from scratch.

That model produces pricing that captures a fraction of the product's actual value. The right framing is the opposite. Pricing is a product decision that carries financial implications, not a financial decision that carries product implications. When I led this initiative, that was the real shift. Everything else followed from it.

## What The Org Was Actually Stuck On

The organization needed a pricing strategy that reflected product value, market position, and customer willingness to pay rather than inherited assumptions from earlier in the company's life. Three frictions kept showing up. Pricing conversations were split across sales, finance, product, and engineering, with no shared model anyone could reason from. Decision-makers lacked a shared view of product value and market comparison, which meant every meeting started over from first principles. And packaging decisions needed clearer revenue and customer-impact tradeoffs before they could be made with confidence.

Weak pricing strategy leaves revenue on the table while making future packaging changes harder to explain to customers. The cost is not just immediate. It compounds, because each pricing change you cannot defend cleanly trains the customer base to negotiate harder on the next one.

## How The Work Got Structured

The first task was to build the analytical and stakeholder alignment layer that would let pricing become a product decision instead of a finance-only exercise. That meant researching market benchmarks and competitor packaging seriously, not just collecting screenshots of pricing pages. It meant modeling pricing scenarios against revenue, adoption, and operational constraints with enough rigor that the model could survive cross-functional pressure. And it meant creating executive-facing materials that made tradeoffs explicit, not buried inside a recommended option.

The principles that ended up driving the strategy were direct. Center the work on product value and customer segment fit, not on what the existing price card happened to say. Separate near-term revenue opportunities from longer-term packaging bets so each was being argued on the right time horizon. And make the underlying model reusable so the work could guide future product lines without being recreated each time.

The cross-functional alignment was the part that moved the recommendation from a recommendation into an actual decision. Engineering, sales, finance, and leadership all had to be able to see their own assumptions inside the model. That is what turned individual objections into shared questions about which scenario to pick.

## What Changed

The initiative generated $4M in additional annual revenue. The market analysis framework got adopted for future pricing work, which is a more durable outcome than the immediate revenue lift. Executive buy-in came through clear scenario modeling rather than through a single recommended price, which made the resulting decision much easier to defend internally and externally.

## What I Would Bring Forward

Pricing work is product work when it changes packaging, value communication, and roadmap tradeoffs. Treating it as anything else leaves money on the table and creates packaging decisions that the product team has to live with for years.

Stakeholder alignment improves when every group can see its own assumptions reflected in the model. The temptation in pricing work is to hide the assumptions inside a confident recommendation, because it feels cleaner. The cleaner version is also the version that falls apart the first time a single assumption gets challenged. Showing the model openly is the shorter path to a real decision.

The thing I would do differently next time is add customer validation earlier, so willingness-to-pay evidence sits beside the market and financial analysis from the start instead of arriving late as a sanity check.
