# The Other End of the Table: The Promoted Curse Broke, but Relegation Still Took Three

I spent more of this season writing about the bottom half of the table than I usually do, because the story down there was more interesting than it had any right to be, and now that it is settled I want to close the loop on it. The headline is that the pattern I kept circling all year, the one where every promoted club goes straight back down, finally broke, and it broke in the most emphatic way possible through a club that came up via the play-offs. Relegation still claimed three teams, but for the first time in a while the promoted clubs were not simply handed two or three of the spots.

Start with Sunderland, because they were the story of the season for me from about September onward. A club that came up through the play-offs, historically the least stable route into the league and the one most likely to lead straight back out of it, finished seventh and qualified for Europe. I kept waiting all season for the fall that never came, and it never came because there was nothing fluky underneath it. This was a promoted club that recruited well, held its shape through the winter congestion that usually breaks teams like it, and belonged in the top half on merit. Leeds United stayed up as well, more quietly, doing the unglamorous work of a promoted club that just needed to be functional enough to survive, and being exactly that. Two of the three promoted clubs surviving, with one of them thriving, is the clearest evidence I have seen that the gap between the divisions is not as permanent as the last two seasons made it look.

## Relegation still claimed three, and one of them was promoted

The pattern broke, but it did not vanish, and the three clubs that went down tell their own story. Burnley were the promoted club that could not sustain it, finishing nineteenth on 22 points, and their season is a reminder that breaking the curse was never guaranteed for all three. Burnley came up alongside Sunderland and Leeds and could not find the same footing, and the honest read is that the difference between a promoted club that survives and one that goes down is often a matter of a few results and a little squad depth rather than some huge gulf in quality. It is a thin line, and Burnley ended up on the wrong side of it.

The two that went down with them are the more sobering cases, because they were not promoted clubs, they were established Premier League teams. Wolves finished bottom on 20 points, a full relegation-level collapse for a club that has spent years in the division, and West Ham went down in eighteenth on 39 points, which is the one that will sting the most because it was so close. Thirty-nine points is usually enough to stay up, and West Ham missed survival by two points, finishing just behind Tottenham, who scraped clear on 41. A club with West Ham's resources and history being relegated by that narrow a margin is the kind of season that gets decisions made in the summer, because it is not a squad that should have been anywhere near the drop, and being there anyway points to something gone wrong beyond bad luck.

## What the bottom of the table said about the whole league

The reason I find the relegation picture worth this much attention is that it connects to the thing I kept noticing all season, which is that the points big clubs used to bank almost automatically had to actually be earned this year. When the promoted clubs are competitive and hard to beat, the pressure does not disappear, it just moves, and it lands on the established clubs that used to feel safe. West Ham and Wolves going down while two promoted clubs stayed up is the same story from the other direction. The gap between the divisions closed, and the clubs that suffered for it were the mid-table incumbents who could no longer count on the promoted sides handing them easy games.

The pattern I would pull out of the bottom of the table is that competitiveness at the bottom is a redistribution, not a reduction, of jeopardy. Nobody got safer this season. The promoted clubs got better, which meant the danger shifted onto established teams that assumed it did not apply to them, and two of them paid for that assumption. Sunderland's seventh-place finish and Leeds staying up are the feel-good end of it, and they deserve the credit for finally breaking a pattern that had started to look structural. But West Ham missing survival by two points is the part I will remember as the warning, that in a league where the promoted clubs can actually play, the floor is no longer where the established clubs think it is.
