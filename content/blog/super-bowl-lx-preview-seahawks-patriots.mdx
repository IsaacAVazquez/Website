# Super Bowl LX: The Patriots' Turnaround Is the Story, but Seattle's Defense Is the Pick

The two weeks before a Super Bowl are built for narrative, and this year the narrative writes itself, because New England went from four wins the season before to a fourteen-win year and a conference title, one of the largest single-season turnarounds the league has ever seen, tied with the kind of company you only mention when something historic happens. I love that story, and I think it is completely real, and I would still bet against it on Sunday. That is not a contradiction. A turnaround is a statement about how far a team traveled, and a Super Bowl is a single game between the two teams standing at the end, and the distance you traveled to arrive does not help you block anybody once the game starts. What decides this one, in my read, is Seattle's defense against a young New England offense, and that matchup is the fact I keep coming back to.

## The Patriots' climb is rare enough to take seriously on its own terms

I do not want to wave away what New England did, because a ten-win improvement in a single season is not a fluke, it is a sign that a lot of things went right at once, from the quarterback taking a real step, to the roster getting healthier and deeper, to a coaching setup that got more out of the group than the prior one did. Teams that make that kind of leap are usually genuinely good, not lucky, and the Patriots earned the number-two seed and won three playoff games to get here, which is not a path you sleepwalk through. So this is not a story of a mediocre team backing into a Super Bowl. It is a story of a fast rise, and fast rises are exciting precisely because they are ahead of schedule, which is also the small crack in the story.

## Ahead of schedule usually means short on the specific experience this game demands

The thing about arriving ahead of schedule is that you arrive without the reps that the biggest game tends to punish you for lacking, and that is where I think New England is most exposed. A young offense built around a young quarterback playing the best defense it has seen all year, on the largest stage, with two weeks of pressure and an exotic game plan it has not faced, is the setup where inexperience shows up as a sack taken instead of a throwaway, a snap-count problem, an interception into coverage the quarterback will wish he had back. None of that is a knock on the talent, it is a statement about the gap between being good enough to get here and being battle-tested enough to win here, and that gap is usually paid in turnovers and stalled drives.

## Seattle's defense and top seed are the more predictive facts

On the other side is a Seattle team that earned the number-one seed, sat on a bye, and then handled two good opponents to reach the Super Bowl on a long winning streak, and the engine of that team is a defense that travels. Defense is the phase that holds up most reliably in a single high-variance game, because a pass rush and a secondary that takes the ball away can win a game the offense does not, and they do not depend on the shootout going your way. When I try to figure out which team's identity is most likely to survive one game against a good opponent, Seattle's answer, get after the quarterback and make the young guy prove he can beat us from the pocket, is the more durable one. The number-one seed is not a magic charm, but it is a season-long signal that this team was the more complete of the two, and complete teams with real defenses are what I want in a single game.

## The rematch angle is fun and almost entirely irrelevant

There is a tidy subplot here, which is that these two franchises met in a Super Bowl over a decade ago in a game New England won on a goal-line interception that people still talk about, and the broadcast will spend real time on it. I would not read anything into it. The rosters, coaches, and quarterbacks are almost entirely different, the league is different, and a game from another era tells you nothing about how this one gets played. It is a good story for the pregame show and it is not a factor, and I mention it mostly to set it aside, because it is exactly the kind of narrative that feels like information and is not.

The pattern I would pull out before this one is that turnaround stories and top seeds are answering different questions, and only one of them is about who wins on Sunday. New England's season is the better story and Seattle is the better bet, and I do not think those two claims are in tension. If the Patriots win, it will be because their young quarterback was ready earlier than young quarterbacks usually are, and that would be a genuinely special thing to watch. I just think the more likely outcome is that Seattle's defense turns the biggest game into the hardest test New England has faced, and that the team built on the phase that travels is the one holding the trophy.
