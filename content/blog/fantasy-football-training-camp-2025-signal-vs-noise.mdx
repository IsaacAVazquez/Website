# Fantasy Football Training Camp Is Mostly Noise, and I Can Tell You Which Parts Aren't

Every year around now the fantasy football conversation gets loud in a way that feels like information and mostly isn't. Camps open, the beat writers start filing, and within a week you can find a story telling you that some running back is in the best shape of his life, some receiver has looked unstoppable in seven-on-seven, and some rookie is turning heads. I understand why this content exists, because there is a month to fill and not much football to fill it with, and I also think that if you let it move your draft board you are going to draft worse than someone who ignored all of it. The useful thing to do with camp is not to consume more of it but to filter it, and the filter is simple once you name it.

What I think camp actually gives you is information about roles, and almost no reliable information about talent. A player looking fast in August tells you very little, because everyone looks fast against a defense that is not allowed to hit them. A player taking first-team reps at a spot he did not hold in June tells you something real, because that is a decision the coaching staff made with information you do not have.

## The best-shape-of-his-life genre is noise, and it is noise on purpose

The clearest tell that a camp story is empty is that it is about how a player looks rather than about what his job is. Best shape of his life, dropped fifteen pounds, added muscle, looks explosive, all of it is the same story every summer about a different name, and it has almost no predictive value for the season. I am not saying conditioning does not matter. I am saying you cannot draft off it, because the base rate is that every veteran arrives claiming to be in great shape and the ones who then get hurt or fade were saying the exact same thing in July. When the only content in a report is the player's physique or a highlight from a non-contact drill, I treat it as entertainment and move on.

## Depth-chart and usage changes are the reports worth reading

The camp news that should actually move your board is boring, which is why it gets less attention. A back who was expected to lead a backfield showing up in a clear timeshare, a receiver sliding into the slot where the targets live, a rookie getting first-team work ahead of the veteran everyone assumed was locked in, these are the reports that carry real information, because they are about the distribution of touches, and touches are what fantasy scoring is downstream of. I would rather know that a player is running with the ones than know that he looks great, because the job is the thing that produces points and the highlight is not.

## Injury recovery is the one place the physical reports matter

The exception to my rule about ignoring how players look is injury recovery, and it matters a lot. Whether a player coming back from a serious injury is practicing fully, on a pitch count, or held out entirely is genuinely useful, because it speaks directly to availability, and availability is the most underrated input in fantasy. A player I love who cannot get on the field in August is a player I am fading in a draft in September, not because his talent changed but because the range of outcomes now includes missing real time. This is the one camp storyline where I let the physical report update my expectations, and even here I am watching the practice participation designation more than the optimistic quote.

## The beat writers are not lying, they are just incentivized differently than you

It helps to remember that the people producing camp content are not trying to help you win your league, and that is not a knock on them. Their job is to cover the team every day, which means every day needs a story, which means a quiet practice still gets written up as if something happened. The result is a feed that systematically overstates how much is changing, because nothing changing is not publishable and a receiver having a good rep is. Once you see that incentive clearly, the whole month reads differently, and you stop mistaking volume of coverage for weight of evidence.

The pattern I would pull out of all of this is that camp is a roles report disguised as a talent report, and the managers who draft well in August are the ones who read it for the former and throw away the latter. Watch who is running with the first team, watch who is getting the injury designation you did not expect, watch where the touches are being redistributed, and let everything about who looks explosive in shorts go by. The [rankings I keep](/fantasy-football) move when a role moves, and they do not move because someone had a good day against air, and that is the discipline I would bring to the next four weeks of noise.
