# Premier League 2025-26 in November: Arsenal's Clean-Sheet Run Becomes the Story

Last month I said Arsenal had taken over at the top by refusing to concede, and I flagged the one risk in a team built that way, which is that a side that lives on clean sheets can go through spells where it also cannot score. November did nothing to trigger that worry. If anything it did the opposite, because Arsenal spent the month extending the run to the point where it stopped being a good month and started being the whole identity of the team. At some point a string of clean sheets crosses a line from a hot streak into the thing that defines a title challenge, and I think that line got crossed in November.

The number that matters is not any single scoreline, it is the length of the run, because a team that keeps not conceding across a stretch of fixtures that includes hard away days and awkward midweek games is telling you the structure is real. When I look at [the dashboard](/premier-league) and scroll Arsenal's recent form, it is a column of shutouts, and a column of shutouts in November is worth more than the same column in September because the schedule by now has included enough genuine tests that you cannot explain it away with an easy run of opponents. David Raya has been at the center of it, and a goalkeeper in this kind of form quietly changes the math of a season, because it turns the draws a title team would otherwise drop into wins and turns the wins into comfortable ones.

## A defensive title team is a specific kind of bet

I have been open about trusting defense over attack when I am judging a front-runner, and November hardened that view rather than complicating it. A team that wins the league on goals conceded is making a different promise than a team that wins it on goals scored. The scoring team promises fireworks and asks you to trust that the finishing holds up over nine months, which historically it does not, not perfectly. The defending team promises a floor, a version of itself that shows up even on the days the attack is misfiring, and that floor is what gets a team through the grind of the winter when nobody is at their best. Arsenal are making the second promise, and it is the one I would rather have in hand heading into December.

The honest counterweight is that a defensive team's ceiling on any given day is lower, and the risk I raised in October has not gone away just because November did not expose it. A run of 1-0 wins and clean-sheet draws is a title-winning formula right up until the attack goes quiet for three weeks and a two-goal cushion in the table turns into a two-point one. I am parking that concern rather than dismissing it, because runs like this always end, and how Arsenal respond when it does will tell me more than the run itself.

## Liverpool's defense of the title started to slip

The development that actually surprised me in November was at Anfield. Liverpool came into the season as champions and made the early running exactly as champions should, and then through the autumn the grip started to loosen in a way I did not expect this soon. I am not ready to write off the defending champions in November, because that is the kind of call that ages badly, but the signs were there that this was not going to be the smooth title defense the fast start had hinted at. When a reigning champion starts dropping the points it used to bank, the question stops being whether it can catch the leader and starts being whether it can hold on to its own place in the top few, and that is a steep fall from where they were in August.

The pattern I would pull out of November is that the top of the table has separated into a team I trust and a team I am starting to doubt, with Arsenal turning a defensive run into a genuine identity while Liverpool's title defense quietly came apart at the edges. Manchester City, as ever, were within range without saying much, which is the most City thing possible and the reason I would not exhale if I were an Arsenal supporter. The winter is coming, the fixtures are about to pile up, and the two questions I am carrying into December are whether Arsenal's attack can keep the scoreboard ticking over during the congestion and whether anyone in the chasing pack is good enough to make them pay if it cannot.
