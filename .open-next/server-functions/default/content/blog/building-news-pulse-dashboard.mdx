# Building News Pulse: A Dashboard For Comparing How Major Outlets Cover The Same Story

If you want to explore the product before reading the breakdown, open [News Pulse Dashboard](/news-pulse).

Reading one publication tells you what happened. Reading several publications tells you how the story is being framed. The gap between those two things is where News Pulse lives.

I built this project because it is surprisingly tedious to compare coverage across outlets in a structured way. Opening a half-dozen homepages gives you raw exposure, but not much synthesis. You can feel the differences in framing, frequency, and tone, but it takes effort to turn that into something legible.

The goal of News Pulse was to make that comparison easier without overengineering the stack.

## The Problem Behind The Product

People who follow the news closely often want to answer questions like:

- Which outlets are covering this story heavily right now?
- Are the headlines converging around the same terms or splitting into different frames?
- Is the tone mostly urgent, negative, neutral, or mixed?
- How fast does a theme spread across sources?

Most consumer news products are designed to deliver articles, not help people analyze editorial patterns. Enterprise media intelligence tools exist, but they are often heavier, more expensive, and more specialized than what this use case needs.

That left a good product gap: a lightweight dashboard for comparing how several major outlets are talking about the same news cycle.

## Why RSS Was The Right Foundation

The product starts with RSS because RSS is still one of the cleanest ways to capture headline-level editorial output across publishers.

That choice shaped the entire build:

- it kept the data model focused on headlines and feed metadata
- it avoided a much more fragile scraping workflow
- it made update timing easier to reason about
- it allowed the product to stay legible as a portfolio project instead of turning into an infrastructure project

RSS is not glamorous, but it is reliable enough for this problem. That is often the right product decision.

## The Core Decisions And Tradeoffs

### 1. Show Coverage Patterns, Not Just A Feed Reader

News Pulse is not trying to be another article reader. It is organized around three higher-level views:

- headlines
- coverage
- analysis

That structure is important because the value of the product is comparative, not archival. The user should feel like they are seeing patterns across sources rather than just skimming a merged list of links.

### 2. Use Lightweight Topic Extraction And Sentiment Instead Of Heavy ML Infrastructure

One of the most deliberate choices in the product was to keep the analysis layer lightweight.

The dashboard extracts topics, summarizes source behavior, and performs headline-level sentiment without introducing a large external dependency chain. That fits the problem well:

- headlines are short and structured enough for lightweight heuristics to be useful
- the goal is directional media intelligence, not perfect semantic classification
- keeping the stack lighter makes the product easier to maintain and reason about

This is a case where "good enough and understandable" is better than "technically impressive but hard to trust."

### 3. Graceful Degradation Matters In Feed Products

Live feeds are messy. Publishers change formats, feeds fail, and partial outages happen.

The product handles that reality by surfacing feed errors without collapsing the whole experience. That is a small but important product choice. If one source is unavailable, the dashboard should still tell the truth about what it can show rather than fail silently or imply complete coverage.

## How The Experience Works

At a high level, the product behaves like a compact media intelligence surface:

- it fetches articles from six major outlets
- it lets the user filter by source when scanning headlines
- it aggregates the full set when showing coverage and analysis
- it attaches lightweight NLP-style interpretation to the headline stream

That lets the user move from raw headlines to broader questions about editorial behavior.

The dashboard is especially useful when a major story breaks and different publications begin emphasizing different nouns, different emotional registers, or different angles of blame and consequence.

## Why A Dependency-Light Stack Was Worth Protecting

There is always pressure to make media-analysis products feel more "AI-native" than they need to be. This project pushed me in the opposite direction.

I wanted the product to feel sharp, timely, and interpretable without becoming dependent on a chain of external services that would add cost, complexity, and brittleness. For this use case, a lighter stack offered real advantages:

- faster iteration
- clearer system behavior
- easier debugging
- a tighter connection between the visible output and the underlying logic

That tradeoff made the project more useful as a product surface and more durable as a codebase.

## What This Project Taught Me

News products become more interesting when they move one step up the abstraction ladder.

A feed is useful. A comparative view of editorial patterns is often more useful.

This project reinforced a product principle I keep coming back to: if users are already doing synthesis manually, there is probably room to build a better intermediate layer. In this case, that layer is not a full newsroom platform. It is a clean dashboard that makes coverage differences easier to spot.

It also reminded me that "lightweight" is not the same as "thin." A product can do meaningful analytical work without pretending every problem requires a giant model stack.
