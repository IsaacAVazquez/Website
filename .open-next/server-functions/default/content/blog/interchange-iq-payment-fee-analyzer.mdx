# Interchange IQ: Building a Payment Fee Analyzer Around Real Processor Economics

If you want to use the tool first, open [Interchange IQ](/fintech-tools/interchange-iq).

Most merchants know they are paying for card processing. Far fewer know what they are actually paying for.

That confusion is not accidental. Processor pricing is full of blended rates, flat-rate convenience, card-mix variation, scheme complexity, and enterprise plans that only become legible once a business is already large enough to negotiate. For operators trying to make a clean decision, the market often feels deliberately hard to compare.

I built Interchange IQ to make that comparison more concrete. The product is designed to answer a practical question: given your monthly volume, average ticket size, and card mix, how different do processor economics actually look?

## The Problem This Product Addresses

For many businesses, processor choice is treated as a branding or convenience decision when it is really a margin decision.

The challenge is that the headline price rarely tells the whole story:

- flat-rate pricing is easy to understand, but it can hide meaningful overpayment at scale
- interchange-plus pricing can be cheaper, but it is harder to reason about without the underlying rate structure
- ticket size changes the impact of fixed per-transaction fees
- debit-heavy and Amex-heavy businesses do not behave the same way economically

A merchant does not need a theory lecture on payments. They need a model they can manipulate.

## Why The Tool Starts With Published Interchange Data

The core product decision was to make the calculator legible from first principles.

Interchange IQ uses representative published interchange values and public processor pricing assumptions to show where the costs come from. That matters because a comparison tool is much more useful when the user can see the difference between:

- network economics
- processor markup
- volume sensitivity
- ticket-size sensitivity

This keeps the product grounded in real payments structure instead of turning it into a black-box recommendation engine.

The product does not try to predict every custom contract. It gives the user a way to understand the shape of the decision and the conditions that change it.

## The Key Product Decisions

### 1. Put Card Mix At The Center Of The Experience

Card mix is where processor comparisons start to become real.

A business with a heavy debit profile behaves very differently from one with a premium credit and Amex-heavy mix. Showing only a headline rate would flatten that difference and make the tool less honest.

That is why Interchange IQ lets the user adjust:

- monthly processing volume
- average transaction size
- credit-card share
- Amex share within the credit mix

Those inputs turn the product from a static comparison table into a working model.

### 2. Compare Flat Rate And Interchange+ On The Same Surface

Many payments explainers implicitly argue for one pricing model before showing the math. I wanted the product to do the opposite.

Interchange IQ places flat-rate and interchange-plus options side by side so the user can see when simplicity is worth paying for and when it becomes too expensive. That is a better product experience than pushing a conclusion without exposing the tradeoff.

It also makes the tool useful across business maturity levels. A smaller operator may decide flat rate is still the right answer. A larger operator may realize the markup on interchange-plus becomes more compelling once volume rises or average ticket size increases.

### 3. Breakeven Analysis Matters More Than A Single Winner

The cheapest option at one ticket size is not necessarily the cheapest option at another.

That is why the tool includes breakeven logic. A single "best processor" label is too shallow. The better question is: at what point does the economic logic flip?

Breakeven analysis teaches the user how the market works, not just what the calculator currently outputs.

## How The Experience Works

The interface is intentionally direct:

- inputs define the business profile
- summary stats show the cheapest current option and monthly fee impact
- a ranked comparison table surfaces effective rates and fee outcomes
- explanatory copy clarifies what card mix and pricing models actually mean

That structure is deliberate. Payments products can become abstract very quickly, so the interface needs to keep the user oriented around real operating questions:

- what happens if my volume doubles?
- what happens if my average ticket falls?
- how much is flat-rate convenience costing me?
- when does interchange-plus start to win?

## Tradeoffs I Chose On Purpose

### Education Over Exhaustiveness

The tool covers major processors and representative rate assumptions. It does not try to model every private contract or every network nuance. That keeps the experience readable and useful for decision framing.

### Transparency Over Recommendation Theater

Interchange IQ shows the math drivers instead of dressing them up as a mysterious score. In payments, a product builds more trust when users can understand the model underneath it.

### Productized Explanation Over Dense Fintech Jargon

The UI explains why debit, credit, and Amex matter because a calculator without explanation only helps people who already know the answer.

## What This Project Taught Me

Payments is a strong product-design domain because so much of the value comes from making complexity legible.

The most important lesson here was that comparison products work best when they help users build intuition, not just produce outputs. The breakeven model, the card-mix controls, and the flat-rate versus interchange-plus framing all push in that direction.

I also came away with a more durable belief about fintech products: users do not always need every edge case modeled. They need enough structure to see how the economics behave and enough clarity to make a better decision than they would have made with marketing pages alone.
